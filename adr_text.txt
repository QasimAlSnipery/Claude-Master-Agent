Adrenal Gland Diseases
Assistant Professor
Dr. Serwan Mohammed Ismail
The adrenals comprise several separate endocrine glands within a single anatomical structure
The adrenal medulla (extension of the SNS) secretes catecholamines into capillaries rather
than synapses.
Most of the adrenal cortex is made up of cells that secrete cortisol & adrenal androgens, &
form part of the HPA.
The small outer glomerulosa of the cortex secretes aldosterone under the control of the RAS.
These functions are important in an integrated control of CVS, metabolic & immune
responses to stress.
There is increasing evidence that subtle alterations in adrenal function contribute
to the pathogenesis of common diseases such as HTN, obesity & type 2 DM.
Both Cushing’s disease & cortisol-secreting adrenal tumours are 4 times more
common in women than men.
In contrast, ectopic ACTH syndrome .. is more common in men.
=====PAGE 2 [T]=====
It is vital to exclude iatrogenic causes in all patients with Cushing’s syndrome
since even inhaled or topical GC can induce the syndrome in susceptible
individuals.
A careful drug history must therefore be taken before embarking on complex
investigations.
An 0800–0900 hrs serum cortisol of <100 nmol/L (3.6 µg/dL) in a pt with a
normal sleep–wake pattern & Cushingoid appearance is consistent with
exogenous synthetic GC use (common) or cyclical secretion of cortisol from
endogenous Cushing’s (uncommon).
=====PAGE 3 [T]=====
Some clinical features are more common in ectopic ACTH syndrome. While ACTH-
secreting pituitary tumours retain some -ve feedback sensitivity to cortisol, this is
absent in tumours that produce ectopic ACTH, typically resulting in higher levels
of both ACTH & cortisol than are observed in pituitary-driven disease.
The high ACTH level are associated with marked pigmentation because of binding
to melanocortin 1 receptors on melanocytes in the skin.
The high cortisol levels also overcome the capacity of 11β-HSD2
to inactivate cortisol in the kidney, causing hypokalaemic alkalosis that aggravates
myopathy & hyperglycaemia (by inhibiting insulin secretion).
When the tumour that is secreting ACTH is malignant, then the onset is usually
rapid & may be associated with cachexia.
For these reasons, the classical features of Cushing’s syndrome are less common
in ectopic ACTH syndrome; if present, they suggest that a less aggressive tumour,
such as a bronchial carcinoid, is responsible.
In Cushing’s disease, the pituitary tumour is usually a microadenoma (<10 mm in
diam.); hence other features of a pit. macroadenoma are rare, but there may be
secondary hypogonadism due to the inhibitory effects of excess GC on
gonadotropin secretion.
If a diagnosis of Cushing’s is suspected, early discussion/referral to a specialist is
recommended.
Investigations
The large number of tests available that reflects the fact that each one has limited
specificity & sensitivity in isolation.
Accordingly, several tests are usually combined to establish the diag.
Testing for Cushing’s syndrome should be avoided under conditions of stress,
e.g(acute illness, because this activates the HPA axis, causing potentially spurious
results.
The diagnosis of Cushing’s is a two-step process:
1. To establish Dx
2. To define its cause .
Some additional tests are useful in all cases of Cushing’s syndrome,
including plasma electrolytes, glucose, HbA1c & BMD measurement.

Establishing the presence of Cushing’s syndrome
If there is appropriate clinical suspicion,Dx is confirmed by using 2 of 3 main
tests:
1.Failure to suppress s.cortisol with LDDST
2. Loss of the normal circadian rhythm of cortisol, with inappropriately elevated
late-night serum or salivary cortisol
3. Increased 24-hr UFC (lower sensitivity than above tests).
Dexamethasone is used for suppression testing because it does not cross-react
in immunoassays for cortisol.
An ONDST involves administration of 1 mg dexa. at 2300 hrs & measurement of
serum cortisol at 0900 hrs the following day.
In a LDDST, s. cortisol is measured following administration of 0.5 mg dexa. 4
times daily for 48 hours.
For either test,a normal response is s.cortisol of <50 nmol/L (1.8µg/dL)
It is important for any oestrogens to be stopped for 6 weeks prior to investig. to
allow CBG levels to return to normal & to avoid false +ve responses, as most
cortisol assays measure total cortisol, including that bound to CBG.
Cyclicity of cortisol secretion is a feature of all types of Cushing’s syn. &, if very
variable, can confuse diagnosis.
Use of multiple salivary cortisol samples over weeks or months can be helpful in
Dx but an elevated salivary cortisol alone should not be taken as proof of Dx.
In iatrogenic Cushing’s syn., cortisol levels are low unless the pt is taking a GC (e.g
prednisolone) that cross-reacts in immunoassays with cortisol.
Determining the underlying cause
Once presence of Cushing’s syn. is confirmed, measurement of plasma ACTH is
the key to establishing the differential diagnosis;
It is best measured in the morning around 0900 hrs.
=====PAGE 5 [T]=====
In the presence of excess cortisol secretion,
Undetectable ACTH (<1.1 pmol/L (5 ng/L)) indicates an adrenal cause. ACTH >3.3
pmol/L (15 ng/L) suggest a pituitary cause or ectopic ACTH.
ACTH levels between these values represent a ‘grey area’ & further evaluation by
a specialist is required.
Tests to discriminate pituitary from ectopic sources of ACTH rely on the fact that
pituitary tumours retain some features of normal regulation of ACTH secretion.
Thus, in pituitary-dependent Cushing’s dis., ACTH secretion is suppressed by
HDDST & ACTH is stimulated by CRH.
In a HDDST), s. cortisol is measured before & after administration of 2 mg of dexa.
4 times daily for 48 hours.
MRI detects around 60% of pituitary microadenomas secreting ACTH.
If available, (BIPSS) with measurement of p. ACTH near the pituitary & comparing
this to peripheral levels is the best means of confirming Cushing’s dis. (increased
gradient), unless MRI shows a tumour bigger than 6 mm, in which case it may not
be needed.
CT or MRI detects most adrenal tumours; adrenal carcinomas are usually large
(>5 cm) & have other features of malignancy.
Management
Untreated severe Cushing’s syn. has a 50% 5-year mortality.
Most pts are treated surgically, but medical therapy may be given in severe cases
for a few weeks prior to operation to improve the clinical state, or where there is
recurrent or incurable disease.
Drugs that inhibit GC biosynthesis form the mainstay or management, with the
most widely available & established being metyrapone & ketoconazole, with
osilodrostat becoming available more recently.
The dose of these agents is best titrated against s. cortisol levels or 24-hr UFC.
Cushing’s disease
Trans-sphenoidal surgery carried out by an experienced surgeon with selective
removal of the adenoma is the treatment of choice, 70% of pts going into
immediate remission.
=====PAGE 6 [T]=====
Around 20% of pts suffer a recurrence, often years later, emphasising the need for
life-long follow-up.
Laparoscopic bilateral adrenalectomy performed by an expert surgeon
effectively cures any type of Cushing dis., but this can result in Nelson syndrome
– an invasive pituitary macroadenoma ( local mass effects) & very high ACTH
levels (pigmentation). The risk of Nelson syndrome is reported as being reduced
by pituitary irradiation in some series, but not all.
The somatostatin analogue pasireotide is also licensed for the treatment of
Cushing’s dis. & works by suppressing ACTH secretion by the tumour.
Adrenal tumours
Laparoscopic adrenal surgery:Is a treatment of choice for adrenal adenomas.
Surgery offers the only prospect of cure for adrenocortical carcinomas but, in
general, prognosis is poor with high rates of recurrence, even in pts with localised
disease at presentation.
Radiotherapy to the tumour bed reduces the risk of local recurrence;
systemic therapy consists of adrenolytic drug mitotane & chemotherapy, but
responses are often poor.
• Ectopic ACTH syndrome
Localised tumours, such as bronchial carcinoids, should be removed surgically .
In incurable malignancy, it is important to reduce the severity of Cushing’s syn.
using medical therapy or, if appropriate, bilateral adrenalectomy.
Adrenal insufficiency
Results from inadequate secretion of cortisol &/or aldosterone.
It is potentially fatal & notoriously variable in its presentation.
A high index of suspicion is therefore required in pts with unexplained fatigue,
hyponatraemia or hypotension.
The most common is ACTH deficiency (secondary adrenocortical failure) , usually
because of inappropriate withdrawal of ch. GC therapy or a pituitary tumour.
CAH& Addison’s dis. (primary adrenocortical failure) are rare causes.

Clinical assessment
In Addison , either GC or MC deficiency may come first, but eventually all pts fail
to secrete both classes .
May present with ch. features &/or in acute circulatory shock.
With a ch. presentation, initial symptoms are often misdiagnosed as chronic
fatigue syndrome or depression.
In prim. adr insuf , weight loss is a uniform presenting feature. Adr. Insuf. should
also be considered in pts with hyponatraemia,even if asymptomatic.
Features of an acute adrenal crisis ( circulatory shock with severe hypotension,
hyponatraemia, hyperkalaemia &, in some instances, hypoglycaemia a&
hypercalcaemia). M. cramps, nausea,vomiting, diarrhoea & unexplained fever
may be present.
The crisis is often precipitated by intercurrent dis., surgery or infection.
Vitiligo occurs in 10%–20% of patients with autoimmune Addison’s disease.
Addison’s disease may be part of 2 distinct APS.
The triad of hypoparathy., adrenal Insuf. , & mucocut. Candidiasis
( type I APS), also called autoimmune polyendocrinopathy 1 (APECED), which
usually manifests in childhood. Other, less common manifestation include

present with features suggestive of chronic adrenal insufficiency.
Secondary adr. insuf. occurs commonly after the discontinuation of exogenous
GC. Alternate-day GC treatment, if feasible, results in less suppression of the HPA
axis than does daily GC therapy.
Complete recovery of the HPA axis can take 1 year or more, & the rate-limiting
step appears to be recovery of the CRH-producing neurons.
Under stress, cortisol secretion is increased. Therefore, the concept
of adrenal fatigue, proposed by some alternative providers, has no bio-
logic validity.
After stabilization of acute adr. insuf.,pts with Addison’s dis. require lifelong
replacement therapy with both GC & MC.
Many pts are overtreated with GC & undertreated with MC.
Assessment of glucocorticoids
RPC is usually low in pts with adrenal insuf. but it may be within the reference
range, yet inappropriately low, for a seriously ill patient.
Random measure of normal levels of plasma cortisol cannot therefore be used to
confirm or refute the Dx, unless it is above 430 nmol/L (>15.6 µg/dL), which
effectively excludes adrenal insufficiency.
More useful is the short ACTH stimulation test ( tetracosactrin or short
Synacthen test). Cortisol levels fail to increase in response to exogenous ACTH in
pts with primary or secondary adrenal insuf.
These can be distinguished by measurement of ACTH (which is low in ACTH
deficiency & high in Addison’s disease).
In chronic symptoms suggestive of adr insuf , a basal early morning pl. cortisol
measure, a 1-hr cosyntropin test, or both, should be performed.
These tests are not recommended in pts without symptoms .
A normal response is a p. cortisol >18 μg/dL at any time during the test. A pt
with a basal morning p. cortisol <5 μg/dL & a stimulated cortisol <18 μg/dL
probably has adr. insuf. & should receive treatment.

A basal plasma morning cortisol 10 -18 μg/dL &stimulated cortisol <18 μg/dL
probably indicates impaired adrenal reserve & a requirement for receiving
cortisol replacement under stress conditions .
Birth control pills & oral estrogens increase CBG levels; &low free cortisol level,
making interpretation of the test difficult for these patients.
• Assessment of MC
MC secretion in pts with suspected Addison’s cannot be adequately assessed by
electrolyte measurements since
1.Hyponatraemia occurs in both aldosterone & cortisol deficiency .
2.Hyperkalaemia is common, but not universal, in aldosterone deficiency.
Plasma renin & aldosterone should be measured in the supine position.
In MC deficiency, plasma renin activity is high, with plasma aldosterone being
either low or in the lower part of the reference range.
• Assessment of adrenal androgens
This is not necessary in men because testosterone from the testes is the principal
androgen.
In women, DHEAS & androstenedione may be measured in a random specimen of
blood, though levels are highest in the morning.

Management
Always need GC replacement therapy & usually, but not always, MC .
Other treatments depend on the underlying cause.
GC replacement
consists of oral hydrocortisone (cortisol) 15–20 mg daily in divided doses.
These are physiological replacement doses that should not cause Cushingoid SE.
Excess weight gain usually indicates over-replacement, while persistent lethargy
or hyperpigmentation may be due to an inadequate dose or lack of absorption.
Measurement of s. cortisol levels is not usually helpful.
MC replacement
Fludrocortisone is administered at the usual dose of 0.05–0.15 mg daily, &
adequacy of replacement may be assessed by measurement of BP, Pl. electrolytes
& pl. renin.
It is indicated for virtually every pt with primary adrenal insufficiency but is not
needed in secondary adrenal insufficiency.
Androgen replacement
DHEAS (50 mg/day) is occasionally given to women with primary adr. insuf. who
have syptoms of reduced libido & fatigue, but the evidence in support of this is

Primary hyperaldosteronism
Estimates of the prevalence of primary hyperaldosteronism vary according to the
screening tests employed, but it may occur in as many as 10% of people with
HTN.
Indications to test for MC excess in hypertensive pts include hypokalaemia
(including hypokalaemia induced by thiazide diuretics), poor control of BP with
conventional therapy, a FH of early-onset HTN, or presentation at a young age.
=====PAGE 13 [T]=====
It is important to differentiate prim. hyperald., caused by an intrinsic abnormality
of the adr. gl. resulting in aldosterone excess, from secondary hyperald., which is
usually a consequence of enhanced activity of renin in response to inadequate
renal perfus. & hypotension.
Most individuals with primary hyperald. have BAH(idiopathic
hyperaldosteronism), while only a minority have APA(Conn syndrome).
Glucocorticoid-suppressible hyperaldosteronism is a rare AD condition in which
aldosterone is secreted ‘ectopically’ from the adrenal zonae fasciculata/reticularis
in response to ACTH.
Rarely, the MC receptor pathway in the distal nephron is activated, even though
aldosterone concentrations are low.
Clinical features
usually asymptomatic but may have features of Na+ retention or k loss.
Na+ retention may cause oedema, while hypokalaemia may cause muscle
weakness (or even paralysis, especially in South-east Asian populations), polyuria
(secondary to RT damage, (nephrogenic DI)) & occasionally tetany (because of
associated Metab. alkalosis & low ionised calcium). BP is elevated but accelerated
phase HTN is rare.
Investigations
• Biochemical
Routine blood tests may show hypokalaemic alkalosis.
Sodium is usually at the upper end of the reference range in primary
hyperald.,but is characteristically low in secondary hyperald. (because low plasma
volume stimulates vasopressin (ADH) release & high angiotensin I stimulate thirst)
The key measurements are pl. renin & aldost. , & in many centres the ARR is
employed as a screening test for primary aldost. in HTN pts.
Almost all antihypertensive drugs interfere with this ratio (β-blockers inhibit while
diuretics stimulate renin secretion).
Thus, individuals with an elevated ARR require further testing after stopping many
antihypertensive drugs for at least 4 weeks (although if the ratio is still high
despite a pt taking ACEI or ARB drugs then this need not be done as these agents
would normally cause a lowering of the ratio). If necessary, antihypertensive
=====PAGE 14 [T]=====
agents that have minimal effects on the RAS, such as CCB & α-blockers, may be
substituted.
Oral K supplementation may also be required, as hypokalaemia itself suppresses
renin activity. If, on repeat testing, pl. renin is low & aldost concentrations are
elevated, then further investigation under specialist supervision may include
saline or fludrocortisone suppression tests.
Imaging & localisation
Imaging with CT or MRI will identify most APAs ,but it is important to recognise
the risk of false +ve (non functioning adrenal adenomas are common) & false -ve
(imaging may have insufficient resolution to identify adenomas with a diameter of
<0.5 cm).
If the imaging is inconclusive & there is an intention to proceed with surgery on
the basis of strong bioch. evidence of an APA, then adrenal vein catheterisation
with measurement of aldosterone (&cortisol to confirm positioning of the
catheters) is usually required.
In some centres, this is performed even in the presence of a unilateral ‘adenoma’,
to avoid inadvertent removal of incidental non-functioning adenoma contralateral
to a radiologically inapparent cause of aldosterone excess.

Management
MRA (spironolactone & eplerenone) are valuable in treating both hypokalaemia &
HTN in all forms of MC excess.
Up to 20% of males develop gynaecomastia on spironolactone.
Amiloride (10–40 mg/d), which blocks the epithelial Na+ channel regulated by
aldosterone, is an alternative.
In pts with an APA, medical therapy is usually given for a few wks to normalise
whole-body electrolyte balance before unilateral adrenalectomy.
Laparoscopic surgery cures the biochemical abnormality but, depending on the
pre-operative duration, HTN remains in as many as 70% of cases, probably
because of irreversible damage to the systemic microcirculation.
Phaeochromocytoma & paraganglioma
Are rare neuro-endocrine tumours that may secrete catecholamines (adrenaline
& noradrenaline).
80% of these tumours occur in the adrenal medulla (phaeoch.), while 20% arise
elsewhere in the body in sympathetic ganglia(paragangl.).
Most are benign , 15% show malignant features.
40% are associated with inherited dis., including NF, VHL syndrome, MEN 2a &
MEN 2b.
Paragangliomas are particularly associated with mutations in the succinate
dehydrogenase A, B, C & D genes.
Other genetic causes include mutations in SDHA, SDHAF2,TMEN127 & MAX.
Clinical features
These depend on the pattern of catecholamine secretion .
Some pts present with HTN, although it has been estimated that phaeo.
accounts for < 0.1% of cases of HTN.
The presentation may be with a complication of HTN, such as stroke, MI, LVF,HTN
retinopathy or accelerated HTN.

The apparent paradox of postural hypotension between episodes is explained
by‘pressure natriuresis’ during hypertensive episodes so that intravascular
volume is reduced. There may also be features of the familial syndromes
associated with phaeo .
Paragangliomas are often non-functioning.
Investigations
Excessive secretion of catecholamines can be confirmed by measuring
metabolites in plasma &/or urine (metanephrine & normetanephrine) .
There is a high ‘false-positive’ rate in stressed pts ( acute illness,following
vigorous exercise or severe pain) & following ingestion of some drug e.g TCA.
For this reason, a repeat sample should usually be requested if elevated levels are
found, although, as a rule, the higher the conc. of metanephrines, the more likely
the diagnosis of phaeo./paragang.
S. chromogranin A is often elevated & may be a useful tumour marker in pts with
non-secretory tumours &/or metastatic dis.
Genetic testing if other features of a genetic syndrome, in those with a FH of
phaeoch./paragangl., &in those presenting under the age of 50 years.
Localisation
Phaeo. are usually identified by abdominal CT or MRI.

Management
In functioning tumours, medical therapy is required to prepare the pt for surgery,
preferably for a minimum of 6 weeks, to allow restoration of normal plasma
volume.
The most useful is the α-blocker phenoxybenzamine , because it is a non-
competitive antagonist, unlike prazosin or doxazosin.
If α-blockade produces a marked tachycardia, then a β-blocker such as
propranolol can be added.
On no account should a β-blocker be given before an α-blocker, as this may
cause a paradoxical rise in BP .
During surgery, sodium nitroprusside & phentolamine are useful in controlling
hypertensive episodes, which may result from anaesthetic induction or tumour
mobilisation.

Congenital adrenal hyperplasia
Inherited defects in enzymes of the cortisol biosynthetic pathway result in
insufficiency of hormones downstream of the block, with impaired
– ve feedback & increased ACTH secretion.
ACTH then stimulates production of steroids upstream of enz. block.
This produces adrenal hyperplasia & a combination of cl. f. that depend on the
severity & site of the defect in biosynthesis.
All of these enzyme abnormalities are inherited as AR traits.
The most common enzyme defect is 21-hydroxylase deficiency.
This results in impaired synthesis of cortisol & aldosterone, & accumulation of
17-OH-progesterone, which is then diverted to form adrenal androgens.
In about 1/3 of cases, this defect is severe & presents in infancy with features of
GC & MC deficiency & androgen excess, such as ambiguous genitalia in girls.
In the other 2/3, MC secretion is adequate but there may be features of cortisol
insuffciency &/or ACTH & androgen excess, including precocious pseudo-
puberty, which is distinguished from ‘true’ precocious puberty by low
gonadotrophins.
Investigations
Sometimes the mildest enzyme defects are not apparent until adult life, when
females may present with amenorrhoea &/or hirsutism. This is called ‘non-
classical’ or ‘late-onset’ CAH.
Defects of all the other enzymes are rare. Both 17-hydroxylase & 11β-hydroxylase
deficiency may produce HTN due to excess production of 11-deoxycorticosterone,
which has MC activity.
Management
The aim is to replace deficient corticosteroids & to suppress ACTH-driven adrenal
androgen production.
A careful balance is required between adequate suppression of adr. androgen
excess & excessive GC replacement resulting in features of Cushing’s syn.
In children, growth velocity is an important measurement, since either under- or
over-replacement with GC suppresses growth.
=====PAGE 19 [T]=====
In adults, there is no uniformly agreed adrenal replacement regimen, & cl. f.
(menstrual cycle, hirsutism, weight gain, BP) & bioch. profiles (pl. renin, 17-OH-
progesterone & testosterone levels) provide a guide.
Women with late-onset 21-hydroxylase deficiency may not require corticosteroid
replacement. If hirsutism is the main problem, anti-androgen therapy may be just
as effective.
