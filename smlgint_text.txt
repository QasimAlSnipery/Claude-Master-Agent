Imaging Techniques
Barium or contrast examination:
• Small bowel follow-through : patient drinks about 200–300 mL of barium and
its passage through the small intestine is observed by taking films at regular
intervals until the barium reaches the colon, usually takes 2–3 hours, but the
transit time is very variable. The indications are suspected inflammatory bowel
disease (Crohn’s disease), suspected stricture, malabsorption, enterocutaneous
fistulae, post small bowel resection, to assess small bowel length, in short bowel
syndrome, malrotation
• Enteroclysis: barium is injected through the tube followed by water or methyl
cellulose to propel the barium through the small bowel, allowing distension of
bowel and a double-contrast effect, gives excellent mucosal detail. The
disadvantage is that it requires intubation with a nasoduodenal tube,
• Barium enema: Firstly, the bowel is prepared, barium is run into the colon
under gravity through a tube inserted into the rectum. Air is then blown in to
push the barium around the colon with the result that the colon is distended with
air and the mucosa coated with barium, giving the ‘double-contrast’ effect.
Single contrast enema is only using barium.

Imaging Techniques
CT: usually performed using IV contrast, sometime using water or
Gastrografin, either orally or rectally or both, according to its indication:
• Assessment of small and large bowel strictures and masses, also
staging them.
• Diagnosis of focal inflammation like diverticulitis, appendicitis, more
diffuse inflammation like inflammatory bowel diseases.
• Intestinal obstruction, site of obstruction.
• Mesenteric vessels ischemia, in this case CT angiography is used,
which should include arterial and venous phases.
• Intestinal perforation, if erect chest X ray is not diagnostic.
• Help in diagnosis of GI bleeding
• In cases of trauma to exclude intestinal perforation, mesenteric
injuries, in addition to diagnosis of solid organ injuries

Imaging Techniques
• Ultrasound: It can demonstrate bowel thickening and free fluid
associated with inflammatory conditions or tumor. It can demonstrate a
thickened appendix suggestive of appendicitis, diverticulitis, presence of
intussusception.
• Perineal or transrectal ultrasound to assess perianal fistula and
abscess.
• MRI: challenging because movement artifact from peristalsis, but mainly
used for rectal and anal imaging where movement is minimal. MR
enterography is used to assess inflammatory small intestinal disease like
Crohn’s disease, but needs preparation and special techniques.
• Nuclear medicine techniques: using (MIBG) or octreotide, may be used
in cases of suspected neuroendocrine or carcinoid tumours.
Technetium-99m (99mTc) may be used to detect Meckel’s diverticulum.

Imaging Findings of disease:
Dilatation
• Small intestine: when diameter is more than 3cm, usually indicates
malabsorption, paralytic ileus or small bowel obstruction .
• Large bowel: when diameter more than 6cm is abnormal dilatation.
Causes are obstruction, paralytic ileus, volvulus, toxic megacolon in
ulcerative colitis, Hirschsprung’s disease and megacolon in pediatric age
group.
• Colonoscopy and barium enema may only show one end of the stricture,
whereas CT will usually demonstrate the cause of the obstruction.
• Paralytic ileus is usually made on clinical grounds with the help of plain
films of the abdomen. A water-soluble contrast enema is occasionally
undertaken which shows a dilated, but otherwise normal, colon in the
case of paralytic ileus .

=====PAGE 11 [T]=====
Imaging Findings of disease:
Narrowing of the lumen more seen in colon, may be due pathology:
• inside the lumen of the bowel (e.g. impacted faeces)
• inside the bowel wall (tumor, may be due to physiological spasm)
• outside the bowel wall due to compression by an extrinsic mass.
When attempting to diagnose the nature of a stricture in the colon the following
points should be borne in mind:
• Shape and length: neoplastic strictures have shouldered edges, an irregular
lumen and are rarely more than 6 cm in length. Whereas benign strictures
classically have tapered ends, a relatively smooth outline and may be of any
length.
• Site of the stricture: diverticular disease are in sigmoid colon. Ischaemic
strictures are usually centred somewhere between the splenic flexure and the
sigmoid colon. Crohn’s disease and tuberculosis have a predilection for the
caecum.
•Associated finding: Ulceration, sacculation, diverticuli

Inflammatory bowel disease
Ulcerative colitis
• a disease of unknown aetiology characterized by
inflammation and ulceration of the colon.
• It involves the rectum, ascends to other parts of colon in
continuity.
• When the whole colon is involved, the terminal ileum may
become dilated.
• Radiological sign is widespread ulceration which are
usually shallow, but in severe cases may be quite deep.
There is loss of the normal colonic haustra in the affected
portions of the colon.
10/27/2025 Soran Hisham 14
=====PAGE 15 [T]=====
Inflammatory bowel disease
• In severe cases narrowing and shortening of the colon, giving the
appearance of a rigid tube, and pseudopolyps are seen. “Pseudopolyps
are small filling defects projecting into the lumen of the bowel,
formed by swollen mucosa in between the areas of ulceration”
• Strictures are rare, and, when present, are likely to be due to
carcinoma.
• On CT & MRE, there is bowel wall thickening up to 5–8 mm in
continuity from the rectum to the proximal extent of disease. Mucosa
enhances brightly “active inflammation”. In chronic disease there is
visible fatty infiltration in the submucosa, resulting in a ‘target sign’
appearance.
• Toxic dilatation (toxic megacolon) is a serious complication. The
diagnosis is made on clinical grounds and on examination of the plain
abdominal film or standard CT.

Inflammatory bowel disease
• Crohn’s disease:
• disease of unknown aetiology characterized by localized areas of chronic
granulomatous inflammation, which nearly always affects the terminal ileum. It
affects several different parts of the small and large intestine and leaving normal
intervening bowel, known as skip lesions.
• The major signs on barium and cross sectional imaging:
– Strictures, which are extremely variable in length. Sometimes a loop of bowel is
so narrow, either from spasm or oedema and fibrosis in the bowel wall, that its
appearance has been called the ‘string sign’. Dilatation proximal to stricture.
– Contraction of the caecum
– Ulcers; these are sometimes quite deep. Fine ulceration combined with mucosal
oedema gives rise to the so-called ‘cobblestone’ appearance’.
– Thickening, distortion or effacement of mucosal folds.
– Separation of bowel loops due to bowel wall thickening or an inflammatory mass
in mesentery between loops.
– Fistulae to other small bowel loops, colon, bladder or vagina

Diverticular disease
• Diverticula are sac-like out-pouchings of mucosa through the
muscular layer of the bowel wall. Diverticula are common,
particularly in the elderly. They are seen in all parts of the colon, but
are commonest in the sigmoid colon.
• Diverticulitis is when diverticulum is infected, then causes symptoms
such as pain, sepsis, diarrhoea or obstruction.
• Barium enema: non-complicated diverticula are seen as spherical
out-pouchings with a narrow neck, but when inflamed the neck of
divertiocum is occluded.
• CT: in diverticulitis the diverticulum and adjacent bowel wall appears
thick-walled with surrounding far stranding, occasionaly perforate, on
CT there might be pneumoperitoneum or abscess.
• Stricture from diverticular disease is impossible to differentiate from
carcinoma.

Appendicitis
• In most cases, the diagnosis of appendicitis is obvious
clinically and imaging is unnecessary. In cases of
doubt the diagnosis can be made with ultrasound,
which shows a distended >6mm, non-compressible
appendix with a thickened oedematous wall, often
accompanied by free fluid and surrounding fat
inflammation.
• Similar changes can be demonstrated with CT. An
appendix abscess can be diagnosed with CT or
ultrasound as a mass in the right iliac fossa.

Intestinal Obstruction
• Small intestine obstruction mainly dur to adhesions or
obstructed hernia, but in large intestine mostly due to
tumors.
• Diagnosis is usually made on clinical history and
examination with the help of plain abdominal films.
• CT is useful technique to demonstrate the site of
obstruction by showing the transition zone from dilated to
collapsed small bowel, and also to confirm or exclude a
mass at the site of obstruction.
• No imaging technique can directly demonstrate adhesions,
the commonest cause of small bowel obstruction.

Volvulus
• A loop of bowel twists on its mesentery. This happens most
frequently in the sigmoid colon, particularly when it is
redundant, and less often in the caecum. The twisted loop
becomes greatly distended and the bowel proximal to the
volvulus is obstructed by the twist and may, therefore, also be
dilated.
• The diagnosis is usually made on plain abdominal films, but a
CT may be helpful in doubtful cases
• Abdominal X-ray shows coffee-bean shaped dilated loop of
large bowel
• CT & US demonstrate “whirl-sign” which denotes twisting of
the mesenteric vessels.

Intussusception
• An intussusception is the invagination of one segment of the bowel
into another. Infants and young children are much more liable to
intussusception than adults.
• The diagnosis of intussusception in children is often made using
ultrasound, by diagnosing an abdominal mass often with a
hyperechoic centre, often confirmed by an enema with air or carbon
dioxide as the contrast agent, and an attempt at reduction of the
intussusception is made using the increased pressure of the introduced
air or carbon dioxide.
• In adults, intussusception is almost always due to the presence of a
neoplasm. The diagnosis is usually made on CT. A sausage-shaped
mass is demonstrated, which has mesenteric fat within the lumen of
the intussuscipiens.

Mesenteric Infarction, Ischaemic colitis
• Small Intestinal infarction, a serious life-threatening condition, is
caused by occlusion of the superior mesenteric artery. There may be
thickening and oedema of the wall of the small bowel, and gas may be
seen within the bowel wall.
• It is diagnosed by CT, performed in the arterial and portal venous
phase, which shows filling defect “thrombus” in artery or vein, in
addition to above mentioned features
• Colon is affected by chronic mesenteric ischaemia, present with vague
symptoms, post-prandial pain. On CT there is narrowing of the
arteries supplying the bowel, also wall thickening or stricture might be
seen.
• On barium & occasionally on plain abdomen X ray, smooth
indentation in lumen called thumb print sign “from mucosal
haemorrhage and oedema”, stricture might be seen. Usually centred
between the splenic flexure and the sigmoid colon “most vulnerable
blood supply”

Tuberculosis
• It causes contraction of the caecum, dilated terminal ileum.
• On CT, there may be signs of diffuse peritoneal
involvement, with ascites, thickening of the omentum,
peritoneal and serosal nodules, and lymph node
enlargement that may contain necrosis.
• These appearances may be indistinguishable from
disseminated intraperitoneal malignancy, or Crohn’s
disease.
• Biopsy is usually required to establish the diagnosis.

Polyps
• The word ‘polyp’ means a small mass of tissue arising
from the wall of the bowel projecting into the lumen. They
are best investigated by endoscopy, but may be found at
barium enema or CT pneumocolon. It is often impossible
on radiological grounds to exclude frank malignancy.
• Only a tiny minority of polyps less than 1 cm in size, and
very few less than 2 cm, are cancers.
• The features that suggest malignancy are a diameter of
more than 2 cm, a short thick stalk or an irregular surface.
• Some polyps might be sessile.
10/27/2025 Soran Hisham 38
=====PAGE 39 [T]=====
Carcinoma
• Carcinomas may arise anywhere in the colon, uncommon in
small intestine, commonest in the rectosigmoid region and
caecum.
• The patient with a rectosigmoid carcinoma often has an annular
stricture and features of obstruction, whereas with a caecal
carcinoma the tumour can become very large without
obstructing the bowel, so anaemia and weight loss are the
common presenting features.
• Barium enema: shows cancer either as irregular stricture with
shouldered edges, rarely more than 6 cm in length, or fungating
mass as irregular filling defect projecting into the lumen of the
bowel.
10/27/2025 Soran Hisham 39
=====PAGE 40 [T]=====
• CT: shows carcinoma as thickening of the bowel wall and
an irregular narrowing of the lumen of the colon.
• MRI: is used for preoperative assessment of the local
extent of rectal carcinoma.
• CT is used to demonstrate distant metastases to the liver or
lungs and to identify para- aortic nodal disease. CT can
also inform on the local staging of a colonic tumor .
• FDG-PET/CT is not currently used routinely for initial
staging of colorectal cancer, but it is used in patients who
are to be considered for potential curative surgery (e.g.
hepatic resection for liver metastases) to ensure there are
no other sites of metastasis.

Lymphoma
• CT and ultrasound can show very marked thickening
of the bowel wall, in spite of marked wall thickening
there is no narrowing of lumen, which can help
differentiate between Crohn’s disease and lymphoma.
• CT can also demonstrate mesenteric and para- aortic
lymphadenopathy and lymphomatous deposits in the
liver and spleen.
