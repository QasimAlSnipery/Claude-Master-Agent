Hypercalcemic crisis: presentation and management
Hypercalcemia is documented in up to 5% of hospitalized patients.
 The vast majority are asymptomatic with a mild to moderate elevation
of serum calcium (12-14mg/dL, respectively) and respond to treatment
 of the underlying etiology with associated dietary modification. 
===SLIDE 5===
A small proportion of patients will have symptoms with a total calcium> 14mg/dL
This is referred to as a hypercalcemic crisis and requires aggressive medical management.
===SLIDE 6===
The typical presentation
Acute confusion
Abdominal pain
Vomiting 
Dehydration
 Anuria
===SLIDE 7===
Prolongation of the PR interval with a shortened QT interval can be identified on an electrocardiogram (ECG) before potentially lethal cardiac arrhythmias. 
Where the calcium is > 18 mg/dL, coma and cardiac arrest can occur.
===SLIDE 8===
Treatment revolves 
Increasing renal excretion of calcium
 Reducing skeletal release of calcium
 Treatment of the underlying cause. 
===SLIDE 9===
Aggressive rehydration plays a pivotal
role. 
Typically, 200–500 mL/h of normal saline is given to
maintain a urine output >100 mL/h 
may be modified to account for associated patient comorbidities
===SLIDE 10===
Once intravascular volume has been adequately restored,
Loop diuretics, such as furosemide, can be used to enhance the
renal excretion of calcium.
 The majority of patients will have normalization of their calcium with these simple measures.
===SLIDE 11===
In patients with advanced malignancy and a serum calcium
level >12mg/dl agents that blunt the release of calcium from
Skeletal stores may be required
===SLIDE 12===
Bisphosphonates. 
Inhibits osteoclast activity. In the acute setting, these are given intravenously owing to poor absorption in the gastrointestinal tract
===SLIDE 13===
Calcitonin It has a short duration of action and is usually used as a bridge to reduce calcium until the sustained action of the bisphosphonates is seen. 
 Glucocorticoids (prednisolone) can be used to enhance the
action of calcitonin. 
They increase calciuresis and decrease intestinal absorption of calcium. They may also play a role in diseases associated with vitamin D excess.

What congenital anomaly arises from the formation ofthe thyroid gland?
A. The thyroid isthmus
B. The cricothyroid arch
C. A thyroglossal duct cyst
D. An endobranchial cyst
===SLIDE 16===
Answer: C
The medial thyroid anlage descends from the base of the
tongue through a channel called the thyroglossal duct at
week 3–4 of gestation.
===SLIDE 17===
The duct normally closes after its descent, but may remain patent and is susceptible to secondary infection and dilatation, referred to as a thyroglossal duct cyst. 
Removal is accomplished with the Sistrunk operation, which also removes the central portion of the hyoid bone.

===SLIDE 31===
Which hormone directly mediates the release of thyroidhormones from the gland?
A. Thyroglobulin
B. Thyrotropin-releasing hormone (TRH)
C. Thyroid Stimulating Hormone (TSH)
D. Adrenalin
===SLIDE 32===
Answer: C
Thyroglobulin is contained within the follicles of the thyroid
gland and its tyrosine residues are iodinated to produce tetra-
iodothyronine or thyroxin (T4) or triiodothyronine (T3).
===SLIDE 33===
The hypothalamus secretes TRH and stimulates the pituitary gland to secrete TSH, which directly mediates the release of T4 and T3 from the gland.
 Adrenalin does not directly regulate T4 or T3 release.

An adolescent patient with a thyroid mass undergoes fine needle aspiration biopsy, which returns as medullary carcinoma of the thyroid (MCT).

===SLIDE 46===
A. Hyperparathyroidism
B. Pheochromocytoma
C. Mucocutaneous ganglioneuromas
D. All of the above
===SLIDE 47===
Answer: D

===SLIDE 48===
Postoperative complications of thyroid surgery which may require immediate reoperation include:
A. Hypocalcemia.
B. Dyspnea.
C. Dystonia.
D. Wound hematoma.
===SLIDE 49===
Answer: D
Inadvertent injury (ischemia) or removal of the parathyroid glands can cause acute neuromuscular excitability due to hypocalcemia.
 This can be ameliorated with intravenous calcium.
 Dyspnea may be a consequence of pain or anxiety, but rarely indicates airway compromise.
===SLIDE 50===
Bilateral vocal cord dysfunction with airway compromise requires reintubation.
An expanding hematoma in the neck may not cause bleeding from the wound but can compress the membranous portion of the trachea and cause dyspnea.
 The wound should be opened at the bedside and the patient returned to the operating room for wound exploration and reclosure
