Etiology
1- Genetic factors
2- Hormonal changes in late pregnancy. So DDH is rare in premature babies .
3- Intrauterine malposition . Breech position with extended legs.
4- Postnatal factors. High incidence of DDH in those swaddling their babies tightly
and putting them in Cradleboard.While it is rare in African people who carry their
babies astride their backs with the hips abducted.

Unilateral = Up to 10 years surgery.In older than 10 years, the force needed for
reduction may damage the hip and causes AVN.
Bilateral = After 4 years No operation is performed because:
1- The deformity is symmetrical ,therefor less noticeable.
2- The untreated patient has a waddling gait through life and may be
surprisingly uncomplaining .
2- Failure to one side leads to asymmetrical deformity.
3- If disability becomes severe, reconstructive surgery may be justified.

Perthes’ disease(Legg-Calve-Perthes)
=====PAGE 24 [T]=====
Perthes’ disease
Idiopathic AVN of the proximal femoral epiphysis of childhood .
Affects 1 in 10 000 children
Boys more affected than girls 2/1
Commonly presents between4-8 years of age
Bilateral cases are seen in 10%
It is associated with other congenital conditions as
Genitourinary malformation ,
Undescended testis,
Inguinal hernia,
Down’s syndrome.
Pathogenesis : Trauma or a non-specific synovitis may result in effusion of the hip. This
effusion can press on the lateral epiphyseal vessels ,leading to avascular necrosis of the
femoral head.
Pathology : The pathological process takes 2-4 years to complete. It starts with bone death
of the femoral head , followed by revasularization and repair if a small piece affected , or
followed by distortion and remodeling if a large piece is affected
=====PAGE 25 [T]=====
Clinical features
• Boy
• 4-8 years age
• Painful or painless limping.
• The pain is often referred to the knee and wore on activity.
• Positive Trendelenburg
• Early on the joint is irritable, so all movements are diminished,
and their extremes are painful. Later on most movements are full
except Abduction and Internal rotation

Treatment
Under 6 years age
Non-operative treatment :
Activity restriction ,Physiotherapy, Abduction brace, Using cruch.
Above 6 years age
Containment : Means taking active steps to seat the femoral head congruently and as fully as possible in
the acetabular socket , so that it may retain its sphericity and not become displaced during the period of
healing and re-modelling .This is achieved by:
1/Holding the hips widely abducted in a plaster cast or in a removable brace for at least one year
2/ Operation : either a varus osteotomy of the femur or innominate osteotomy of the pelvis or Both

=====PAGE 32 [T]=====
Femoral anteversion(In-toe-gait)
• The commonest cause of in-toe gait is excessive anteversion of the femoral neck so that
internal rotation of the hip is increased and external rotation diminished.
• The gait looks clumsy.
• The child tends to trip when running
• While standing , the patellae are turned inwards( Squinting patellae)
• These children often sit on the floor in the so called Television position( with the knees
facing each other) .
• Femoral anteversion can be assessed clinically , by US, or Axial CT scan.
• Usually improves with growth .The anteversion decreases gradually from 40 to 20
degreesby the age of 10 years.
• These children should be encouraged to adopt the Buddha postion( with the knees turned
outwards)
• Correction by osteotomy rarely indicated and certainly not before the age of 8 years .

Congenital Talipes Equinovarus
( Idiopathic Club foot)
=====PAGE 35 [T]=====
Congenital = Present at birth .
Talipes = Tali ( Talus) + Pes (Foot)= Ankle and Foot
Equinus = Horse foot
Varus = Displacement towards the midline.
Club = Heavy stick with a thick end
=====PAGE 36 [T]=====
• The foot is curved downwards and inwards.
• Ankle : Equinus ( Plantarflexion)
• Heel(Calcaneum) : Varus
• Forfoot : Adducted + Supinated + Downward
• Skin + Soft tissues of the Calf + Medial side of foot are short.
• If not corrected early , secondary bony changes occur.
=====PAGE 37 [T]=====
Incidence :
• 1-3 / 1000 births
• Boys / Girls = 2:1
• Bilateral in 50% of cases.
Cause : Unknown
=====PAGE 38 [T]=====
Clinical Features:
• Obvious at birth.
• The sole faces posteromedially .
• Heel : Small and High
• There are deep Creases posteriorly and medially.
• In a normal baby , the foot can be dorsiflexed and everted until the toes almost
touch the front of the leg . In clubfoot this is impossible .
• In babies with clubfoot always exam for Congenital hip dislocation and Spina
bifida .
=====PAGE 39 [T]=====
Treatment
Treatment should begin early,within few days of birth.
Ponseti technique is the international standard of care with more
than 90% success rate.
=====PAGE 40 [T]=====
Ponseti method
• Consists of repeated manipulation and POP casting weekly for 5-6 sessions.
• Followed by Percutaneous Tenotomy of the Achilles tendon + POP for 3
weeks.
• Then wearing Foot Abduction Brace for 3 months continuously , and for 3
years during sleep.
=====PAGE 41 [T]=====
• Resistant cases will need open release of the tight
structures , fixing by K wires and POP .
• Relapsed cases in older children need Ilizarov technique.
=====PAGE 42 [T]=====
Peripheral nerve injuries
Three degrees of PNI:
1-Neurapraxia: Demyelination of axon
Caused by mechanical pressure
Full recovery is expected
2- Axonotmesis: Interruption of axons
After closed fractures and dislocations
May be full recovery , partial recovery, or no recovery
3- Neurotmesis: Division of the nerve trunk
Occurs in open wounds
After surgical repair there may be adequate function.
=====PAGE 43 [T]=====
Clinical features
• Feeling of numbness or tingling
• Muscle weakness.
• Abnormal posture
• Trophic ulcers
• Loss of Sweating
• Change of sweating
• Electrodiagnostic tests are accurate after few
weeks from the injury.
=====PAGE 44 [T]=====
Principles of treatment
Closed injury = Neurapraxia OR Axonetmesis. Wait for few weeks, expecting
spontaneous recovery.
Open injury = The earlier the repair ,the better the recovery . End to end suturing
OR Nerve graft.
If presented after weeks or months from injury = Delayed repair.
Care of paralyzed parts: While recovery is awaited:
(1) The joints moved through full range twice a day to prevent stiffness.
(2) Skin is protected from friction damage and burns.
(3) Splintage to prevent contracture.
Tendon transfer : Sometimes performed when no muscle recovery within 18-24
months . The donor muscle should have adequate power. Best example is radial nerve.

Obstetrical Brachial Plexus Injuries
Caused by excessive traction on the brachial plexus during childbirth.
The patterns are:
1- Upper root injury(Erb’s palsy)
2- Lower root palsy(Klumpke’s palsy)
3- Total plexus palsy.

• commonest
• Injury of C5 + C6 ± C7
• Paralysis of :
Shoulder Abductors
Shoulder Ext. Rotators
Forearm Supinators
• The Arm is held:
To the side
Internally rotated
Pronated

Treatment
After few weeks from birth:
1- Complete recovery
2- Partial recovery
3- No recovery at all
• Physiotherapy from the start to keep the joints mobile.
• X-ray to exclude fracture clavicle or around shoulder.
• If no biceps recovery by 6 months ,operation should be
considered.This surgery includes excision of the scar and
nerve graft OR nerve transfer. These are very demanding
surgeries , should be undertaken only in specialized
centers

Later in childhood, the possible surgeries are :
1- Subscapularis release to correct adduction and internal rotation.
2- Tendon transfer to improve abduction.
3- Rotation osteotomy of the humerus to improve the appearance.

Treatment of radial nerve injury
• In closed fracture of humerus,crutch palsy,tourniqet palsy and saturday night palsy ,the lesion is usually
neurapraxia or axonotmesis ,spontaneous recovery is the rule. If no sign of recovery by 8-12 weeks, the
nerve should be explored and repaired or grafted.
• In open wound,nerve repair as early as possible.
• Nerve injury after manipulation or surgery of fractured humerus needs exploration(Iatrogenic injury)
• If no recovery after repair, tendon transfer gives rewarding results.
• In all cases while recovery is awaited , the wrist should be splinted in extension

Hamstring muscles in the thigh
+ All muscles below knee
+ Skin below knee except skin over the shin of tibia.
=====PAGE 62 [T]=====
Sciatic nerve injury
1-Gunshot.
2- Stab wounds .
3-Iatrogenic accident during surgery( e.g Hip replacement)
4- Traction lesions with traumatic hip dislocation and pelvic fractures .
Features:
• Paralysis of all muscles below knee ( No foot dorsiflexion no plantarflexion)
• Weak knee flexion(hamstrings paralysis)
• Sensation is lost below the knee except the skin over the shin of tibia.
• Trophic ulcers in the foot and toes may occur.
• Foot drop , so there is high stepping gait to avoid dragging the insensitive foot on the ground.
• Ankle jerk is absent .
