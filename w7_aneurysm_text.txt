Introduction:
• An arterial aneurysm is defined as an increase in
arterial diameter by 1.5 times the normal diameter
with involvement of all three layers.
• It occurs throughout the body but is most prevalent
in the infrarenal aorta.
• For the infrarenal aorta an aneurysm is defined as
greater than 3 cm or 50% larger than a normal
proximal segment.

Introduction:
• For infrarenal abdominal aorta other definitions have
used a 3-cm threshold .
• Iliac, popliteal, and femoral aneurysms follow aortic
aneurysms in frequency and are often coexistent with
aortic aneurysms.
• Aneurysms throughout the rest of the peripheral
vascular system may be related to other pathological
processes such as arteritis, infection, and connective
tissue disorders.

Introduction:
• Ectasia: intermediate stage of enlargement, when an artery is
abnormally large but less than 50% greater than normal.
• Arteriomegaly: diffuse continuous enlargement of multiple arterial
segments dilated to greater than 50% of normal.
• Aneurysmosis: is a term used to describe multiple aneurysms in
several anatomical locations.
• Both aneurysms and arteriomegaly are associated with an
increased risk of aneurysmal disease in first degree relatives.

Introduction:
• True vs. False aneurysms:
• The distinction is dependent on the vascular
wall.
• A true aneurysm has all three layers of the
arterial wall.
• A false aneurysm or pseudo aneurysm has
part of the wall containing connective tissue or
contained hematoma.

=====PAGE 6 [T]=====
Introduction:
• False aneurysm ( pseudo aneurysm ) :
• It usually has a neck or narrow conduit between the affected
artery and the main pseudo aneurysm cavity.
• Peripheral false aneurysms are usually the result of iatrogenic
peripheral arterial access required for imaging or therapy.
• Another cause of false aneurysms is loss of anastomotic
integrity at the site of prior surgical anastomosis.

=====PAGE 7 [T]=====
Introduction:
• Shape of aneurysms:
• Fusiform or saccular
• Fusiform: generalized increase in the diameter of the
affected vessel.
• Saccular: localized, eccentric defects arising from a
focal ulcer or weakness in the arterial wall, often as a
result of trauma or infection.
• Saccular concentric aneurysms.

=====PAGE 8 [T]=====
Introduction:
• Most peripheral arterial aneurysms are associated with
atherosclerotic degeneration of the entire wall and a
resultant fusiform or concentric saccular morphology.
• Renal and cerebral artery aneurysms have eccentric
saccular morphology.
• Penetrating atherosclerotic ulcers and intramural
hematomas cause focal tear or partial disruption of the
arterial wall and cause saccular aneurysms.

Abdominal aortic aneurysm ( AAA ) :
• Aneurysms occur throughout the length of the aorta with
more tendency towards the infrarenal segment.
• 30% of aneurysms occur in the infrarenal aorta.
• Abdominal aortic aneurysms are nine times more
common than thoracic aortic aneurysms.
• The incidence of aortic aneurysm increases by 6% for
each decade after the age of 65.
• Thoracoabdomianl aneurysms are less frequent than
isolated thoracic or abdominal aneurysms.

Iliac artery aneurysm:
• Common iliac artery aneurysm is the most common in the
presence of a concomitant infrarenal aortic aneurysm.
• Internal iliac aneurysm is the second most common in
this circulatory bed.
• Isolated internal iliac artery aneurysms present with
rupture in 40% of patients and 31% death rate.
• Bilateral common iliac artery aneurysms are present in
50% of patients.
• External iliac artery is rarely affected.

Femoral artery aneurysm:
• The incidence of true aneurysms is relatively low.
• True degenerative femoral artery aneurysms do not cause
complications unless they are greater than 3.5 cm or contain
thrombus.
• Aneurysms are bilateral.
• 88% of patients have a synchronous aneurysm at another site.
• Mostly occurs in common femoral artery and less commonly in the
profound femurs.

Popliteal artery aneurysm( PAA ) :
• Most common ( 70% ) of peripheral arterial aneurysms, although rare in the
general population.
• They are true aneurysms with the exception of occurrence in trauma.
• Occur in 1% of men aged 65 to 80 years ( exclusively in men).
• most common bilateral aneurysms, about 50% of patients with PAA have
bilateral aneurysms.
• 30 to 50% of patients have associated AAA.
• In patients treated for isolated PAA, there is a 50% likelihood of developing
another remote aneurysm over 10 years.

Popliteal artery aneurysm ( PAA ) :
• Clinical findings:
• More than half of patients are symptomatic at time of
presentation, although they may be found incidentally.
• Lower limb ischemia ( acute and chronic ) secondary to
thrombosis and embolization is the most common presentation.
• Less commonly patients Amy have compressive symptoms ( vein:
swelling, DVT ) or ( nerve compression ).
• Rarely patients may present with severe swelling and pain when
aneurysm ruptures.

Visceral artery aneurysm:
• Splenic artery aneurysms are second most common aneurysm in
the abdominal cavity and account for approximately 60% of all
visceral or splanchnic aneurysms.
• Splenic aneurysms occur in women and are associated with portal
hypertension.
• Pregnancy is a specific risk factor for rupture.
• Many visceral aneurysms can be managed with endovascular
techniques.
• Splenic artery aneurysms involving the hilum require splenectomy.

=====PAGE 34 [T]=====
Renal artery aneurysms:
• Renal aneurysms occur in 0.1% of the population.
• Saccular aneurysms often occur in association with
fibromuscular dysplasia.
• There is low resistance and high flow characteristics in the
renal circulatory bed, explaining location and frequency of
these aneurysms.
• Complications include: thrombotic and embolic events, poorly
controlled hypertension and rupture.

Renal artery aneurysm:
• Risk of rupture is elevated in third trimester of pregnancy
with maternal and fetal mortality rates of 70% and 100%
respectively.
• Indications for repair include: size more than 2-3 cm, lesions
in women of child bearing age, refractory hypertension,
flank pain, and hematuria.
• Endovascualr and open surgical techniques are used for
repair.

Cerebrovascular artery aneurysm:
• Intracranial aneurysms are common and represent a major
challenge for neurosurgeons and neuroradiologists.
• Extracranial cerebral arteries ( carotid arteries ) are rarely affected
by aneurysms.
• Dissection after trauma can led to internal carotid and vertebral
artery aneurysm.
