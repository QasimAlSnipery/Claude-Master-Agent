Acute Limb Ischemia (ALI)
Definition
Acute Limb Ischemia (ALI) refers to a sudden reduction in arterial perfusion of an
extremity due to occlusion of a native artery, bypass graft, or stent, resulting in a threat to
limb viability and requiring urgent evaluation and intervention. By definition, ALI is
considered when the duration of symptoms is less than two weeks; beyond this period,
the condition is classified as chronic limb-threatening ischemia (CLTI). ALI frequently
occurs in patients with significant comorbidities, emphasizing the importance of systemic
assessment alongside local limb evaluation. The incidence is approximately 1 per 6000
population / Year
Etiology
1) Arterial Embolism – typically of cardiac origin (e.g., atrial fibrillation, mural
thrombus, ventricular aneurysm).
2) Acute-on-Chronic Thrombosis – thrombosis superimposed on a pre-existing stenosis.
3) Thrombosis of Arterial Reconstructions – occlusion of previous bypass grafts or
stents.
4) Traumatic Arterial Injury – penetrating, blunt, or combined injuries (e.g., stab,
gunshot, or road traffic trauma).
5) Thrombosis of Peripheral Arterial Aneurysms – particularly popliteal aneurysm
thrombosis.
6) Acute Arterial Dissection – spontaneous or iatrogenic.
Pathophysiology
An embolus or thrombus causes abrupt interruption of blood flow, leading to tissue
hypoxia and metabolic acidosis. The sequence of ischemic injury progresses from
sensory nerve dysfunction to motor paralysis and finally muscle necrosis, with the
window for reversible ischemia generally being 6 hours.
Sources of Emboli
Cardiac Sources
• Atrial Fibrillation: Thrombus formation within the left atrial appendage due to stasis.
• Mural Thrombus: Following myocardial infarction or ventricular aneurysm.
• Prosthetic ( usually mechanical ) Heart Valves: Rare with appropriate
anticoagulation.

• Paradoxical Embolism: Passage of venous thrombus through a patent foramen ovale.
Non-Cardiac Sources
Atheroembolism: Dislodgement of atherosclerotic debris from the aortic arch or proximal
arteries, spontaneous or iatrogenic (catheter manipulation).
Risk factors:
1. Non coagulated cardiac arrythmias , atrial fibrillation
2. Valvular heart pathologies as Mitral regurgitation, mitral stenosis , aortic stenosis
3. Diabetes mellitus
4. Dyslipidemia
Mortality and Morbidity:
Emergency diagnosis is vey crucial as ALI carries very high rate of morbidity and
mortality if not diagnosed in proper time. Estimates of mortality after ALI is ranging
from 9-22%. Limb salvage following ALI is estimates at 70-90% if diagnosis and
managed in the golden hours of the first 6 hour. Amputations and life loss is more
frequent after thrombotic occlusion compared to embolic occlusion.
Clinical Presentation
Classic Features: “The Six Ps”
• Pain: sharp continuous even not responding to strong analgesics
• Pallor: Due to lack of well perfused oxygenated blood
• Pulselessness: No pulses could be detected by palpation
• Poikilothermia (coldness): refers to the inability of the affected limb to maintain its
normal temperature, resulting in the limb becoming cold and adopting the ambient
temperature.
• Paresthesia: due ischemic damage to the sensory nerve endings
• Paralysis: usually the last stage due complete motor nerve damage

Investigations
Diagnosis should not delay revascularization preferably within 6 hours of symptom onset.
Baseline investigations include CBC, renal function, coagulation profile, serum CK, and
electrolytes. Diagnosis of ALI is mostly clinical, but if time allowed , the following
investigation is of benefit:
• Duplex Ultrasound (DUS): Rapid bedside assessment to identify occlusion or
residual flow.
• Computed Tomography Angiography (CTA): Defines level and extent of
occlusion; helpful in surgical planning.
• Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA): Gold standard; allows diagnosis and
simultaneous intervention.
Management Principles
Initial and Supportive Measures
• Systemic Anticoagulation: Start unfractionated heparin( UFH) bolus 100 IU/kg
followed by infusion 1000 IU/hr. ; maintain aPTT 2-3× normal.
• Analgesia: Avoid intramuscular opioids; IV or patient-controlled analgesia preferred.
• Hydration and Oxygen supplement : Prevent renal failure and optimize perfusion.
• Monitor urine output and renal function due to risk of myoglobinuria.
Revascularization Strategies
• Endovascular Approaches
• Catheter-Directed Thrombolysis (CDT): Local tPA infusion for non-immediately
threatened limbs (Rutherford I-IIA).
• Mechanical Thrombectomy Devices: Disrupt and aspirate thrombus, often combined
with thrombolysis.

• Fogarty Catheter Embolectomy/Thrombectomy: Standard for embolic occlusion

Complications After Reperfusion
• Acute Compartment Syndrome
• Rhabdomyolysis with acute Hyperkalemia and metabolic Acidosis
• Myoglobinuria and Acute Kidney Injury and failure
ALI due to Arterial Trauma
Definition:
Arterial trauma refers to injury to an arterial vessel resulting in disruption of blood
flow, hemorrhage, or ischemia. It may occur in isolation or as part of complex
polytrauma.
Etiology:
1. Penetrating injuries – stab wounds, gunshot injuries.
2. Blunt trauma – road traffic accidents, crush injuries, fractures or dislocations
(especially around joints such as the knee or elbow).
3. Iatrogenic causes – during vascular or orthopedic interventions.
4. Blast or combined injuries – common in military and industrial accidents.
Pathophysiology:
Arterial wall damage can lead to:
• Hemorrhage (external or concealed).
• Thrombosis or occlusion, causing distal ischemia.
• Arteriovenous fistula formation.
• Pseudoaneurysm ( False aneurysm) formation.
• Distal embolization due to intimal disruption.
Clinical Features:
Divided into Hard and Soft Signs of vascular injury:
• Hard signs: presence of one hard sign is diagnostic for ALI due to arterial injury
o Pulsatile bleeding
o Absent distal pulses
o Expanding or pulsatile hematoma
o Audible bruit or palpable thrill

o Signs of distal ischemia (pain, pallor, paralysis, paresthesia, poikilothermia)
• Soft signs: presences of soft signs alone necessitate further investigations
o Diminished distal pulses
o Small, stable hematoma
o Associated fractures or dislocations
o Injury in the course of a major vessel
o Unexplained hypotension or hemorrhage history
Investigations:
• Duplex Ultrasound (DUS) – rapid bedside assessment.
• CT Angiography (CTA) – defines extent and location of injury.
• Conventional Angiography – diagnostic and therapeutic option in hybrid settings.
Management Principles:
1. Adherence to ATLS protocol – treat life-threatening injuries first.
2. Hemorrhage control – direct pressure, temporary vascular shunt, or tourniquet (if
necessary).
3. Definitive repair:
o Primary repair or end-to-end anastomosis.
o Interposition graft using vein or rarely prosthetic material.
o Bypass grafting if segmental loss.
4. Adjunctive measures:
o Fasciotomy for acute compartment syndrome prevention.
o Anticoagulation when appropriate.
Complications:
• Limb ischemia and gangrene that may needs Amputation
• Pseudoaneurysm or AV fistula
• Infection
• Compartment syndrome
• Late thrombosis or graft failure
Summary of Key Points
• ALI is a time-critical vascular emergency; limb salvage depends on intervention
within 6 hours.
• Rutherford classification guides urgency of intervention.
• Early systemic heparinization and specialist vascular management are essential.
• Endovascular and hybrid approaches are preferred in suitable candidates.
• Monitor closely for renal and metabolic complications after reperfusion.
• Arterial trauma is a surgical emergency requiring prompt recognition and repair to
prevent limb loss and life-threatening hemorrhage. Early diagnosis, rapid control of
bleeding, and timely revascularization are key to optimal outcomes.
