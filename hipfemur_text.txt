Dislocations Of The Hip
• Complete loss of contact between the articular surfaces
forming that joint.
Classified according to the direction of the femoral head
dislocation to:
• Posterior (commonest).
• Anterior.
• Central:
(fracture of acetabulum).
10/11/24 Areewan MS Saeed 3
=====PAGE 4 [T]=====
Posterior Dislocation
• Constitute 90% of traumatic dislocations.
• Mechanism of injury:
Road traffic accident when the victim thrown forward
striking the knee against the dashboard with the hip and
knee flexed forcing the head out of the acetabulum,
some time posterior wall fracture happen
(fracture dislocation).
10/11/24 Areewan MS Saeed 4
=====PAGE 5 [T]=====
Clinical Features
• Pain and inability to bear weight.
• The leg is shortened, adducted, internally rotated and
flexed at the hip joint.
• Be careful if associated with fracture femur (rule).
• Always examine for signs of sciatic nerve injury.

Radiological evaluation
■ Plain radiography:
• AP view of the pelvis is a must in all severely traumatized
patient.
1. The head is out, above the acetabulum and smaller than
the other head; the lesser trochanter is not visualized.
2. Search for posterior acetabular wall, anterior femoral
head and femoral neck fractures.

• CT scan: show the anatomy of concomitant fractures,

Thompson and Epstein Classification
■ Type I: Dislocation with no or small chip fracture.
■ Type II: With large posterior wall fracture.
■ Type III: With posterior wall comminution.
■ Type IV: With fracture acetabular column.
■ Type V: With fracture femoral head.

Treatment
• Urgent reduction under GA within
6houres:
(assistant stabilizes the pelvis, traction
applied, flexion of hip and knee to 90◦ then gradual
rotation till you here clunk of reduction.
• Check stability at flexion
and adduction.
• Check radiography is essential
to confirm reduction and
exclude fractures.
• Post reduction CT scan
[fractures, retained intraarticular
loose bony fragment]

=====PAGE 10 [T]=====
After Treatment
• Type I: Rest in traction till the acute pain
subside, non wt. bearing 6 weeks then partial
wt. bearing for 6 weeks.
• Type II: Open reduction and rigid internal
fixation of posterior wall.
• Type III: Traction for 6 weeks, bone graft.
• Type IV&V: closed reduction may lead to
automatic reduction of the fractures, if not open
reduction and internal fixation.
o Full weight bearing allowed only after 12 weeks.

Complications
Early:
1. Sciatic nerve injury(10-20%).
2. Vascular injury (superior gluteal artery).
3. Associated fractures( femoral shaft, acetabulum,
head, neck femur).
Late:
1. Avascular necroses:
Immediate reduction 10%.
Delayed reduction 40%.
2. Myositis ossificans.
3. Unreduced dislocation.
4. Osteoarthritis(cartilage,
loose body, necrosis).
10/11/24 Areewan MS Saeed 11

Hip Fractures
• Involves head, neck and subtrochanteric fractures.
• Femoral neck fractures involve the narrow neck
between the round head of the femur and the shaft.
• Neck fractures are classified into (vascularity,
treatment, complications):
1. Intra capsular.
2. Extra capsular.
10/11/24 Areewan MS Saeed 12
=====PAGE 13 [T]=====
Intra Capsular Fractures
• Fracture happen in the intracapsular part of the
neck.
• Common in females, seventh and eighth decade.
• Risk factors:
1. Osteoporoses and osteomalacia.
2. Diabetes mellitus.
3. Alcoholism.
4. Chronic debilitating diseases.
5. Muscle imbalance and fall.
10/11/24 Areewan MS Saeed 13
=====PAGE 14 [T]=====
Mechanisms of injury
1. Fall on the greater trochanter directly.
2. Catching the toe in the carpet
and twisting the hip to
external rotation.
• In young patients (FFH,RTA).
• Stress fractures in runners and
military personnel.

Clinical Features
• Fall followed by pain in the hip.
• The patient lies with the
limb externally rotated
and short.
• Young patients usually have associated severe
injuries of the lower limb( fracture femur).
• Unconscious, dementia, impacted fractures.

Fracture displacement is assessed by
the Garden Classification:
1.Garden I : incomplete fracture
of the neck (so-called abducted
or impacted, Valgus).
2.Garden II : complete without displacement.
10/11/24 Areewan MS Saeed 17
=====PAGE 18 [T]=====
3. Garden III: complete with partial
displacement; fragments are still
connected by posterior retinacular
attachment; there is malalignment
of the femoral trabeculae
(adducted, varus).
4. Garden IV : this is a complete femoral neck fracture
with full displacement; the proximal
fragment is free and lies correctly
in the acetabulum so that the trabeculae
appear normally aligned.

Treatment
Initial traction to relief pain till the definitive treatment.
Fractures at this level have a poor capacity for union due to
the following factors:
A) Interference with the blood supply to the proximal
fragment.
B) Difficulty in controlling the small proximal fragment.
C) The lack of organization of the fracture hematoma due to
the presence of the synovial fluid.
D) Lack of periostium.
Because of these facts there is no role of conservative
treatment.

Complications
• Mortality is 20% at 4 months.
• If above 80 years 50% fail to regain
independent walking.
1. General (DVT, PE, pneumonia, bed sores).
2. Avascular necroses of the femoral head.
3. Non union.
4. Osteoarthritis.

Conclusions
• Posterior hip dislocation ts the commonest
type.
• Early reduction decreases the chance of AVN.
• Femoral neck fractures are common elderly
fractures.
• Early proper surgical management improves
the overall outcome and decrease the rste of
complications.
