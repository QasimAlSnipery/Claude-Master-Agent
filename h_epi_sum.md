# Epidemiology of Communicable Diseases

## The Big Picture
A communicable disease = an infectious disease **transmitted person-to-person**. It needs three things — the **epidemiologic triad: agent, host, environment** — and a complete **chain** for the agent to spread. Master the triad, the **carrier** concept, **modes of transmission**, the **time periods** (latent vs incubation, infectious period), and the **types of epidemics**.

## Key Definitions
- **Infectious disease** — caused by an infective agent.
- **Communicable disease** — infectious + transmitted person-to-person.
- **Epidemiologic triad: agent + host + environment.**
- Describe a problem by **Person** (age, sex, race), **Place** (geography), **Time** (when rates rise).
- Infection forms: **subclinical** vs **clinical** (with signs/symptoms).

## What an Agent Needs to Establish
1. Multiply, 2. have a **susceptible host**, 3. **exit** that host, 4. **infect another host**.

## The Triad
- **Agent:** viruses, bacteria, protozoa, helminths, fungi (or their toxins).
- **Host factors:** defence mechanisms (skin, mucosa, inflammation), **immunity** (cellular/humoral, natural or vaccine), nutrition (malnutrition ↑ susceptibility, e.g. measles), fatigue, pregnancy (e.g. severe malaria), genetics, age, sex, immunosuppression (HIV, diabetes).
- **Environment:** mixing patterns, poverty, education, sanitation, climate.

## Reservoir & Carrier
- **Reservoir** — where the agent multiplies/survives: **human, animal, non-living (soil)**.
- **Human reservoir = active case OR carrier.**
- **Carrier** — infected but **no symptoms**, still transmits → **more dangerous** (e.g. **meningitis 90% carriers**; cholera carriers > symptomatic).
- **Types of carrier:** temporary (**incubatory, convalescent, post-convalescent**) and **chronic** (e.g. Salmonella).

## Modes of Transmission
1. **Contact** — direct (flu, STDs) or indirect (paper, pen/fomites).
2. **Inhalation** — droplets (direct) or airborne (indirect).
3. **Ingestion** — food & water.
4. **Skin penetration** — by vector, direct, or contaminated wound.
5. **Transplacental.**

## Time Periods (the key distinction)
**Of infection:**
- **Time of infection** — agent enters host.
- **Latent period** — infection until the person **can transmit** (measles 7–18 d).
- **Infectious/communicable period** — when the person **can transmit** (measles ~8 d).
  - Starts **at infection** (diphtheria, streptococcal), **before symptoms** (measles, hepatitis), or **after symptoms** (cholera).
- **Non-infectious period** — transmission ends (recovery/immune, recovery/susceptible, or death).

**Of disease:** time of infection → **incubation period** (infection to symptom onset) → symptomatic → asymptomatic.
- **Latent ≠ incubation:** latent = until infectious; incubation = until symptoms.
- **Infection ≠ disease** — asymptomatic infection occurs; proportion developing disease varies by agent.

## Types of Epidemics
- **Common source:**
  - **Point source** — single brief exposure (cases peak within one incubation period).
  - **Extended source** — prolonged exposure.
- **Propagated (progressive)** — person-to-person spread, successive waves one incubation period apart.

## Walk-Away Points
- **Triad = agent + host + environment**; describe by **person, place, time**.
- **Carrier** (asymptomatic transmitter) is most dangerous; reservoir = human/animal/non-living.
- Transmission: contact, inhalation, ingestion, skin penetration, transplacental.
- **Latent = until infectious; incubation = until symptoms**; infectious period timing varies (cholera after symptoms, measles before).
- Epidemics: **common source (point/extended) vs propagated**.
