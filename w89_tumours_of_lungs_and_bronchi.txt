What Is Lung tumor ?
Lung tumor is a type of neoplasm that starts in the lungs. Tumor is abnormal mass of tissues from uncontrolled cell growth which can be benign or malignant. Cancer starts when cells in the body begin to grow out of control. 
What is lung cancer?
Lung cancer is a disease caused by uncontrolled cell division in the lungs. The cells divide and make more copies of themselves as a part of their normal function. But sometimes, they get changes (mutations) that cause them to keep making more of themselves when they shouldn’t. Damaged cells dividing uncontrollably create masses, or tumors.

===SLIDE 6===
Lung cancer, also called lung carcinoma, is malignant tumor that originates in the tissues of the lungs. Lung cancer is caused by genetic damage to the DNA of cells in the airways, often caused by cigarette smoking or inhaling damaging chemicals. Damaged airway cells gain the ability to multiply unchecked, causing the growth of a tumor. 
Lung cancer or bronchogenic carcinoma refers to tumors originating in the lung parenchyma or within the bronchi. It is one of the leading causes of cancer-related deaths

- One of the top causes of cancer death in both sexes

Normal structure and function of the lungs
The lungs are 2 sponge-like organs in our chest. The right lung has 3 sections, called lobes. The left lung has 2 lobes. The left lung is smaller because the heart takes up more room on that side of the body.
When you breathe in, air enters through the mouth or nose and goes into the lungs through the trachea (windpipe). The trachea divides into tubes called bronchi, which enter the lungs and divide into smaller bronchi. These divide to form smaller branches called bronchioles. At the end of the bronchioles are tiny air sacs known as alveoli.
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 13===
The alveoli absorb oxygen into blood from the inhaled air and remove carbon dioxide from the blood when we exhale. Taking in oxygen and getting rid of carbon dioxide are our lungs’ main functions. Lung cancers typically start in the cells lining the bronchi and parts of the lung such as the bronchioles or alveoli.
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 14===
A thin lining layer called the pleura surrounds the lungs. The pleura protects the lungs and helps them slide back and forth against the chest wall as they expand and contract during breathing. Below the lungs, a thin, dome-shaped muscle called the diaphragm separates the chest from the abdomen. When you breathe, the diaphragm moves up and down, forcing air in and out of the lungs.

Normal Lung & Bronchial Anatomy 
Understanding normal structure helps to localize and interpret tumors.
Conducting airways: trachea → bronchi → bronchioles
- Respiratory portion: respiratory bronchioles, alveolar ducts, alveoli
- Bronchial lining: pseudostratified ciliated columnar epithelium
- Other components: smooth muscle, cartilage, vessels, lymphatics, pleura

===SLIDE 17===
Lungs
Location: Occupy the chest cavity, on either side of the heart.
Lobes: The right lung has three lobes (superior, middle, and inferior), while the left lung has two (superior and inferior). The left lung is smaller to accommodate the heart.
Protective layers: Enclosed by a membrane called the pleura, which reduces friction during breathing.
Support: The diaphragm, a muscle below the lungs, helps them expand and contract during respiration
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 18===
Bronchial tree
Trachea: The windpipe, which starts at the larynx and branches into the two primary bronchi at the carina.
Primary Bronchi: The trachea splits into a left and right main bronchus, with the right being wider, shorter, and more vertical.
Secondary and Tertiary Bronchi: The bronchi branch into smaller airways called lobar (secondary) bronchi, which then divide into segmental (tertiary) bronchi.
Bronchioles: The smallest airways, lined with simple cuboidal ciliated epithelium and smooth muscle, but without cartilage or submucosal glands.
Alveoli: Tiny air sacs at the end of the bronchioles where oxygen enters the blood and carbon dioxide leaves

Basic Definitions
- Primary lung tumor: arises from lung or bronchial tissue
- Metastatic tumor: secondary deposit from another primary site
- Central tumor: located in main bronchi / hilum
- Peripheral tumor: located in outer lung parenchyma
These locations influence symptoms, imaging appearance, and approach to biopsy.

===SLIDE 21===
Etiology
Smoking is the most common cause of lung cancer. It is estimated that 90% of lung cancer cases are attributable to smoking. The risk is highest in males who smoke. The risk of lung cancer secondary to passive smoking increases by 20 to 30%.  
The risk is further compounded with exposure to other carcinogens, such as asbestos.
 Other factors include Exposure to metals such as chromium, nickel, arsenic, and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons is also associated with lung cancer. 
Lung diseases like idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis increase the risk of lung cancer independent of smoking. 
Asbestos and radon are established risk factors for lung cancer as well. Asbestos exposure, particularly occupational exposure, increases the risk for lung cancer in a dose-dependent manner but varies according to the type of asbestos fiber.

===SLIDE 26===
Risk factors for lung cancer
Smoking(active and passive) any kind of tobacco products, including cigarettes, cigars or pipes is the biggest single risk factor. Experts estimate that 80% of lung cancer deaths are smoking-related.
Other risk factors include:
Being exposed to secondhand tobacco smoke.
Being exposed to harmful substances, like radon, asbestos, uranium, diesel exhaust, silica, coal products and toxins in the air.
Having previous radiation treatments to your chest (for instance, for breast cancer or lymphoma).
Having a family history of lung cancer

Smoking and Carcinogenesis
- Tobacco smoke contains multiple carcinogens
- Causes chronic inflammation and repeated epithelial injury
- Sequence in central bronchi:
  - Squamous metaplasia → dysplasia → carcinoma in situ → invasive carcinoma
- Promotes accumulation of mutations in tumor suppressor genes and oncogenes

Types of lung cancer:
There are 2 main types of lung cancer.
1- Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC)
2- Small cell lung cancer (SCLC).
1- Non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC)
About 80% to 85% of lung cancers are NSCLC. The main subtypes of NSCLC are:
adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and large cell carcinoma. These subtypes, which start from different types of lung cells, are grouped together as NSCLC because their treatment and prognoses are often similar.

Clinical Grouping: NSCLC vs SCLC
- Non-Small Cell Lung Carcinoma (NSCLC):
  - Adenocarcinoma
  - Squamous cell carcinoma
  - Large cell carcinoma
  - Generally slower course, more often surgically treated
- Small Cell Lung Carcinoma (SCLC):
  - Highly aggressive neuroendocrine carcinoma
  - Almost always in smokers

Adenocarcinoma: Adenocarcinomas start in the cells that would normally secrete substances such as mucus. This type of lung cancer occurs mainly in people who smoke or used to smoke, but it is also the most common type of lung cancer seen in people who don't smoke. It is more common in women than in men, and it is more likely to occur in younger people than other types of lung cancer. Adenocarcinoma is usually found in the outer parts of the lung and is more likely to be found before it has spread.

Cancers that spread to the lungs:
 Cancers that start in other organs (such as the breast, pancreas, kidney, or skin) can sometimes spread (metastasize) to the lungs, but these are not lung cancers. For example, cancer that starts in the breast and spreads to the lungs is still breast cancer, not lung cancer. Treatment for metastatic cancer to the lungs is based on where it started (the primary cancer site).

What are the symptoms of lung cancer?
Most lung cancer symptoms look similar to other, less serious illnesses. Many people don’t have symptoms until the disease is advanced, but some people have symptoms in the early stages. For those who do experience symptoms, it may only be one or a few of these:
A cough that doesn’t go away or gets worse over time.
Trouble breathing or shortness of breath (dyspnea).
Chest pain or discomfort.
Wheezing.
Coughing up blood (hemoptysis).
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 70===
Hoarseness.
Loss of appetite.
Unexplained weight loss.
Unexplained fatigue (tiredness).
Shoulder pain.
Swelling in the face, neck, arms or upper chest (superior vena cava syndrome).
Small pupil and drooping eyelid in one eye with little or no sweating on that side of face (Horner’s syndrome).
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 71===
What are the first signs of lung cancer?
A cough or pneumonia that keeps coming back after treatment can sometimes be an early sign of lung cancer (though it can also be a sign of less serious conditions). The most common signs of lung cancer include a persistent or worsening cough, shortness of breath, chest pain, hoarseness or unexplained weight loss.
Depending on where in your lungs cancer starts, some of these symptoms can happen early (in stages I or II) but often they don’t happen until cancer has progressed to later stages. That’s why it’s important to get screened for lung cancer if you’re at higher risk.
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 72===
How long can you have lung cancer without knowing?
Cancer can grow in your body for a long time — years — before you know it’s there. Lung cancer often doesn’t cause symptoms in early stages

How is lung cancer diagnosed?
Diagnosing lung cancer can be a multi-step process.
History taking
 physical exam (like listening to your heart and lungs). Since lung cancer symptoms are similar to many other, more common illnesses, 
blood tests 
a chest X-ray.
CT scan, and then a biopsy. Other tests include using a PET/CT scan to see if cancer has spread, and tests of cancerous tissue from a biopsy to help determine the best kind of treatment.

TNM Staging:
The TNM staging system for lung cancer classifies its spread using three components:
T (Tumor size/extent). 
N (Node involvement in lymph nodes). 
M (Metastasis to distant sites).
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 85===
TNM Staging: T Component
 Based on tumor size and local extent
 Includes:
  - Maximum diameter
  - Invasion of pleura, diaphragm, mediastinum
  - Involvement of main bronchus and distance from carina
- T status influences resectability and prognosis
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 86===
TNM Staging: N and M Components
 N (Nodes):
  - N0: no regional node metastasis
  - N1–N3: involvement of ipsilateral or contralateral nodes in specific locations
M (Metastasis):
  - M0: no distant metastasis
  - M1: distant organ metastasis or malignant pleural/pericardial effusion
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 87===
TNM Components Explained
T (Tumor): Describes the primary tumor's size and local invasion.
T1: Small tumors (≤3 cm), further divided (T1a, T1b, T1c) by size.
T2: Larger tumors (e.g., >3 cm but ≤5 cm), with subcategories (T2a, T2b).
T3: Tumors >5 to ≤7 cm, or invading nearby structures.
T4: Very large tumors (>7 cm) or invading major structures like the heart or diaphragm.
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 88===
N (Nodes): Indicates spread to nearby lymph nodes.
N0: No lymph node involvement.
N1: Cancer in ipsilateral (same side) peribronchial/hilar nodes (inside or near lung).
N2: Involvement of ipsilateral mediastinal/subcarinal nodes (more central).
N3: Spread to contralateral (opposite side) nodes or supraclavicular nodes.
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 89===
M (Metastasis): Shows if cancer has spread to distant parts of the body.
M0: No distant spread.
M1: Distant metastasis present, with subcategories:
M1a: Spread to other lung, pleural/pericardial nodules, or malignant pleural/pericardial effusion.
M1b: Single extrathoracic metastasis in one organ.
M1c: Multiple extrathoracic metastases in one or multiple organs
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 90===
Stage Grouping and Prognosis
- TNM combined into stages I–IV
- Early stages (I–II): better prognosis, often surgical candidates
- Stage III: locally advanced, combined modality therapy
- Stage IV: advanced systemic disease, mainly palliative systemic treatment
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 91===
General Approach to Management
Depends on:
  - Tumor type (NSCLC vs SCLC)
  - Stage (TNM)
  - Patient’s performance status and comorbidities
- Multidisciplinary team: pulmonologist, oncologist, thoracic surgeon, radiologist, pathologist
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 92===
Surgical Treatment
 Best option for localized NSCLC (early stages)
 Procedures:
  - Lobectomy (standard)
  - Segmentectomy or wedge resection in selected patients
  - Pneumonectomy for extensive disease
- Requires adequate cardiopulmonary reserve
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 93===
Radiotherapy and Chemotherapy
Radiotherapy:
  - Curative in some localized cases (e.g., stereotactic body radiotherapy)
  - Palliative for symptom control (bone pain, brain mets)
 Chemotherapy:
  - Platinum-based regimens common in NSCLC and SCLC
  - Often combined with radiotherapy in SCLC
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 94===
Prevention and Screening
- Most effective prevention: tobacco control
- Smoking cessation reduces risk over time
- Screening:
  - Low-dose CT for high-risk populations (heavy smokers)
  - Aims to detect early-stage cancers for curative treatment
Ahmed ibraheem alazzawi
===SLIDE 95===
Summary & Key Take-Home Messages
- Lung tumors are diverse, but adenocarcinoma, squamous cell carcinoma, and SCLC dominate
- Smoking remains the principal avoidable risk factor
- NSCLC: more often surgically treated, significant role for targeted and immune therapies
- SCLC: aggressive, early spread, mainly treated with chemo-radiation
- Accurate classification, staging, and molecular testing are crucial for optimal management
