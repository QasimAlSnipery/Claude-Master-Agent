Learning Objectives
After this session, students should be able to:
• Differentiate between dynamic (mechanical) and adynamic IO
• Explain the pathophysiology leading to bowel distension,
dehydration, and systemic effects.
• Recognize the cardinal clinical features of small versus large
bowel obstruction.
• Identify the clinical and radiological signs suggestive of
strangulation and closed-loop obstruction.
• Outline the supportive and definitive (surgical) management
principles for acute obstruction.

Obstruction by adhesions and bands
Adhesions
The common causes of adhesions:
• Acute inflammation: Sites of anastomoses,
reperitonealisation of raw areas, trauma, ischemia.
• Foreign material: Talc, starch, gauze, silk.
• Infection: Peritonitis, tuberculosis.
• Chronic inflammatory conditions Crohn’s disease.
• Radiation enteritis
=====PAGE 7 [T]=====
Prevention of adhesions
Factors that may limit adhesion formation include:
• Good surgical technique
• Washing of the peritoneal cavity with saline to remove clots
• Minimising contact with gauze
• Covering anastomoses and raw peritoneal surfaces

Pathophysiology of Mechanical Obstruction
I. Proximal and Distal Bowel Changes
• Proximal to obstruction: Bowel dilates. Initially, peristalsis
increases to overcome the blockage. If unrelieved, peristaltic
strength reduces, leading to flaccidity and paralysis.
• Distal to obstruction: Exhibits normal peristalsis and absorption
until it becomes empty and collapses.
=====PAGE 10 [T]=====
Pathophysiology of Mechanical Obstruction
II. Causes of Proximal Distension
• Gas: Significant overgrowth of aerobic and anaerobic organisms.
Following reabsorption of O₂ and CO₂,
• the majority of the gas is nitrogen (90%) and hydrogen sulphide.
• Fluid: Accumulation of various digestive juices
• saliva, gastric, bile, pancreatic secretion
• up to 2.5 L/24 hours total
• Accumulation occurs because absorption by the obstructed gut is
retarded.
=====PAGE 11 [T]=====
Pathophysiology of Mechanical Obstruction
III. Systemic Consequences
• Dehydration and Electrolyte Loss are critical due to:
• Reduced oral intake.
• Defective intestinal absorption.
• Losses from vomiting.
• Sequestration in the bowel lumen (third spacing).
• Transudation of fluid into the peritoneal cavity.
• Clinical effects:
• Oliguria,
• dry skin/tongue,
• rising blood urea, and haematocrit (secondary polycythaemia).
• Hypokalaemia is usually not a feature of simple mechanical
obstruction.
=====PAGE 12 [T]=====
Strangulation
• Definition: Immediate life-threatening complication where the
blood supply is compromised, leading to bowel ischemia and
infarction.
• Mechanisms of Compromise (Figure 78.2/Summary box 78.2):
• Direct pressure (Hernial orifices, Adhesions/Bands).
• Interrupted mesenteric blood flow (Volvulus, Intussusception).
• Increased intraluminal pressure (Closed-loop obstruction).
• Pathology: Venous return is compromised before arterial supply.
Impaired perfusion leads to hemorrhagic infarction, and viability
loss allows translocation and systemic exposure to anaerobic
organisms and endotoxin.

Distension: Principally confined to

• Clinical Significance: High risk of

• Classic Example: Malignant

=====PAGE 14 [T]=====
Intussusception and Volvulus
• Intussusception: One gut segment invaginates
into an adjacent segment.
• Most common in children (5–10 months).
• Adult cases are almost invariably secondary to a
pathological lead point (polyp, tumor).
• Ileocolic is the most common anatomical variety.
• Volvulus: Axial rotation/twisting of a portion of
bowel about its mesentery.
• Rotation causes luminal obstruction (>180°) and
vascular occlusion (>360°).
• The most common spontaneous type in adults is
sigmoid volvulus.
• Sigmoid volvulus may sometimes be relieved by
decompression per anum.

Clinical Features: The Cardinal Quartet
I. Dynamic Obstruction: The Classic Quartet
• Pain: Sudden, severe, and colicky. Usually centered on the umbilicus (small
bowel) or lower abdomen (large bowel).
• Colicky pain may be replaced by diffuse, constant pain with increasing distension.
• Vomiting:
• High SBO: Occurs early and is profuse, leading to rapid dehydration. Minimal
distension.
• Low SBO: Occurs later; may become faeculent due to bacterial overgrowth. Central
distension is pronounced.
• LBO: Vomiting is a later feature.
• Distension:
• Minimal or absent in high SBO.
• Early and pronounced in LBO.
• Visible peristalsis may be present in thin patients.
• Absolute Constipation: Neither faeces nor flatus is passed.
Cardinal feature of complete obstruction.
=====PAGE 16 [T]=====
Clinical Features
II. Exceptions to Absolute Constipation
• Richter’s hernia (only part of the bowel wall is incarcerated).
• Gallstone ileus.
• Partial or subacute obstruction (diarrhea may occur).
=====PAGE 17 [T]=====
Clinical Features
III. Clinical Features of Strangulation
• Requires immediate distinction from simple obstruction.
• Key signs:
• Pain becomes constant and severe (beware pain not controlled by
opiates).
• Tenderness with rigidity and peritonism. Localized tenderness is
significant.
• Shock (suggests underlying ischemia).
• Pyrexia may indicate ischemia or perforation.

Imaging Modalities
I. Plain Abdominal Radiograph
(Supine/Erect)
• SBO Characteristics: Straight, generally
central loops, lying transversely. Valvulae
conniventes (cross the entire width, giving a
‘ladder’ effect) are visible in the jejunum.
Minimal/no gas in the colon.
• LBO Characteristics: Haustral folds
(irregularly spaced, do not cross the whole
diameter). Large amount of gas in the
caecum.
• Erect Films: Demonstrate fluid levels;
pronounced levels suggest advanced
obstruction (Figure 78.13).
=====PAGE 19 [T]=====
Imaging Modalities
II. Computed Tomography (CT) Scanning
• Role: Highly accurate in investigating obstruction.
• Limitation: Diagnosing ischemia remains difficult.
• CT Signs Suggesting Strangulation:
• Reduced enhancement of the bowel wall.
• Absence of mesenteric fluid on CT decreases the probability of
strangulation.
• Crucially: Diagnosis of strangulation remains primarily clinical.
• Intussusception: The ‘target sign’ or ‘sausage-shaped’ mass is
pathognomonic
=====PAGE 20 [T]=====
Imaging Modalities
III. Contrast Studies
• Water-Soluble Contrast: Used in SBO without strangulation to
predict resolution. If contrast reaches the colon in 4–24 hours,
resolution is highly likely (96% sensitivity). If it fails to reach the
colon, surgery is required in approximately 90% of patients.
• Barium Follow-Through: Contraindicated in acute obstruction.
=====PAGE 21 [T]=====
Management: Supportive and Surgical
I. Supportive Management
• Resuscitation is mandatory before surgery unless strangulation
or closed-loop obstruction is present.
• Gastrointestinal Drainage: Via nasogastric tube. Essential for
decompression and reducing aspiration risk during anesthesia.
• Fluid & Electrolyte Replacement: Address sodium and water
loss using Hartmann’s solution or normal saline.
=====PAGE 22 [T]=====
Management: Supportive and Surgical
II. Indications for Early Surgical Intervention
• Obstructed external hernia.
• Clinical features of intestinal strangulation (constant pain,
peritonism).
• Obstruction in a previously unoperated abdomen.
• Note: Where adhesive obstruction is likely, conservative management
may continue up to 72 hours if no ischemia is present.
=====PAGE 23 [T]=====
Management: Supportive and Surgical
III. Principles of Surgical Intervention
Surgical goals include managing:
• The segment at the site of obstruction.
• The distended proximal bowel.
• The underlying cause of obstruction.
• Intraoperative Decompression: Milk contents retrogradely into a
large-bore orogastric tube for aspiration. Avoid injury to the distended,
oedematous bowel.
• Viability Assessment: Check for color, sheen, peristalsis, and
mesenteric vessel pulsation after wrapping the bowel in hot packs for
10 minutes. If uncertain, resection is required.

Summary
• Key Takeaways:
• Dynamic obstruction is marked by the quartet: Pain, Vomiting,
Distension, Absolute Constipation.
• Dehydration and shock result from fluid sequestration and vomiting.
• Strangulation (constant pain, peritonism, shock) is a surgical
emergency.
• CT is highly accurate, but the viability diagnosis is primarily clinical.
• Supportive care (NG tube, fluids) precedes surgery, but prompt
laparotomy is necessary for signs of compromise or closed-loop.
