# Arterial Aneurysms (Infrarenal & Peripheral)

## The Big Picture
An aneurysm = artery dilated to **≥1.5× normal**, involving **all 3 wall layers**. Commonest in the **infrarenal aorta**. Key exam themes: **true vs false**, **shape**, **size thresholds for rupture/repair**, and the named peripheral sites with their tell-tale percentages.

## Definitions to Nail
- **Aneurysm** — ≥1.5× normal, all 3 layers.
- **AAA** — infrarenal aorta **>3 cm** (or 50% bigger than proximal).
- **Ectasia** — enlarged but <50% over normal (intermediate).
- **Arteriomegaly** — diffuse enlargement of many segments >50%.
- **Aneurysmosis** — multiple aneurysms, several locations.
- **True** — all 3 layers. **False/pseudo** — wall is only connective tissue/contained haematoma, with a **neck**; usually iatrogenic (arterial access) or anastomotic breakdown.

## Shape (matters for rupture)
- **Fusiform** — uniform circumferential dilation (most degenerative/atherosclerotic).
- **Saccular** — focal, eccentric outpouching from ulcer/trauma/infection; **higher rupture risk → repair at smaller size**. Renal & cerebral aneurysms are saccular/eccentric.

## Causes
Degenerative (age), inflammatory, dissection, trauma, infection, congenital, **smoking**.

## Abdominal Aortic Aneurysm (AAA)
- **Where** — 30% infrarenal; **9× more common than thoracic**; incidence +6% per decade after 65.
- **Smoking = single biggest modifiable risk** (dose-dependent: 12× at >1 pack/day × 35 yrs).
- **Rupture risk** — stratified by diameter; jumps at **>5 cm**; saccular worse.
- **Presentation** — usually **asymptomatic**; if symptomatic, new diffuse abdominal/back pain ("different from usual"), pulsatile abdominal sensation/mass in thin patients. History of tobacco, HTN, atherosclerosis.
- **Imaging** — US (screen), **CTA (excellent)**, MRA; contrast angiography invasive/avoided.
- **Management** — no drug reliably slows growth; β-blockers (BP), antiplatelets (thrombosis). Repair: **open vs EVAR (endovascular)**.

## Peripheral & Visceral Sites (memorise the numbers)
- **Iliac** — common iliac commonest (with AAA); **internal iliac** ruptures in 40% / 31% death; bilateral common iliac in 50%; external iliac rarely.
- **Femoral** — true aneurysms rare; complications if **>3.5 cm** or thrombus; **88% have a synchronous aneurysm elsewhere**. **Infected pseudoaneurysm from IV drug abuse = most dangerous** → open surgery. Small <2.5 cm observe; pseudo can be treated with **US-guided thrombin injection / compression**.
- **Popliteal (PAA)** — **most common peripheral aneurysm (70%)**; almost exclusively **men** 65–80; **bilateral 50%**; **30–50% have AAA**. Presents with **limb ischaemia (thrombosis/embolism)**, rarely rupture. Prominent popliteal pulse + gangrene in non-diabetic man = think PAA. **Repair if symptomatic or >2 cm.**
- **Visceral** — **splenic = commonest visceral (~60%)**; women, portal hypertension; **pregnancy = rupture risk**; hilar lesions need splenectomy.
- **Renal** — 0.1%; saccular + **fibromuscular dysplasia**; rupture in **3rd-trimester pregnancy (maternal 70% / fetal 100% mortality)**; repair if >2–3 cm, childbearing women, refractory HTN.
- **Cerebral/UE** — intracranial common; carotid rare; subclavian from thoracic outlet repetitive trauma; brachial pseudoaneurysm iatrogenic.

## Walk-Away Points
- AAA >3 cm; **rupture risk climbs >5 cm**; smoking = top modifiable risk; CTA + EVAR/open.
- **Popliteal = 70% of peripheral**, male, bilateral 50%, presents as ischaemia.
- Femoral: **88% have another aneurysm**; infected IVDU pseudoaneurysm most dangerous.
- Splenic = commonest visceral; renal aneurysm + pregnancy = catastrophic rupture.
- Saccular ruptures earlier → repair at smaller diameter.
