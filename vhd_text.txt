The heart valves allow forward movement of blood through the cardiac
chambers when they are open & prevent backward flow when closed.
A diseased valve either narrowed, obstructing forward flow, or become
leaky, causing backward flow or regurgitation.
In developing countries, rheumatic heart disease (RHD) remains a common
cause of valvular heart disease (VHD).
In industrialized countries, the burden of RHD has significantly decreased, &
the most common etiology is degenerative disease.
Prevalence increases with age to as high as 13.3% in those 75yrs & older.
Moderate or severe VHD is associated with excess mortality.
SOB is a common symptom of valve disease, & acute severe SOB may be a
presenting symptom of valve failure.
Predisposition to valvular disease may be genetically determined , can arise
as the result of rheumatic fever or infections, or can occur in association with
dilatation of the cardiac chambers in heart failure.

Rheumatic heart disease
• Acute rheumatic fever( A.Rh.F)
Acute Rh.F usually affects children & young adults 5 - 15 years.
It is now rare in high-income countries in Western Europe & North
America, where the incidence is about 0.5/100 000, but remains
endemic in South Asia, Africa & South America.
Recent studies indicate that the current incidence of RHD in India
ranges between 13 & 150 cases /100 000population / year, where it is
the commonest cause of acquired HD in childhood & adolescence.
=====PAGE 9 [T]=====
Pathogenesis
The condition is triggered by an immune-mediated delayed response to
infection with specific strains of group A streptococci, which have Ag
that cross-react with cardiac myosin& sarcolemmal membrane protein.
Ab. produced against the strept. Ag cause inflammation in the
endocardium, myocardium & pericardium, as well as the joints & skin.
Histologically,fibrinoid degeneration is seen in the collagen of C. tissues.
Aschoff nodules are pathognomonic & occur only in the heart. They are
composed of multinucleated giant cells surrounded by macrophages &
T cells, & are not seen until the subacute or chronic phases of rheumatic
carditis.
=====PAGE 10 [T]=====
Clinical features
A. Rh.F is a multisystem dis. that usually presents with fever, anorexia,
lethargy &joint pain, 2–3 weeks after an episode of strept. pharyngitis
although there may be no history of sore throat.
Arthritis occurs in approximately 75% of patients. Other features
include rashes, S.C nodules, carditis & neurological changes .
Using the revised Jones criteria, the diagnosis is based on 2 or more
major criteria, or 1 major & 2 or more minor criteria, along with
evidence of preceding strept. Inf.
A presumptive diagnosis of A. Rh.F can be made without evidence of
preceding strept. Inf. in cases of isolated chorea or pancarditis , if other
causes have been excluded.
In cases of established RHD or prior Rh.F , A.Rh.F can be diagnosed
based only on the presence of multiple minor criteria & evidence of
preceding group A strep. pharyngitis.
=====PAGE 11 [T]=====
Carditis
RF cause a pancarditis involving endoc.,myoc.& peric. to varying degrees
Its incidence declines with increasing age, ranging from 90% at 3 yrs to
30% in adolescence.
It may manifest as dyspnea (due to HF or pericardial effusion),
palpitations or chest pain (usually due to pericarditis or pancarditis).
Other features include tachycardia, cardiac enlargement & new or
changed murmurs.
A soft systolic murmur due to MR is very common.
A soft mid-diastolic murmur ( Carey Coombs murmur) is typically due to
valvulitis, with nodules forming on the MVLs.
AR occurs in 50% of cases but the tricuspid &pul. valves rarely involved.
Pericarditis may cause chest pain, a pericardial friction rub & precordial
tenderness.
HF may be due to myocardial dysfunction or valvular regurgitation.
ECG evidence commonly includes ST & T wave changes. Conduction
defects, including AV block, sometimes occur & may cause syncope.
=====PAGE 12 [T]=====
Arthritis
This is the commonest major manifestation & occurs early when ASO
titres are high. An acute painful, asymmetric & migratory inflammation
of the large joints typically affects the knees, ankles, elbows & wrists.
The joints are involved in quick succession & are usually red, swollen &
tender for between a day & 4 weeks.
Skin lesions:
Erythema marginatum occurs in <5% of patients. The lesions start as
red macules that fade in the centre but remain red at the edges, &
occur mainly on the trunk & proximal extremities but not the face.
The resulting red rings or ‘margins’ may coalesce or overlap .
Subcutaneous nodules occur in 5%–7% of patients. They are small
(0.5–2.0 cm), firm & painless, & are best felt over extensor surfaces of
bone or tendons. They typically appear >3 weeks after the onset of
other manifestations & therefore help to confirm rather than make
diagnosis.
=====PAGE 13 [T]=====
Sydenham’s chorea
Also known as St Vitus dance, is a late neurological manifestation that
appears at least 3 months after the episode of A. Rh.F , when all the other
signs may have disappeared.
It occurs in up to 1/3 of cases and is more common in females.
Emotional lability may be the first feature & is typically followed by
purposeless, involuntary, choreiform movements of the hands, feet or face.
Speech may be explosive & halting.
Spontaneous recovery usually occurs within a few months. Approximately 1/4
of affected patients will go on to develop chronic rheumatic valve disease.
Other features
Other systemic manifestations, such as pleurisy, pl. effusion & pneumonia,
may occur but are rare.

Management
The aims of management are to limit cardiac damage & relieve symptoms
Bed rest
Is important,as it lessens joint pain & reduces cardiac work load. The duration
should be guided by symptoms, along with temp., WBC & ESR, & should be
continued until these have settled.
Patients can then return to normal physical activity but strenuous exercise
should be avoided in those who have had carditis .
Treatment of HF
Some patients, particularly those in early adolescence, can develop a fulminant
form of the disease with severe MR &, sometimes, concomitant AR.
If HF does not respond to medical treatment, valve replacement may be
necessary & is often associated with a dramatic decline in rheumatic activity.
Occasionally, AV block may occur but is seldom progressive & usually resolves
spontaneously. Rarely, pacemaker insertion may be required.
=====PAGE 22 [T]=====
Antibiotic therapy
A single dose of benzathine benzylpenicillin (1.2 million U IM) or oral
phenoxymethyl penicillin (250 mg 4 times daily for 10 days) should be
given on diagnosis to eliminate any residual strept. inf.
If patient is penicillin-allergic,erythromycin or cephalosporin can be
used
Patients are susceptible to further attacks of RF if another strept. inf.
occurs, & long-term prophylaxis with penicillin should be given with
oral phenoxy-MP (250 mg twice daily) or as benzathine benzylpenicillin
(1.2 million U IM monthly), if adherence is in doubt.
Sulfadiazine or erythromycin may be used if the patient is allergic to
penicillin; sulphonamides prevent inf. but are not effective in the
eradication of group A streptococci.
Further attacks of RF are unusual after the age of 21, when antibiotic
treatment can usually be stopped.
=====PAGE 23 [T]=====
The duration of prophylaxis should be extended if an attack has
occurred in the last 5 years, or if patient lives in an area of high
prevalence & has an occupation ( teaching) with a high risk of exposure
to strept. inf.
In those with residual heart disease, prophylaxis should continue until
10 years after the last episode or 40 years of age, whichever is later.
While long-term antibiotic prophylaxis prevents further attacks of
acute RF, it does not protect against infective endocarditis.

Aspirin
This usually relieves the symptoms of arthritis rapidly & a response
within 24 hours helps confirm the diagnosis.
A reasonable starting dose is 60 mg/kg /d, divided into 6 doses.
In adults, 100 mg/kg/ d may be needed up to the limits of tolerance or
a maximum of 8 g per day.
Mild toxicity includes nausea, tinnitus & deafness;
vomiting, tachypnoea & acidosis are more serious.
Aspirin should be continued until the ESR has fallen & then gradually
tailed off.

Chronic rheumatic heart disease
Chronic VHD develops in at least 50% of those affected by RF with cardi.
Two-thirds of cases occur in women.
Some episodes of RF pass unrecognised & it is possible to elicit a history
of RF or chorea in only about 1/2 of all patients with chronic RHD.
The MV is affected in >90% of cases; AV is the next most frequently
involved, followed by the tricuspid & then the pul. valve.
Isolated MS accounts for about 25% of all cases, & an additional 40%
have mixed MS & MR
=====PAGE 29 [T]=====
Pathogenesis
The main pathological process in chronic RHD is progressive fibrosis.
The HV are predominantly affected but involvement of the pericardium
& myocardium also occurs & may contribute to HF & conduction dis.
Fusion of the MV commissures & shortening of the chordae tendineae
may lead to MS with or without MR.
Similar changes in the AV& TV produce distortion and rigidity of the
cusps, leading to stenosis & regurgitation.
Once a valve has been damaged, the altered haemodynamic stresses
perpetuate & extend the damage, even in the absence of a continuing
rheumatic process.

Mitral valve disease
Mitral stenosis
MS is almost always rheumatic in origin, although in older people it can be
caused by heavy calcification of the MV.
Less common causes of MS include mitral annular calcification, radiation
exposure, congenital, or mechanical obstruction from an atrial myxoma,
vegetation, or thrombus.
Pathogenesis
In rheumatic MS, the MV orifice is slowly diminished by progressive fibrosis,
calcification of the valve leaflets, & fusion of the cusps & subvalvular apparatus.
The MV orifice is normally about 5 cm2 in diastole but can be reduced to < 1cm2
in severe MS.
The patient is usually asymptomatic until the orifice is < 2 cm2.
As MS progresses, LV filling becomes more dependent on LA contraction. There is
dilatation & hypertrophy of the LA & LA pressure rises, leading to pul. venous
congestion & SOB.
Any increase in HR shortens diastole when the MV is open & produces a further
rise in LA pressure.
Situations that demand an increase in CO , such as pregnancy & exercise, also
increase LA pressure & are poorly tolerated.
=====PAGE 31 [T]=====
AF is very common due to progressive dilatation of the LA.
Its onset often precipitates pul. edema because the accompanying
tachycardia & loss of atrial contraction lead to haemodynamic
deterioration & a rapid rise in LA pressure.
In the absence of AF, a more gradual rise in LA pressure may occur.
Irrespective of AF, PHT may occur, which can protect the patient from
pul. edema.
PHT leads to RVH & RV dilatation, TR & right HF.
<20% of patients remain in sinus rhythm but many of these have a
small fibrotic LA & severe PHT.
=====PAGE 32 [T]=====
• Clinical features
Effort-related dyspnoea is usually the dominant symptom .
Typically, exercise tolerance diminishes very slowly over many years
until symptoms eventually occur at rest.
Patients frequently do not appreciate the extent of their disability until
the diagnosis is made & their valve disease is treated.
Acute pul. edema or PHT can lead to haemoptysis.
Fatigue is a common symptom due to a low CO .
Thromboembolism is a common complication, especially in patients
with AF. Prior to the advent of anticoagulant therapy, emboli caused 1/4
of all deaths.
=====PAGE 33 [T]=====
The physical signs of MS are often found before symptoms develop &
their recognition is of particular importance in pregnancy.
The forces that open & close the MV increase as LA pressure rises.
S1 is therefore loud & can be palpable (tapping apex beat).
An opening snap (OS)may be audible & moves closer to the S2 as the MS
becomes more severe & LA pressure rises.
However, the S1 & OS may be inaudible if the valve is heavily calcified.
Turbulent flow produces the characteristic low-pitched mid-diastolic
murmur & sometimes a thrill . The murmur is accentuated by exercise &
during atrial systole (pre-systolic accentuation).
Early in the disease a pre-systolic murmur may be the only auscultatory
abnormality, but in patients with symptoms, the murmur extends from
the opening snap to the S1.
Coexisting MR causes a pansystolic murmur that radiates towards axilla.
PHT may ultimately lead to RVH & RV dilatation with secondary TR,
which causes a parasternal lift, & a systolic murmur & giant ‘v waves’ in
the venous pulse.

Management
Patients with mild symptoms can be treated medically but intervention
by catheter balloon valvuloplasty, M. valvotomy or MVR should be
considered if the patient remains symptomatic despite med. treatment
or if PHT develops.
Medical management
This consists of anticoagulation to reduce the risk of systemic emboism,
ventricular rate control with digoxin, β-blockers or rate-limiting calcium
antagonists for AF, & diuretic to control pul. congestion.
Direct oral anticoagulants e.g Rivaroxaban have not been approved for
this indication.
Antibiotic prophylaxis against infective endocarditis is no longer
routinely recommended.
=====PAGE 39 [T]=====
Mitral balloon valvuloplasty & MVR
Valvuloplasty is the treatment of choice if specific criteria are fulfilled
,although surgical closed or open mitral valvotomy is an acceptable
alternative.
Patients who have undergone mitral valvuloplasty or valvotomy should
be followed up at 1–2-yearly intervals because restenosis may occur.
Clinical sym. & signs are a guide to the severity of MS but Doppler echo.
provides a more accurate assessment.
MVR is indicated if there is substantial MR or if valve is rigid & calcified.

Pathogenesis
Chronic MR causes gradual dilatation of the LA with little increase in
pressure & relatively few symptoms. Nevertheless, the LV dilates slowly
& the LV diastolic & LA pressure gradually increase as a result of chronic
volume overload of the LV.
In contrast, acute MR causes a rapid rise in LA pressure (because LA
compliance is normal) & marked symptomatic
deterioration.

Mitral valve prolapse (MVP)
This is also known as Floppy’ MV & is a common cause of mild MR .
Some cases are thought to be due to a developmental abnormality of
the MV & others by degenerative myxomatous change in a normal MV .
Rarely, MVP may occur in association with Marfan syndrome.
In its mildest forms, the valve remains competent but bulges back into
the LA during systole, causing a mid-systolic click but no murmur.
In the presence of a MR , the click is followed by a late systolic murmur,
which lengthens as the MR becomes more severe.
A click is not always audible .
Progressive elongation of the chordae tendineae leads to increasing MR ,
& if chordal rupture occurs, MR suddenly becomes severe. This is rare
th
before the 5th or 6 decade of life.
MVP is associated with a variety of typically benign arrhythmias,
atypical chest pain & a very small risk of embolic stroke or TIA.
Nevertheless, the overall long-term prognosis is good.

Management
MR of moderate severity can be treated medically with diuretics
&vasodilator.
Digoxin & anticoagulants should be given if AF is present
If systemic HTN is present, it should be treated withsuch as ACE
inhibitors or ARBs, since high afterload may worsen the degree of MR.
All patients should be reviewed at regular intervals, both clinically & by
Echo.
Worsening symptoms,progressive cardiomegaly or echo.evidence of
deteriorating LV function are indications for MVR or repair.
MV repair is now the treatment of choice for severe MR, because early
repair appears to prevent irreversible LV damage.
MR often accompanies LVF associated with CAD. If such patients are to
undergo CABG surgery, it is common practice to repair the MV &restore
MV function by inserting an annuloplasty ring to overcome annular
dilatation & to bring the VL closer together.

Aortic valve disease
• Aortic stenosis(AS)
AS is defined by restriction in leaflet motion resulting in LVOT obstruction.
Less common causes of LVOT obstruction include lesions at the supravalvular
or subvalvular level.
There are several causes of AS but the age at which patients present can give
a clue to the most likely diagnosis.
In congenital AS , obstruction is present from birth or becomes apparent
during infancy.
With bicuspid AV,obstruction may take years to develop as the valve
becomes fibrotic & calcifed, & these patients present as young to
middle-aged adults.
Rheumatic AV dis. presents at similar age but usually accompanied by MV dis.
In older people, tricuspid AV may become stenotic as the result of fibrosis &
calcification.
Stenosis develops slowly, typically occurring at 30–60 yrs in those with
rheumatic disease, 50–60 yrs in those with bicuspid AV & 70–90 yrs in those
with calcific AV dis.
=====PAGE 54 [EMPTY]=====
=====PAGE 55 [T]=====
Pathogenesis
CO is initially maintained in patients with AS at the cost of a steadily
increasing PG across the AV .
With progression of the AS, the LV becomes hypertrophied & coronary
blood flow may be inadequate to supply the myocardium, such that
angina can develop even in the absence of coexisting CAD.
The fixed outflow obstruction limits the increase in CO required on
exercise.
Eventually, the LV can no longer overcome the LVOT obstruction & LV
failure results, leading to pul. edema.
=====PAGE 56 [T]=====
Clinical features
AS is commonly picked up in asymptomatic patients at routine clinical
examination .
3 cardinal symptoms are angina,SOB & syncope.
Angina arises either because of the increased demands of LVH working
against the high-pressure LVOT obstruction, or the presence of
coexisting CAD, which affects over 50% of patients.
Exertional SOB suggests cardiac decompensation as a consequence of
the excessive pressure overload placed on the LV.
Syncope usually occurs on exertion when CO fails to rise to meet
demand , leading to a fall in BP.
Sometimes patients with severe AS do not complain of symptoms.
If, on clinical evaluation, this appears to be due to a sedentary lifestyle,
a careful exercise test may reveal symptoms on modest exertion.
No physical examination findings can reliably exclude severe AS.
=====PAGE 57 [T]=====
A harsh ejection systolic murmur radiates to the neck, with a soft S2 ,
particularly in those with calcific valves. S4 can be felt in the setting of a
noncompliant LV.
The murmur is often likened to a saw cutting wood & may (especially
in older patients) have a musical quality like the ‘mew’ of a seagull .
Gallavardin’s phenomenon (murmur radiate to apex)
Precordial thrill may be palpable.
When palpating the carotid artery, a delayed, low amplitude pulse may
be appreciated (pulsus parvus et tardus).
=====PAGE 58 [T]=====
The severity of AS may be difficult to gauge clinically, as older
patients with a non-compliant ‘stiff’ arterial system may have an
apparently normal carotid upstroke in the presence of severe AS.
Milder AS may be difficult to distinguish from aortic sclerosis, in
which the valve is thickened or calcified but not obstructed.
A careful examination should be made for other valve lesions,
particularly in RHD , when there is frequently concomitant MV dis.
In contrast to patients with MS , which tends to progress very slowly,
patients with AS typically remain asymptomatic for many years but
deteriorate rapidly when symptoms develop; if otherwise untreated,
they usually die within 3–5 yrs of presentation.

Investigations
Echo. is a pivotal investigation in patients suspected of having AS.
It can demonstrate restricted AV opening & Doppler assessment permits
calculation of the systolic gradient across the AS, from which the severity of
AS can be assessed.
In patients with impaired LV function, velocities across the AV may be
diminished because of a reduced SV ; this is called low-flow AS .
When marked AR or elevated CO is present, velocities are increased because
of an increased SV & this may overestimate AS severity on Doppler echo.
In advanced cases, ECG features of LVH are often pronounced ,& down
sloping ST segments & T inversion (‘strain pattern’) seen in lateral leads( LV
fibrosis).
Nevertheless, the ECG can be normal, despite severe AS.
Occasionally, there is evidence of AV block due to the encroachment of the
fibrocalcific process on the adjacent AV node & His–Purkinje system; an
occasional cause of syncope in these patients.
Imaging with CT may be useful in assessing the degree of valve calcification
where there is uncertainty of disease severity.

Management
Irrespective of the severity of AS, patients with asymptomatic AS have a
good immediate prognosis & conservative management is appropriate.
Development of angina, syncope, symptoms of low CO or HF has a
poor prognosis & is an indication for prompt surgery.
The management of asymptomatic AS involves close monitoring, early
detection of symptoms, & treatment of CV risk factors & comorbidities
such as HTN, hyperlipidemia, & CAD .
No treatments have been shown to prevent the progression of AS.
Patients with moderate or severe AS should be evaluated every 1–2 yrs
with Doppler echo. to detect evidence of progression in severity.
More frequent monitoring (typically 3–6-monthly) in older patients with
heavily calcified valves .
Those with severe asymptomatic AS may also meet indications for AVR
if they have concurrent LV systolic dysfunction, very severe AS, rapidly
progressing AS, or if they are undergoing another cardiac surgery.
=====PAGE 66 [T]=====
Patients with symptomatic severe AS should have prompt AVR .
Delay exposes the patient to the risk of sudden death or irreversible
deterioration in LV function.
Old age is not a contraindication to AVR & results are very good in
th
experienced centres, even for those in their 80 . This is especially the
case with transcatheter aortic valve implantation (TAVI ).
Aortic balloon valvuloplasty is useful in congenital AS but has limited
value in older patients with calcific AS.
Anticoagulants are required only in patients who have AF or those
who have had a AVR with a mechanical prosthesis.
=====PAGE 67 [T]=====
Rather than an open procedure requiring sternotomy, TAVI most commonly
involves accessing the femoral artery & using a catheter to deliver a
bioprosthetic valve into position by expanding a balloon & effectively
crushing the native aortic valve against the aortic wall .
Less common approaches include transapical, transaortic, & subclavian.
The role of TAVI was initially established in patients with severe symptomatic
AS & prohibitive surgical risk. TAVI led to significant mortality benefit in this
patient population when compared to standard therapy.
Since the landmark trial in 2010, TAVI has emerged as an effective therapy
for severe symptomatic AS (from extreme risk to low risk).
Patients being considered for AVR should undergo a thorough individualized
evaluation by a multidisciplinary heart valve team.
The patient’s life expectancy, surgical risk, comorbidities, frailty, anatomy,
quality of life, values, & preferences should all be considered .

Aortic regurgitation
This condition can result from either disease of the AV cusps,
infection, trauma or dilatation of the aortic root.
In developing countries, chronic AR is usually due to RHD .
In developed countries, aortic root dilation, calcific degeneration, &
bicuspid AV are the most common causes.
=====PAGE 73 [T]=====
Pathogenesis
Regurgitation of blood through the AV causes the LV to dilate as CO
increases to maintain the demands of the circulation.
The SV of the LV may eventually be doubled & the major arteries are
then conspicuously pulsatile. As the disease progresses, LVF develops,
leading to a rise in LVED pressure & pul. edema.
=====PAGE 74 [T]=====
Clinical features
Until the onset of SOB , the only symptom may be palpitation,
particularly when lying on the left side, which results from the increased
SV.
st
PND is sometimes the 1 symptom, & peripheral edema or angina may
occur. The characteristic murmur is best heard to the left of the
sternum during held expiration ; a thrill is rare.
A systolic murmur due to the increased SV is common & does not
necessarily indicate AS .
The regurgitant jet causes fluttering of the MV &, if severe, causes
partial closure of the Ant.MVL, leading to functional MS & a soft
mid-diastolic(Austin Flint) murmur.
=====PAGE 75 [T]=====
Acute severe AR may occur as the result of perforation or tear of an
aortic cusp in endocarditis. In this circumstance there may be no time
for compensatory LVH & LV dilatation to develop & the features of HF
may predominate.
The classical signs of AR in such patients may be masked by tachycardia
& an abrupt rise in LVED pressure.
The pulse pressure may also be normal or near-normal & the diastolic
murmur may be short or even absent.

Management
Treatment may be required for underlying condition,e.g IE or syphilis.
AVR is indicated if AR causes symptoms,&this may need to be combined
with aortic root replacement & CABG.
Those with chronic AR can remain asymptomatic for many years
because compensatory LV dilatation & LVH occur, but should be advised
to report the development of any symptoms of SOB or angina.
Asymptomatic patients should also be followed up annually with echo.
for evidence of increasing LV size. If this occurs or if the EDD increases
to 55 mm or more, then AVR should be undertaken.
If systemic HTN is present, non-rate-limiting vasodilators, such as
nifedipine, should be used to control SBP. There is conflicting evidence
regarding the need for AVR in asymptomatic patients with severe AR.
When aortic root dilatation is the cause of AR, as can occur in Marfan
syndrome., aortic root replacement is usually necessary.

TRICUSPID STENOSIS
In TS there is restriction of blood flow between the RA & RV.
The etiology of TS is most commonly rheumatic & is generally associated
with MS.
Isolated TS is rare but can be seen in congenital TV atresia, right heart
tumors, carcinoid syndrome, & endocarditis.
Most patients with rheumatic TS have concomitant significant AV &/or
MV disease. TV atresia is managed with multiple surgeries starting in the
neonatal period into early childhood.
Patients present with signs & symptoms of systemic venous congestion
including ascites, peripheral edema, & hepatomegaly. Patients
may report a fluttering sensation in the neck from prominent a waves.
=====PAGE 82 [T]=====
With an increase in RAP there is JV distension. A prominent a wave can
often be appreciated. A rise in JVP with inspiration (Kussmaul sign) may
also be seen. Other signs of systemic venous congestion can be present
including hepatomegaly, ascites, peripheral edema, & anasarca.
The murmur of TS is described as a low-frequency, diastolic murmur
best heard at the left lower sternal border.
Diagnosis
TS can be diagnosed using TTE. In rheumatic TS, as seen in MS, the
leaflets are restricted, thickened,&calcified. TTE is also used to assess
for concomitant valve disease & to estimate the RA size & pressure.
Using Doppler, the diastolic PG can be measured across the TV & the TV
area can be estimated. A valve area of 1.0 cm2 or less is considered to
be severe TS.
=====PAGE 83 [T]=====
Treatment
There are limited data to guide treatment in TS.
Options include medical therapy such as diuretics to help with systemic
venous congestion, surgical intervention, or percutaneous balloon
valvotomy.
The decision for surgical versus percutaneous approach should be
individualized & based on valve anatomy, surgical risk, & operator
experience.
A surgical approach is typically reserved for symptomatic patients with
severe TS or asymptomatic patients with severe TS requiring cardiac
surgery for another indication.

TR is common, & is most frequently functional, occurring as a result of

Clinical features
Symptoms are usually non-specific, with tiredness related to reduced
CO , & edema & hepatic enlargement due to venous congestion.
The most prominent sign is a ‘giant’ v wave in the JV pulse (a cv wave
replaces the normal x descent).
Other features include a pansystolic murmur at the left sternal border &
a pulsatile liver.
Echo. may reveal dilatation of the RV.
If the valve has been affected by rheumatic disease, the leaflets will
appear thickened &, in endocarditis, vegetations may be seen.

Management
TR due to RV dilatation often improves when the HF is treated.
Patients with a normal PA pressure tolerate isolated TR well, & valves
damaged by endocarditis do not usually need to be replaced.
Patients undergoing MVR, who have TR due to marked dilatation of the
tricuspid annulus, benefit from valve repair with an annuloplasty ring
to bring the leaflets closer together. Those with rheumatic damage may
require TRV.

Pulmonary valve disease
Pulmonary stenosis(PS)
This can occur in the carcinoid syndrome but almost always congenital
& usually occurs as an isolated lesion,or associated with abnormalities,
such us TOF, congenital rubella syndrome, & Noonan syndrome.
Clinical features
Isolated PS is generally well tolerated & survival is comparable to the
general population.
Patients with mild PS are asymptomatic & may not be diagnosed with
PS until adulthood.
Moderate PS is usually identified in childhood & patients are usually
symptomatic due to RV pressure overload. Decreasing right-sided CO
leads to symptoms of dyspnea on exertion & fatigue.
In more advanced PS, patients can have RV failure & cyanosis.

Investigations
Doppler echo. is the definitive investigation.
ECG may show evidence of RVH,
CXR may show post-stenotic dilatation in the PA
MRI (CMR) can be considered as an alternative imaging modality to

Management
Mild to moderate isolated PS is relatively common & does not usually
progress or require treatment.
Severe PS (resting gradient > 50 mmHg with a normal CO) can be
treated by percutaneous pulmonary balloon valvuloplasty or, if this is
not available, by surgical valvotomy. Long-term results are very good.
Postoperative PR is common but benign.
A surgical approach is usually recommended for dysplastic valves, in the
presence of severe PR, or if there is another indication for surgery.

Pulmonary regurgitation
This is rare in isolation & is usually associated with PA dilatation due to
PHT. It may complicate MS, producing an early diastolic decrescendo
murmur at the left sternal border that is difficult to distinguish from AR
(Graham Steell murmur).
The PHT may be secondary to other disease of the left side of the heart,
primary pulmonary vascular disease or Eisenmenger syndrome.
Trivial PR is a frequent finding in normal individuals & has no clinical
significance.

Prosthetic valves
Diseased heart valves can be replaced with mechanical or biological
prostheses.
The 3 main types of mechanical prosthesis are the ball & cage, tilting
single disc & tilting bi-leaflet valves. All generate prosthetic sounds or
clicks on auscultation.
Pig or allograft valves mounted on a supporting stent are the most
commonly used biological valves. They generate normal HS.
All prosthetic valves used in the aortic position produce a systolic flow
murmur.
All mechanical valves require long-term anticoagulation because they
can cause systemic thromboembolism or may develop valve thrombosis
or obstruction ; the prosthetic clicks may become inaudible if the valve
malfunctions.
=====PAGE 92 [T]=====
Biological valves have the advantage of not requiring anticoagulants to
maintain proper function although many patients undergoing valve
replacement surgery, especially MVR , will have AF that requires
anticoagulation anyway. Biological valves are less durable than
mechanical valves and may degenerate 7 or more years after
implantation, particularly when used in the mitral position. They are
more durable in the aortic position & in older patients, so are
particularly appropriate for patients over 65 undergoing AVR.
=====PAGE 93 [T]=====
Transcatheter aortic valve implantation (TAVI)
For patients being considered for AV surgery, especially due to AS , TAVI
is an alternative to surgical AVR.
The native valve is not removed but is compressed by the new
bioprosthetic valve, which is implanted within it.
The bioprosthetic valve is mounted on a large stent-like structure & is
implanted through a catheter inserted in the femoral artery .
TAVI has several major advantages. It avoids the need for a
sternotomy, is associated with a short recovery period, can be used in
high-risk & otherwise inoperable patients, & is much better tolerated by
older patients.
Complications include stroke (2%) & HB necessitating pacemaker
implantation (5%–15%).

Prosthetic valve dysfunction
Symptoms or signs of unexplained HF in a patient with a prosthetic heart
valve may be due to valve dysfunction, & urgent assessment is required.
Metallic valves can suffer strut fracture & fail, causing catastrophic
regurgitation. Alternatively, they may thrombose & cause systemic
thromboembolism or valve obstruction, especially in the presence of
inadequate anticoagulation.
Biological valve dysfunction is usually associated with the development
of a regurgitant murmur & may begin to develop 8–10 years after
implantation.
