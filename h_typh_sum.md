# Typhoid Fever

## The Big Picture
Typhoid = systemic febrile infection by **Salmonella typhi** (less commonly paratyphi). **Humans are the only reservoir** — spread by faecal-oral contaminated food/water. Hallmarks: **step-ladder fever, relative bradycardia, rose spots, splenomegaly**; the dreaded complication is **intestinal perforation/haemorrhage**. Diagnose by **blood culture (early) / stool (late)**; treat with **ciprofloxacin**.

## Cause & Spread
- **Salmonella typhi** — Gram-negative bacillus (Enterobacteriaceae); antigens **H (flagellar), O (somatic), Vi (virulence capsule)**.
- **Only human reservoir** (no animal vector). Spread: contaminated **food/water**, hand-to-mouth, sewage, food handled by a **chronic carrier**.
- Survives drying/refrigeration; killed by pasteurisation/cooking/irradiation.

## Pathogenesis
Ingestion → invade small intestine → bloodstream → carried by WBCs to **liver, spleen, bone marrow** → multiply → re-enter blood → invade **gallbladder + Peyer's patches** → shed in stool. (Blood culture positive within 1st week; stool positive later.)

## Epidemiology
Worldwide in poor-sanitation developing nations (Asia, Africa, Latin America). **~9.2 million cases, 110,000 deaths (2019).** Children at greatest risk.

## Clinical Course (untreated = 4 weeks)
- **Incubation 6–30 days**, insidious onset.
- **Week 1 (bacteraemia):** rising **step-ladder fever** (up to 39–40°C, lowest AM, peaks PM), headache, malaise, cough, abdominal pain, constipation OR diarrhoea.
- **Week 2–3 (fastigium):** fever plateau, very toxic, weight loss, **"pea-soup" diarrhoea**, abdominal distension; **typhoid state** (apathy, confusion, psychosis); **Peyer's patch necrosis → perforation/peritonitis**.
- **Week 4 (defervescence):** slow improvement if survives.
- **Week 5 (convalescence):** symptoms gone but **can relapse**; some become **carriers**.

## Signs
- **Relative bradycardia** (fever without matching tachycardia).
- **Splenomegaly + hepatomegaly.**
- **Rose spots** — faint pink maculopapular, 2–4 mm, on trunk, fade on pressure, gone in 2–3 days.

## Complications
- **Intestinal haemorrhage / perforation = most serious** (week 2–3; serious bleed 2–8%; clue: sudden temperature drop + rising pulse + shock + blood in stool).
- Also myocarditis, pneumonia, meningitis, osteomyelitis.

## Diagnosis
- **Blood culture** — positive **90% week 1**, declining to 25% by week 4.
- **Bone marrow culture** — most sensitive (even after antibiotics).
- **Stool/urine culture** — better weeks 3–4. **Duodenal string test** — best for carriers (bile).
- **Widal test** — detects O & H antibodies; rises week 2–3; **neither sensitive nor specific** (needs paired samples).

## Treatment
- **Ciprofloxacin 15 mg/kg/day × 7 days.** Quinolone-resistant → **azithromycin** or **ceftriaxone**.
- **Dexamethasone** in severe (obtundation/meningism) → cuts mortality 80–90%.
- General: isolation, rest, fluids, antipyretics, easy diet.
- **Carrier** (positive stool >1 yr): co-trimoxazole 6 wk or ciprofloxacin 4 wk; **exclude from food handling until 3 negative stool cultures** (≥1 month apart); Vi antibody screens carriers.

## Prevention & Vaccines
- Hand hygiene, safe water, hot food, avoid raw produce.
- **3 vaccines:** **Ty21a** (oral live, 4 doses, booster 5 yr — contraindicated in pregnancy/immunocompromised, space 72 h from antibiotics); **Typhim-Vi** (polysaccharide, 1 IM, booster 2 yr); **TCV** (conjugate — usable <2 yr). Protection 50–80%.
- High-risk: water/food workers, lab staff, prisoners.

## Walk-Away Points
- **S. typhi, human-only reservoir**, faecal-oral; **step-ladder fever + relative bradycardia + rose spots + splenomegaly**.
- **Perforation/haemorrhage = most serious** (week 2–3).
- **Blood culture early, stool late**; Widal is weak; treat with **ciprofloxacin**.
- Carriers harbour it in gallbladder → 3 negative stools before food handling.
