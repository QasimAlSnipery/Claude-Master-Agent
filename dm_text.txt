Blood glucose homeostasis
• Blood glucose is tightly regulated and maintained within a narrow
range .This is essential for ensuring a continuous supply of glucose to
the central nervous system. The brain has little capacity to store
energy in the form of glycogen or triglyceride,and the blood-brain
barrier is largely impermeable to fatty acids,so the brain depends on
the liver for a constant supply of glucose for oxidation and hence
generation of ATP.
• Glucose homeostasis is achieved through the coordination of actions
of multiple organs,but mainly reflects a balance between the entry of
glucose into the circulation from the liver.
=====PAGE 4 [T]=====
After ingestion of a meal containing carbohydrate, normal blood glucose
levels are maintained by:
• Suppression of hepatic glucose production
• Stimulation of hepatic glucose uptake
• Stimulation of glucose uptake by peripheral tissues
In the post-prandial state portal vein insulin &glucose rise and portal
glucagon(secreted by α cells of pancreatic islets) concentration fall.
In response to a rise in blood glucose,e.g after a meal, Insulin is released
,suppressing gluconeogenesis (formation of new glucose is formed from
amino acids,lactate and glycerol)and promoting glycogen synthesis and
storage.Insulin promotes the peripheral uptake of glucose ,particularly in
skeletal muscle and it`s storage (as muscle glycogen)
=====PAGE 5 [T]=====
• Insulin promotes protein synthesis and lipogenesis ,and suppress
lipolysis.
• Insulin controls the release of intermediate metabolites includings
- Amino acids(glutmine,alanine)
- 3-carbone intermediates in oxidation( lactate, pyruvate)
- Free fatty acids(FFAS)
In the absence of insulin,e.g during fasting, these processes are
reversed and favor gluconeogenesis in liver from
glycogen,glycerol,amino acids and other 3-carbone precursors

• Categories of diabetes:
• Type 1 DM: Usually adolescent onset but may occur at any age.
• Cause: insulin deficiency from autoimmune destruction of insulin-secreting
pancreatic β cells. Antibodies consist directed to insulin,glutamic acid
decarboxylase(GAD),(IA-2) & zinc transportet ZnT8.Patients must have insulin,
and are prone to ketoacidosis and weight loss. Associated with other
autoimmune diseases (>90% HLA DR3 ± DR4). Concordance is only ~30% in
identical twins, indicating environmental influence. Four genes are important:
one(6q) determines islet sensitivity to damage (e.g from viruses or cross-
reactivity from cows’ milk-induced antibodies). In approximately 85% of newly
diagnosed type1 DM anti-GAD & anti-IA-2 antibodies are measured together.
• Latent autoimmune diabetes of adults (LADA) is a form of type 1 DM, with slower
progression to insulin dependence in later life.
=====PAGE 9 [T]=====
• Type 2 DM: (Formerly non-insulin-dependent DM, NIDDM) It`s
characterized by reduced sensitivity to the action of insulin and an inability
to produce sufficient insulin to overcome this`insulin resistance` itis at
‘epidemic’ levels in many places, mainly due to changes in lifestyle, but also
because of better diagnosis and improved longevity. Higher prevalence in
Asians, men, and the elderly (up to 18%).Most are over 40yrs, but
teenagers are now getting type 2 DM .
• Cause:
• ↓insulin secretion ± insulin resistance. It is associated with obesity, lack of
exercise,
• calorie and alcohol excess. ≥80% concordance in identical twins, indicating
stronger genetic influence than in type 1 DM.
=====PAGE 10 [T]=====
• . Typically progresses from a preliminary phase of impaired glucose
tolerance (IGT) or impaired fasting glucose (IFG). (This is a unique
window for lifestyle intervention.)
• Maturity onset diabetes of the young (MODY) is a rare autosomal
dominant form of type 2 DM affecting young people.
• Impaired glucose tolerance (IGT): Fasting plasma glucose <126mg/dl
and OGTT (oral glucose tolerance) 2h glucose ≥140mg/dl but
<200mg/dl.
=====PAGE 11 [T]=====
• Impaired fasting glucose (IFG): Fasting plasma glucose ≥ 110mg/dl but
<126mg/dl(WHO criteria). Do an OGTT to exclude DM. The cut-off
point is somewhat arbitrary.
• IGT and IFG denote different abnormalities of glucose regulation
(post-prandial and fasting). There may be lower risk of progression to
DM in IFG than IGT.
• Manage both with lifestyle advice + annual review. Incidence of DM if
IFG and HbA1C at high end of normal (5.5–6.4%) is ~25%.

Diagnosis of Diabetes mellitus:WHO criteria
• Symptoms of hyperglycaemia (eg polyuria, polydipsia, unexplained weight
loss,visual blurring, genital thrush, lethargy) AND raised venous glucose
detected once—fasting ≥ 126mg/dL(≥7mmol/L) or random ≥200mg/dL(
≥11.1mmol/L)
OR
Raised venous glucose on two separate occasions—fasting ≥126mg/dL,
random ≥200mg/dL
OR
oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT)—2h value ≥200mg/dL
.

How to perform an OGTT
-Patient be on unrestricted carbohydrate diet for 3 days
-Fasted overnight for at least 8 hours
-Rest for 30 mins
-Remain seated for duration of the test , with no smoking
-Measure plasma glucose before and 2 hrs after a 75g oral glucose
• HbA1c the value must be below 5.7%,the value 5.7%- 6.4% is
consider to be prediabetic , while diabetes can be diagnosed with a
HbA1c≥6.5%
=====PAGE 16 [T]=====
HbA1c is a blood test shows average blood glucose level over the past 2-3 months.
HbA1c may not accurately reflect the mean plasma glucose concentration. Avoid in
pregnancy, , and haemoglobinopathies,HbA1c results may be influenced by a
number of factors, including conditions that alter red cell survival or cause
interference with a specific assay.
It may be falsely low in patient with liver cirrhosis (increased red cell
turnover), recovery from recent acute blood loss (greater percentage of
younger erythrocytes with shorter exposure to glucose), or transfusion
(dilution of patient’s blood with nondiabetic donor blood).
In these instances, measurement of glycated serum proteins (fructosamine) or
direct measurement of plasma glucose concentration will more
accurately reflect glycemic control.

Differentiating type1 and 2 diabetes
• Occasionally it may be difficult to differentiate whether a patient has type 1 or 2
• DM, although they can present differently. Features of type 1 include
• weight loss; persistent hyper glycaemia despite diet and medications; presence of
autoantibodies: islet cell antibodies (ICA) and anti-glutamic acid decarboxylase
(GAD) antibodies; ketonuria.
Differences between type 1 and type 2 diabetes
• Type 1 DM Type 2 DM
• Epidemiology Often starts before puberty Older patients (usually)
• Genetics HLA D3 and D4 linked No HLA association
• Cause Autoimmune β-cell destruction Insulin resistance/β-cell dysfunction
• Presentation Polydipsia, polyuria,↓weight, ketosis Asymptomatic/complications, eg MI

• Not all new-onset DM in older people is type 2: if ketotic ± a poor
response to oral hypoglycemic (and patient is slim or has a family or
personal history of autoimmunity),think of latent autoimmune
diabetes in adults (LADA) and measure islet cell antibodies.
• C-peptide is the connecting peptide that is cleaved in the production
of insulin from pro-insulin.it can help clarify the differential diagnosis
of diabetes, as it is usually very low in long-standing type1 diabetes
and very high in sever insulin resistance. It is also useful in the
diagnosis of spontaneous hypoglycemia.

Management of diabetes mellitus
The aims are to improve symptoms of hyperglycemia and minimize the risks of long-term
microvascular and macrovascular complications.Treatment methods of DM includes dietary/lifestyle
modification,oral antidiabetic drugs and injected therapies.
General :Focus on education and lifestyle advice (eg exercise to ↑insulin sensitivity),
• healthy eating: ↓saturated fats, ↓sugar, ↑starch-carbohydrate, moderate
protein. Foods made just for diabetics are not needed. One could regard bariatric
surgery as a cure for DM in selected patients. Be prepared to negotiate HbA1c target
and review every 3–6 months.
Assess global vascular risk; start a high-intensity statin
, eg atorvastatin or rosuvastatin as tolerated, control BP . Give foot-care .
pregnancy care should be in a multidisciplinary clinic. Advise informing DVLA (Drive and Vehicle
Licensing Agency) and not to drive if hypoglycaemic spells (loss of hypo glycaemia awareness)
may lead to loss of license;
=====PAGE 20 [T]=====
• In patients with suspected type1 DM ,urgent treatment with insulin is
required and prompt referral to a specialist is usually needed.
• In patients with suspected type2 DM ,the first approach to
management involves advice about dietary and lifestyle modification.
Oral antidiabetic drugs are usually added in those who do not achieve
glycemic targets , or who have symptomatic hyperglycemia at
diagnosis and a high HbA1c.

Oral hypoglycemic agents
• Metformin: A biguanide. It`s classified as (Insulin sensitizer)↑ insulin
sensitivity & it`s main effects are on fasting glucose and are insulin
independent ,it reduces hepatic glucose production ,may also
increase insulin-mediated glucose uptake ,and has effects on gut
glucose uptake and utilization,dose not cause hypoglycemia and
helps weight.
• It is employed as first line therapy in all patients who tolerate it.
• Usually introduced at low dose 500mg twice daily ,the usual
maintenance dose is 1g twice daily.
• SE: nausea; diarrhoea(try modified-release version); abdominal pain;
Avoid if eGFR ≤36mL/min (due to risk lactic acidosis).
=====PAGE 23 [T]=====
• Sulfonylureas(SU):they are (Insulin secretagogues) ↑ insulin
secretion;
Their long term benefits in lowering microvascular complications of
diabetes were established. e.g gliclazide 40mg/d.glimepiride and
glipizide,the dose –response of all SU is steepest at low doses.
SE: hypoglycaemia (monitor glucose); it ↑ weight.
Glibenclamide is long-acting and prone to inducing hypoglycemia,so
should be avoided in older patients.
=====PAGE 24 [T]=====
• DPP4 inhibitors/gliptins: Block the action of DPP-4, an enzyme which destroys the
hormone incretin(the effect is the augmentation of insulin secretion seen when a glucose
stimulus is given orally rather than iv,and reflect the release of incretin peptides from the
gut).The incretin hormones are primarily glucagon-like peptide 1(IGPL-1) and gastric
inhibitory polypeptide(GIP)which act to potentiate insulin secretion,these are rapidly
broken down by dipeptyl peptidase 4(DPP-4).
• The `gliptins`,or DPP-4 inhibitors ,prevent break down and enhance concentration of
endogenouse GLP-1 &GIP.
• The first DPP-4 inhibitor was Sitagliptin others now available are vildagliptin,saxsagliptin
,linagliptin and alogliptin.they are very well tolerated and weight neutral.
• Glitazone: ↑insulin sensitivity; SE: hypoglycaemia, fractures, fluid retention, ↑LFT (do
LFT every 8wks for 1yr, stop if ALT up >3-fold). CI: past or present congested cardiac failure;
osteoporosis; monitor weight, and stop if ↑or oedema.
=====PAGE 25 [T]=====
• SGLT2 inhibitors: Selective sodium–glucose co-transporter-2 inhibitor.
Blocks the reabsorption of glucose in the kidneys and promotes
excretion of excess glucose in the urine (eg empagliflozin, shown to
reduce mortality from cardiovascular disease in patients with type 2
DM, when compared to placebo).
• Other e.g Dapagliflozin,canagliflozin
• SE :Genital fungal infection ,Euglycemic diabetic ketoacidosis(i.e DKA
not associated with marked hyperglycemia)has been recognized as a
rare complication of this class of drugs.

Using Insulin
• Insulin was discovered in 1921 and transformed the management of type1 DM which was
a fatal disorder until then.
• Up to the 1980s,insulin was obtained by extraction and purification from pancreases of
cows &pigs(bovine and porcine insulins).Recombinant DNA technology enabled large-
scale production of human insulin.the aminoacid sequence of insulin can be alterd to
produce analogues of insulin ,which differ in their rate of absorption from the site of
injection,e.g rapid acting insulin analogues:(insulin lispro,aspart,glulisine).The onset of
action of insulin analogues may be further hastened by the addition of excipients to the
formulation .e.g of Long-acting insulin analogues:(glargine,detemir,degludec).
• Vital to educate to self-adjust doses in the light of exercise, finger prick glucose,calorie
intake, and carbohydrate counting.
• Can modify diet wisely and avoid binge drinking (danger of delayed hypoglycaemia).
• Partner can abort hypoglycaemia: sugary drinks; GlucoGel orally if coma (no risk of
aspiration).Dose titration to target—eg by 2–4 UNIT steps.
=====PAGE 27 [T]=====
• Subcutaneous insulins Short-, medium-, or long-acting. Strength: 100U/mL.
1- Ultra-fast acting (HumalogR; NovorapidR); inject at start of meal, or just after
(unless sugar-laden)—helps match what is actually eaten (vs what is planned).
2- Isophane insulin (variable peak at 4–12h): favoured by NICE (it’s cheap!).
3- Pre-mixed insulins (eg NovoMixR 30 = 30% short-acting and 70% long-acting).
4- Long-acting recombinant human insulin analogues (insulin glargine) are used
at bedtime in type 1 or 2 DM. There is no awkward peak, so good if nocturnal
hypoglycaemia is an issue. Caution if considering pregnancy. Insulin detemir
is similar and has a role in intensive insulin regimens for overweight type 2 DM
=====PAGE 28 [T]=====
• Common insulin regimens :Plan the regimen to suit the lifestyle, not vice versa.
Disposable pens: dial dose; insert needle 90° to skin. Vary injection site (outer
thigh/abdomen); change needle daily.
• ‘BD biphasic regimen’: twice daily premixed insulins by pen (eg NovoMix )—
useful in type 2 DM or type 1 with regular lifestyle.
• ‘QDS regimen’: before meals ultra-fast insulin + bedtime long-acting analogue:
useful in type 1 DM for achieving a flexible lifestyle (eg for adjusting doses with
size of meals, or exercise).
• Once-daily before-bed long-acting insulin(Basal Inulin): a good initial insulin
regimen when switching from tablets in type 2 DM. Typical dose to work up to
(slowly!): ≥1U/24h
• for every unit of BMI in adults. Consider retaining metformin if needed for tight
control and patient is unable to use BD regime.
=====PAGE 29 [T]=====
• Dose adjustment for normal eating (DAFNE): Multidisciplinary teams
promoting autonomy can save lives. DAFNE found that training in
flexible, intensive insulin dosing improved glycaemic control as well as
well-being.
• Subcutaneous insulin dosing during intercurrent illnesses (eg
influenza)
-Advise patients to avoid stopping insulin during acute illness.
-illness often increases insulin requirements despite reduced food
intake.
-Maintain calorie intake, eg using milk.
=====PAGE 30 [T]=====
-Check blood glucose ≥ 4 times a day and look for ketonuria. Increase insulin
doses if glucose rising. Advise to get help from a specialist diabetes nurse or GP
if concerned (esp. if glucose levels are rising or ketonuria). One option is 2-hourly
ultra fast-acting insulin (eg 6–8U) preceded by a finger prick glucose check.
-Admit if vomiting, dehydrated, ketotic, a child, or pregnant.
• Insulin pumps (continous subcutaneous insulin) Consider when attempts to
reach HbA1C with multiple daily injections have resulted in disabling
hypoglycaemia
• or person has been unable to achieve target HbA1C despite careful management.

Glucagon-like peptide (GLP) analogues
• Work as incretin mimetics. Incretins are gut peptides that work by aug
menting insulin release. Plasma glucose is lowered by effects on
insulin,glucagon secretion and by decelerating gastric emptying ,they
are given by subcutaneous injection. Patients must have BMI >35 and
specific psychological or other medical problems associated with
obesity, or have a BMI lower than 35 kg/m2, and for whom insulin
therapy would have significant occupational implications, or weight
loss would benefit other significant obesity related comorbidities.
• To continue a GLP 1 mimetic, a person should have a beneficial
metabolic response (a reduction of HbA1C by at least 1%) and an
weight loss of at least 3% of initial body weight in 6 months.
