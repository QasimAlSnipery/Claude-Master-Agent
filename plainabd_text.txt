Plain Abdominal X ray
• Standard plain film of the abdomen is supine
anteroposterior (AP) view.
• Erect film: little further diagnostic information
at the expense of increased radiation
• Free intraperitoneal air is best assessed on
erect chest X Ray
• If patient unable to sit or stand, a lateral
decubitus view (patient lying on side)

Intestinal gas pattern:
• Relatively large amounts of gas are usually present in
the stomach and colon in a normal patient.
• Stomach can be readily identified In LUQ by the
band-like shadows of the gastric rugae in the supine
view.
• There may be some gas in the normal small bowel, but
it is rarely sufficient to outline the whole of a loop.
• If the bowel is dilated it is important to try and decide
which part is involved.

Pneumoperitoneum
• The most common cause of spontaneous pneumoperitoneum is
a perforated peptic ulcer and two-thirds of such cases are
recognizable radiologically.
• Free intraperitoneal air is a normal finding after laparotomy or
laparoscopy. In adults air is usually absorbed within 7 days. In
children air absorbs within 24 hours.
• An increase in the amount of air on successive films indicates
continuing leakage of air.
• Pneumoperitoneum under the right hemidiaphragm is usually
easy to recognize on an erect CXR as a curvilinear collection
of gas between line of diaphragm and the opacity of the liver.

• Abdominal calcification
• It is essential to localize the calcification, as once the organ of
origin is known the pattern or shape of the calcification will
usually limit the diagnosis to just one or two choices
• Faecoliths: Calcified faecoliths may be seen in any part of
abdomen or pelvis.
• Appendicolith are an important radiological observation if
patient has features of appendicitis, is a strong indication that
the patient has acute appendicitis, often with gangrene and
perforation. However, only a small proportion of patients with
appendicitis have a radiologically visible appendicolith.
• Pelvic vein phleboliths are very common; they may cause
diagnostic confusion in that they may be mistaken for urinary
calculi and faecoliths.

• Calcified mesenteric LNcaused by old tuberculosis, they
are often mobile.
• Vascular calcification has curvilinear appearance.
Calcification is frequently present in the walls of abdominal
aortic aneurysms
• Uterine fibroids may contain numerous irregularly shaped,
well-defined calcifications conforming to the spherical
outline of fibroids.
• Soft tissue calcification in the buttocks may be seen
following injection of certain medicines. These shadows
can at times be confused with intra-abdominal
calcifications.
• Ovarian masses that contains calcification “usually shape of
tooth” is the dermoid cyst

=====PAGE 17 [T]=====
• Adrenal calcification occurs after adrenal haemorrhage,
tuberculosis and occasionally in adrenal tumours. Only a minority
of patients with Addison’s disease have adrenal calcification.
• Liver calcification occurs in hepatoma, hydatid cysts, abscesses
and tuberculosis may also calcify over time.
• Gall stones about 20% of gall stones are visible on plain film,
typically, have a dense outer rim with a more lucent centre.
• Splenic calcification is rarely of clinical significance.
• Pancreatic calcification occurs in chronic pancreatitis. Multiple
calcifications obliquely located at level of L1 vertebra.
• Renal stones and other calcifications of the urinary tract are
dense calcifications, may take shape of PCS and called staghorn
stones.

Gastrointestinal Tract Imaging Modalities
• GI Contrast Studies “Barium studies”
• have dramatically reduced as endoscopy services have
developed.
• Barium sulphate: is the best contrast medium
because produces excellent opacification. But it has
disadvantages: it may solidify and impact proximal to
a colonic or rectal stricture.
• Contraindication: suspected perforation, causes severe
peritonitis if there is barium leak from the bowel.
10/27/2025 Soran Hisham 22
=====PAGE 23 [T]=====
Gastrointestinal Tract Imaging Modalities
• Gastrografin: water-soluble contrast medium,
predominantly used when perforations or anastomotic
leaks are suspected. Disadvantages: less radio-opaque
than barium, irritant when enters the lungs, may cause
pulmonary edema.
• Double-contrast technique: the mucosa is initially
coated with barium and then the lumen is distended by
introducing air or some other gas, used for stomach
and colon to demonstrate mucosa abnormality.

Gastrointestinal Tract Imaging Modalities
• Ultrasound can detect intra-abdominal fluid, assess
bowel wall, used when suspecting infantile pyloric
stenosis, intussusception, appendicitis, diverticulitis.
• EUS: is a specialized procedure that notably assessing
the depth of invasion of tumours in esophagus, gastric
or rectal wall. Also diagnosing small tumors in the
pancreas and duodenum.
• Both are used to guide FNA & Biopsies.

Gastrointestinal Tract Imaging Modalities
• CT: show the full width of GI wall, surrounding fat, but it is not
good to assess mucosa.
• Lumen of GI tract can be evaluated using
– Gastrografin “positive contrast agent”
– Air as the contrast agent (virtual colonoscopy)
– Water as a negative contrast agent especially in the staging of upper
GI malignancies
• CT is used for diagnosing and staging GI tumors, diagnose
inflammation “appendicitis, diverticulitis”, intestinal
obstruction and suspected damage to the bowel wall following
trauma postoperative complications.
• Mesenteric CTA can assess mesenteric vessels to diagnose
ischemia.

Gastrointestinal Tract Imaging Modalities
• MRI: has limited role in GI imaging due to artefacts
caused by peristalsis. But routinely used for local
staging of rectal carcinoma and increasingly for
imaging the small bowel “MR Enterography, in which
patient is prepared and given antiperistaltic agents”.
• (FDG-PET)/CT is used to identify sites of metastatic
disease in selected patients with a primary carcinoma
of the gastrointestinal (GI) tract.

=====PAGE 30 [T]=====
• Filling defect: any process that prevents
the normal filling of the lumen.
• (i) intraluminal filling defects (e.g.food),
surrounded by barium
• (ii) intramural filling defects (e.g. a
carcinoma or leiomyoma) which cause an
indentation on the lumen and are not
completely surrounded by barium
• (iii) extramural filling defects
compressing the lumen from the outside
(e.g. enlarged pancreas or lymph nodes),
in which the mucosa is preserved but is
stretched over the filling defect
