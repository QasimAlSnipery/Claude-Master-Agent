# Leishmaniasis

## The Big Picture
Leishmaniasis = **intracellular protozoan** (*Leishmania*) spread by the **female sandfly (Phlebotomus)** bite. Three clinical forms: **cutaneous** (Baghdad boil), **mucocutaneous**, and **visceral (kala-azar — fatal if untreated)**. Cutaneous is prevalent across Iraq. Diagnosis by demonstrating the parasite; treatment with **antimonials / amphotericin B**.

## Parasite & Vector
- Genus ***Leishmania***, family Trypanosomatidae; intracellular in macrophages.
- **Vector: female phlebotomine sandfly** — injects **promastigotes**, which become **amastigotes** in the human macrophage/reticuloendothelial system.
- Old World (Africa/Asia/Middle East — cutaneous + visceral) vs New World (Americas — + mucocutaneous).

## Clinical Forms & Species
- **Cutaneous: L. tropica, L. major** ("oriental sore / Baghdad boil").
- **Mucocutaneous: L. braziliensis.**
- **Visceral: L. donovani, L. infantum** (kala-azar).

## Cutaneous Leishmaniasis (CL)
- "Oriental sore / Baghdad boil." Vectors: P. papatasi, P. sergenti.
- **On exposed skin** (face, ears, nose, limbs); incubation 2 wk–years (usually 2–6 months).
- Lesion: **macule → papule → ulcer** with raised margin + central necrosis; heals with scar; secondary bacterial infection common. No systemic symptoms usually.
- **Diagnosis:** Giemsa-stained smear/scraping from **active margin**, biopsy, **NNN culture**; antibody of limited value; PCR best.
- Often self-heals; treat to speed healing/reduce scarring: topical **paromomycin**, oral **miltefosine**, **antimonials**.

## Visceral Leishmaniasis (Kala-azar / Dum-Dum fever)
- *L. donovani*; parasite spreads to **spleen, liver, bone marrow** (reticuloendothelial).
- Incubation ~90 days (insidious).
- **Features:** massive **hepatosplenomegaly**, fluctuating fever (37.5–40°C), **pancytopenia** (↓ erythropoiesis + **thrombocytopenia → mucosal bleeding**), weight loss; complications = diarrhoea/dysentery, bronchopneumonia. **Post-kala-azar dermal leishmaniasis (PKDL)** can follow.
- **Diagnosis:** **bone marrow aspirate (60–85%)**, splenic aspirate (>95% but bleeding risk), serology useful (unlike CL), **PCR** (high sensitivity, species ID).
- **Treatment (all VL needs prompt full treatment):** **amphotericin B (first-line)**, miltefosine, sodium stibogluconate, paromomycin.

## Mucocutaneous (MCL)
- Destructive nasopharyngeal lesions; systemic **antimonials**, amphotericin B for severe.

## Epidemiology
- 98 countries; ~700,000–1.3 million new cases/yr. **Cutaneous prevalent across Iraq except the 3 Kurdistan governorates.** Iraq ~8000 CL + ~1800 VL/yr.

## Prevention & Control
- **Early diagnosis + treatment** (reduces transmission), **vector control** (insecticide spray, **insecticide-treated nets**, environmental management), **personal protection** (long sleeves, repellent, bed nets), surveillance, control of animal reservoirs, community education.

## Walk-Away Points
- **Sandfly (Phlebotomus) → promastigote → amastigote in macrophages.**
- 3 forms: **cutaneous (Baghdad boil, L. tropica/major)**, mucocutaneous, **visceral (kala-azar, L. donovani)**.
- Visceral = **hepatosplenomegaly + pancytopenia + fever**, fatal untreated → **bone marrow/splenic aspirate**, **amphotericin B**.
- Cutaneous on exposed skin, ulcer + scar; antimonials remain first-line overall.
