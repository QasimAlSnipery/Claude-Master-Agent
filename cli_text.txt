Peripheral Arterial Disease
(Chronic limb ischemia)
Peripheral Arterial Disease
Is a chronic atherosclerotic condition characterized by progressive occlusion of the arteries
supplying the lower limbs, and less commonly, the upper extremities. It represents a significant
global health challenge, driven largely by the aging population and the increasing prevalence of
major cardiovascular risk factors including diabetes mellitus, cigarette smoking, hypertension,
and dyslipidemia.
Age is the most important risk factor for PAD: the disease is uncommon before the age of 40,
increases sharply in incidence from the sixth to eighth decades, and may affect 25% or more of
individuals aged 80 years and above.
Etiology of PAD
• Atherosclerosis (> 95%) of the cases.
• Rare causes (< 5%): Non atherosclerotic cause as : Cystic adventitial disease, Popliteal
entrapment syndrome, Persistent sciatic artery, Fibromuscular dysplasia, Thromboangiitis
obliterans (TAO), also called Buerger disease, is a non-atherosclerotic, segmental, inflammatory
disease that most commonly affects the small to medium-sized arteries and veins of the
extremities
Pathophysiology
The normal resting oxygen requirement of the lower limb in adults is approximately 130–150
mL/min. In patients with symptomatic PAD, an imbalance arises between the metabolic
demands of the limb and the arterial perfusion supplying it. During exercise, the increased
oxygen requirement of skeletal muscle cannot be met because blood flow is limited by arterial
obstruction. This leads to a shift toward anaerobic metabolism with the accumulation of
metabolites—such as lactate—producing the characteristic cramp-like pain of intermittent
claudication.
When oxygen delivery becomes insufficient even at rest, the patient develops persistent
ischemic rest pain, marking the transition to chronic limb-threatening ischemia (CLTI). At
this stage, both limb viability and overall patient survival are at significant risk.

In the majority of cases, PAD results from the slow and progressive buildup of atherosclerotic
plaque within the arterial wall, causing gradual luminal narrowing. In advanced or critical
disease, resting perfusion is inadequate to meet the basic metabolic needs of the tissues. When
tibial pressures fall below 50 mmHg (or toe pressures below 30 mmHg), severe ischemia
ensues, often leading to tissue necrosis due to critically reduced blood flow.
Epidemiology
Peripheral arterial disease (PAD) affects approximately 10% of the general population, with
higher prevalence among smokers and individuals with diabetes mellitus. The condition is more
common in men and becomes increasingly prevalent with advancing age. Importantly, PAD is a
strong indicator of systemic atherosclerosis, serving as an independent surrogate marker for both
coronary artery disease and carotid artery disease.
Risk Factors
The risk factors for PAD mirror those of atherosclerosis and include the following:
1. Smoking: Cigarette smoking is one of the strongest and most consistent predictors of PAD
across populations. Chronic active smokers exhibit a two- to fourfold higher incidence of PAD,
and even exposure to secondhand smoke increases risk. Smoking accelerates disease
progression and substantially heightens the likelihood of amputation and mortality.
2. Diabetes Mellitus: Diabetes is strongly associated with both the prevalence and severity of
PAD. Reported odds ratios are similar to those of smoking (two- to fourfold increase). The
combination of diabetes and smoking is particularly detrimental, conferring a markedly higher
risk of critical limb ischemia, limb loss, and cardiovascular mortality.

3. Hypertension: Hypertension is common among individuals with PAD. The strength of its
association is significant and contributes to both the development and progression of
atherosclerotic disease in the peripheral circulation.
4. Dyslipidemia: Elevated total cholesterol and low high-density lipoprotein (HDL) levels are
well-established contributors to PAD. Some studies also suggest an association between elevated
triglycerides and increased PAD risk.
5. Obesity and Metabolic Syndrome: Central obesity, particularly as part of the metabolic
syndrome characterized by abdominal obesity, hypertension, elevated triglycerides, low HDL
levels, and impaired fasting glucose is more strongly associated with PAD than overall body
weight alone.
6. Inflammation : Systemic inflammation plays a significant role in the pathogenesis of
atherosclerosis. Elevated levels of inflammatory biomarkers such as C-reactive protein (CRP)
and fibrinogen are consistently linked to increased PAD risk.
7. Socioeconomic Status: Lower socioeconomic status—including reduced income, limited
education, and higher psychosocial stress has been associated with a higher prevalence of PAD,
reflecting disparities in both risk factor exposure and access to preventive healthcare.
Clinical presentations
Clinical presentation in PAD may be asymptomatic or symptomatic, and the disease can manifest
along a spectrum ranging from chronic, non-critical ischemia to acute or critical limb-threatening
ischemia. The predominant symptoms include pain during walking (intermittent claudication) or
pain at rest, depending on the severity of arterial stenosis.
Symptom severity is determined by both the extent and anatomical distribution of atherosclerotic
lesions. These lesions may be isolated and focal or, particularly in diabetic patients, diffuse and
multilevel. The superficial femoral artery (SFA) is the most commonly affected vessel, followed
by the popliteal artery, especially in individuals with diabetes. Below-knee arteries are often
severely involved, contributing to advanced ischemia.

Asymptomatic Disease
Asymptomatic PAD is often detected incidentally during examination or vascular studies
performed for other reasons. These patients typically do not require surgical intervention.
Management focuses on risk factor modification and best medical therapy, which also confers
substantial protection against coronary events.
Symptomatic Disease
1. Chronic Presentation
Chronic PAD most commonly manifests as intermittent claudication, which occurs when arterial
stenosis exceeds approximately 70%. The exact threshold varies depending on collateral
circulation and the rapidity of disease progression.
2. Acute-on-Chronic Ischemia
This refers to a sudden decline in limb perfusion occurring on a background of established PAD.
It may rapidly progress to critical ischemia, requiring urgent evaluation and management.

Intermittent Claudication (IC)
Intermittent claudication is characterized by cramp-like muscle pain induced by walking or
exertion and relieved by rest.
Key characteristics include:
• The distance walked before symptom onset is termed the claudication distance, typically
reproducible on repeated exertion (sometimes slightly longer due to reactive vasodilation).
• Calf claudication commonly reflects superficial femoral artery (SFA) disease.
• More proximal lesions (e.g., aorto-iliac disease) produce thigh or buttock claudication.
• As PAD progresses, claudication distance shortens, reflecting worsening ischemia.
Critical Limb Ischemia (Chronic Limb-Threatening Ischemia, CLTI)
CLTI is defined as PAD associated with rest pain, ulceration, or gangrene lasting more than two
weeks. It carries a high risk of major amputation, mortality, and significantly reduced quality of
life.
Clinical features:
• Rest pain, typically beginning in the toes and forefoot—the most distal sites of circulation.
• Pain is unrelated to exercise and reflects severe ischemia, where even resting oxygen
demands cannot be met.
• Rest pain often worsens at night due to:
o Loss of gravitational assistance to perfusion when lying flat
o Reduced nocturnal cardiac output
o Vasodilation from warm bedding causing a “steal” of blood toward the skin
• Patients often obtain relief by dangling the foot over the edge of the bed, which improves
perfusion through gravity and cooling-induced vasoconstriction of superficial vessels.

Blue Toe Syndrome (“Foot Trash”)
This condition results from microemboli originating from a proximal source such as an
abdominal aortic aneurysm, popliteal aneurysm, or cardiac thrombus occluding small vessels of
the toes and feet.
Clinical features:
• Painful, cyanotic toes or localized ischemic skin patches
• May progress to ulceration or digital gangrene
• Management includes anticoagulation and treatment of the embolic source
• Can become chronic due to progressive fibrosis of embolized vessels
Gangrene
Gangrene is defined as the localized death (necrosis) of tissue resulting from a critical reduction
or complete cessation of blood supply, often accompanied by secondary bacterial invasion. The
pathological process involves coagulative necrosis of affected tissues due to severe ischemia,
with or without superimposed infection.
Dry Gangrene
• Occurs without infection
• Tissue becomes dry, mummified, black, and typically insensate
• Progresses slowly; often well-demarcated
Wet Gangrene
• Necrosis in the presence of infection
• Often polymicrobial
• Can develop due to critical ischemia or secondary to infection-induced microvascular
thrombosis
• Represents a surgical emergency due to risk of sepsis
Evaluation and Diagnostic Workup
The evaluation of patients with peripheral arterial disease (PAD) aims to determine the severity
of ischemia, assess whether symptoms warrant interventional management, and evaluate the
patient’s cardiorespiratory fitness for potential invasive procedures. A structured approach
includes clinical assessment, bedside vascular tests, and imaging studies.

1. Clinical History and Physical Examination
A thorough history and examination are essential to establish symptom severity and overall
cardiovascular risk.
History
• Obtain a full cardiovascular and respiratory history to assess suitability for invasive
treatment.
• Document the claudication distance and any changes over time.
• Record the presence of rest pain, ulceration, or gangrene.
Examination
• Palpate and document all peripheral pulses.
o If pedal pulses are present at rest, reassess them post-exercise to identify inflow
disease.
• Perform Buerger’s test and measure Buerger’s angle.
• Conduct a complete vascular, musculoskeletal and neurological examination of the lower
limbs.
2. Bedside Vascular Tests
Ankle-Brachial Pressure Index (ABPI)
• Conducted in a rested patient in a warm environment.
• Calculated by dividing the highest ankle pressure (dorsalis pedis or posterior tibial) by the
highest brachial pressure.
• Interpretation:
o <0.7 → consistent with claudication
o <0.3 → suggests rest pain / critical ischemia
• Should be interpreted alongside Buerger’s test; the angle of Doppler signal disappearance
correlates with the degree of ischemia.
Exercise ABPI
• Useful in aorto-iliac disease, where resting pulses may be preserved.
• Performed after supervised treadmill exercise or walking until claudication occurs.
• A drop of >20 mmHg in ankle pressure indicates a hemodynamically significant lesion.
Toe-Brachial Pressure Index (TBI)

• Particularly useful in patients with heavily calcified arteries (Diabetes, CKD), where ABPI
may be falsely elevated.
• Digital arteries resist calcification, making TBI a more reliable measure of perfusion.
• Toe pressure ≥ 30 mmHg → good potential for wound healing.
• Toe pressure < 30 mmHg → poor healing; revascularization often required.
• Toe pressure < 20 mmHg → extremely poor healing; high risk for major amputation.
Segmental Limb Pressure Studies
• Measures pressures at multiple levels (mid-thigh, distal thigh, popliteal, ankle).
• Helps localize the level of arterial obstruction.
Transcutaneous Oxygen Pressure (TcPO₂)
• Measures the partial pressure of oxygen at the skin surface around an ulcer.
• Helps determine healing potential and the need for revascularization.
3. Vascular Imaging
Arterial Duplex Ultrasound
• First-line non-invasive imaging modality.
• Assesses severity of stenosis, inflow, and runoff.
• Diagnostic features:
o 50% stenosis → biphasic waveform with increased peak systolic velocity
o >70% stenosis → monophasic waveform
CT Angiography (CTA) / MR Angiography (MRA)
• Provide detailed anatomical information:
o Level and severity of disease
o Number of lesions
o Quality of inflow and runoff vessels
• CTA is limited by contrast nephrotoxicity and radiation exposure.
• MRA may be less accurate in small or heavily calcified vessels.
4. Catheter-Directed Angiography
• Considered the gold standard for diagnosing PAD.
• Typically performed when endovascular treatment is planned.
• Provides high-resolution, dynamic images of arterial flow.
Complications of catheterization
• Contrast-induced nephropathy or allergic reactions

Management Strategies for Peripheral Arterial Disease (PAD)
Management of PAD involves a combination of conservative therapy, best medical treatment,
and revascularization when indicated. All patients regardless of whether they undergo
endovascular or surgical intervention should receive aggressive risk-factor modification and
long-term medical therapy.
Approximately:
• 1/3 of Intermittent claudicating patients improve with conservative therapy,
• 1/3 remain stable, and
• 1/3 experience worsening symptoms within 6 months.
1. Conservative (Non-Surgical) Management
Lifestyle Modification
Essential components include:
• Smoking cessation, supported with nicotine replacement and behavioral counselling.
• Blood pressure control, aiming for <140/90 mmHg.
• Optimization of lipid levels: Dietary and statins
• Strict glycemic control and management of chronic kidney disease.
• Structured exercise therapy, ideally in supervised programs:
o Walk until near-maximal pain
o Rest briefly
o Resume walking
o Duration: 30–45 minutes, 3–4 times weekly, for ≥12 weeks
• Weight reduction and adherence to a heart-healthy diet
2. Best Medical Therapy (BMT)
Antiplatelet Therapy
A cornerstone of PAD management to prevent arterial thrombosis:

• Aspirin: works by irreversibly inhibiting cyclooxygenase-1 (COX-1) in platelets. This
blocks the synthesis of thromboxane A₂, a potent promoter of platelet aggregation and
vasoconstriction. Because platelets cannot synthesize new COX enzymes, the antiplatelet
effect lasts for the entire lifespan of the platelet (7-10 days).
• Clopidogrel: P2Y12 receptor inhibitor (commonly used due to superior risk-reduction
data). It irreversibly inhibits the P2Y12 component of ADP receptors on platelet surfaces.
This prevents platelet activation and aggregation that reduces risk of clot formation.
Vasoactive Pharmacotherapy
Used for symptom relief in intermittent claudication and must be combined with risk-factor
control.
Naftidrofuryl Oxalate (Praxilene)
• 5-HT2 receptor antagonist
• Increases claudication distance
• Useful for patients unable to participate in supervised exercise programs
Cilostazol
• Phosphodiesterase III inhibitor
• Increases intracellular cAMP
• Inhibits smooth muscle contraction and platelet aggregation
• May reduce smooth muscle proliferation (similar mechanism relevant in restenosis)
Prostanoids
Used in selected cases of severe ischemia:
• PGE1 and PGI2
• Potent vasodilators
• Anti-platelet and endothelial-protective effects
• Iloprost (stable PGI2 analogue) has a longer half-life (~30 min) and is clinically useful
Statin Therapy
Recommended regardless of baseline lipid profile due to strong benefits in:
• Plaque stabilization
• Reduction of cardiovascular events
• Improvement of endothelial function

3. Revascularization
Revascularization may be endovascular, surgical, or hybrid, depending on patient and disease
characteristics.
Factors Influencing the Decision
• Overall health and perioperative risk (30% mortality within 5 years for PAD patients)
• Lesion characteristics (length, location, number)
• Availability of suitable access for endovascular treatment
• Availability of an adequate distal target artery for bypass
• Whether amputation is more appropriate (non-reconstructable disease or poor functional
status)
Indications for Intervention
Absolute Indications
• Critical limb ischemia, including:
o Rest pain
o Tissue loss
o Gangrene
Relative Indications
• Disabling short-distance claudication (<50 meters)
• Symptoms limiting lifestyle or occupation
• Failure to improve after sustained conservative therapy
• Progressive worsening of symptoms
Endovascular Interventions
These include:
• Balloon angioplasty (including subintimal angioplasty)
• Stent implantation
• Atherectomy
o Mechanical (directional)
o Laser atherectomy
Advantages:
Less invasive, no general anesthesia, lower cardiac stress, fewer wound complications, and
quicker recovery.
Patency rates vary with lesion length, anatomical location, and degree of calcification

Open Surgical Revascularization
Open surgery remains the gold standard for complex or long-segment disease but is associated
with higher physiological stress.
Types of Open Procedures
1. Endarterectomy with Patch Angioplasty
• Removal of atherosclerotic plaque through a longitudinal arteriotomy
• Closure with vein or synthetic patch
• Common procedures: femoral endarterectomy, profundaplasty
2. Bypass Surgery
Used when segmental occlusion is extensive. Conduits may be vein (preferred) or synthetic.
Common bypasses procedures :
• Aorto-bifemoral
• Ilio-femoral
• Femoral–femoral crossover
• Axillobifemoral
• Femoral–popliteal (above or below knee)
• Distal bypass (ATA, PTA, peroneal, dorsalis pedis)
Success depends on:
• Good inflow and runoff
• High-quality conduit (vein >3 mm, no fibrosis/varicosities)
• Well-constructed, disease-free anastomosis
• Adequate anticoagulation
• Continued BMT post-operatively
Hybrid Procedures
Combine open and endovascular approaches during the same session.
Examples:
• Femoral endarterectomy + patch angioplasty + antegrade SFA puncture and stenting
• Retrograde puncture with iliac stenting

Other Treatment Options
1. Amputation
Indicated for:
• Extensive necrosis
• Non-reconstructable arterial disease
• Severe infection / sepsis
• Patients too frail for revascularization
A quick amputation may be safer than a prolonged, high-risk reconstruction.
2. Lumbar Sympathectomy
• Can be open or chemical
• Destroys the lumbar sympathetic chain → reduces vasoconstriction
• Limited effectiveness in diabetic patients due to autonomic neuropathy
• Now rarely used, but may help in selected cases of non-reconstructable disease
3. Stem cell therapy is an emerging treatment option for patients with chronic limb-threatening
ischemia (CLTI) who are not suitable for surgical or endovascular revascularization. It involves
the intramuscular injection of autologous or allogeneic progenitor cells, most commonly bone
marrow–derived or adipose-derived stem cells. These cells promote angiogenesis and improve
microcirculation through growth factor release and endothelial repair. Clinical studies have
demonstrated improvements in rest pain, ulcer healing, and limb perfusion in selected “no-
option” patients. Although promising, stem cell therapy remains experimental and is currently
used mainly within clinical trials or specialized centers.
