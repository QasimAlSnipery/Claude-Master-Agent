shape of foot is regarded as club foot but the

heavy tapering stick with a knobby end or

DEFORMITIES OF THE FOOT
The normal position of the foot is plantigrade - i.e. when the
patient stands, the sole is at right angles to the leg. Equinus
(like a horse's foot) means .that the hindfoot is fixed in
plantarflexion (pointing downwards), Plantaris looks similar,
but the ankle is neutral and only the forefoot is plantar
flexed.
Calcaneus is fixed dorsiflexion at the ankle. A dorsiflexion
deformity in the mid-foot produces a rocker-bottom foot.

•
• Normally the medial border of the foot, even when weight-
bearing, forms a longitudinal arch. The arrangement of the
heads of metatarsals also produces an anterior or transverse
arch in the forefoot. Flattening of the longitudinal arch is
referred to as a planus deformity or flat-foot; and a dropped
metatarsal arch as anterior flat-foot. An excessively high arch
produces a cavus deformity.
• Common deformities of the toes are lateral deviation of the big
toe (hallux valgus), proximal interphalangeal flexion of one of
the lesser toes (hammer-toe)
• Claw toe is partial flexion of interphalangeal joints and
hyperextension of metatarsophalangeal joints.

Causes of Club foot
• Exact cause of club foot is unknown but the commonest type is
“Idiopathic Talipes Equino Varus”. Other causes of club foot are:
• Neurological disorders like Poliomyelitis, Cerebral Palsy (C.P.), any
soft tissue tumor compressing lumbosacral plexus.
• Trauma including burn.
• Spine disorders like “ Spina Bifida”.
• Some time it may be a part of generalized disorders like
Arthrogryposis Multiplex Congenita.

CONGENITAL TALIPES EQUINOVARUS (IDIOPATHIC CLUB-
FOOT)
 In this deformity the heel is in Equinus (pointing
downwards), the entire Hindfoot in Varus (tilted towards
the midline) and the mid-foot and forefoot adducted and
supinated (twisted medially and the sole turned
upwards). It is relatively common; the incidence is 1 or 2
per 1000 birth and boys are affected twice as often as
girls. The condition is bilateral in one-third of cases.
Similar deformities are seen in neurological disorders e.g.,
myelomeningocele, and in arthrogryposis.

Treatment of Club foot
In 1948 Ponseti proposed the following guidelines for
treatment of Club foot:
1- All the components of the Club foot deformity should be
corrected simultaneously except obviously the EQUINUS,
which is finally corrected at the last otherwise it will be
ended by Rocker Bottom Deformity in which the talus is
vertically located causing rigid flat foot.
2- The Cavus ( High arched foot) is the result of pronation
of fore foot in relation to the hind foot. It is corrected as
the foot is abducted in supinated position of forefoot and
thereby placing it in proper alignment with hind foot.
11/10/2025 Prof.Dr.Omer Barawi 15
=====PAGE 16 [T]=====
3- While the whole foot is held in supination It is gradually and
gently abducted under the talus which is held secured against
rotation in the ankle mortise by applying counter- pressure
with the thumb over the lateral aspect of the head of the
talus.
4- The heel Varus and supination of foot will automatically get
corrected when the entire foot is fully abducted under the
talus.
5- Finally, the EQUINUS is corrected by dorsiflexing the foot
with or without percutaneous elongation of tendoachills.

Treatment
The aim of treatment is to produce and
maintain a plantigrade, supple foot that will
function well. There are several methods of
treatment, but relapse is common, especially
in babies with associated neuromuscular
disorders.
11/10/2025 Prof.Dr.Omer Barawi 19
=====PAGE 20 [T]=====
treatment
• Treatment should begin early, preferably within a
day or two of birth. This consists of repeated
manipulation and adhesive strapping or application
of plaster-of-Paris , POP casts, which will maintain
the correction. Treatment is supervised by a
orthopaedist , Plaster-of-Paris casting requires
serial changes and manipulations in a clinic setting
at weekly intervals usually for 5 weeks. Sometimes
surgical release of the Achilles tendon is needed to
complete the correction.
11/10/2025 Prof.Dr.Omer Barawi 20
=====PAGE 21 [T]=====
Operative treatment
Resistant cases will need surgery.
Late presenters often have severe deformities with secondary
bony changes, and the relapsed club-foot is complicated by
scarring from previous surgery. A gradual correction by means
of a circular external fixator (the Ilizarov method) has proved
effective in treating difficult relapsed cases and severe
deformities; the early results are encouraging.

Treatment :
• Encourage the patient to wear flat shoes are
probably better than those with raised heel .
• When the condition become severely painful ,
surgery should be done to correct the deformity.
• Surgery should not be done from the cosmetic
aspect of the condition as there is high chance of
recurrence and transferring the pain to other
metatarsal.
