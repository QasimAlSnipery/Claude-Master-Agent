# PEDIATRIC ORTHOPEDICS

## Developmental Dysplasia of the Hip (DDH)
- Spectrum: **acetabular dysplasia → subluxation → frank dislocation**.
- **Incidence**: more in Kurds, Sami (Finland); **girls 7:1**; left > right; bilateral 20%.
- **Etiology**: genetic; late-pregnancy hormones (rare in premature); **breech with extended legs**; **tight swaddling/cradleboard** (rare in African carrying with hips abducted).
- **Pathology**: shallow saucer-shaped acetabulum; underdeveloped femoral head ossific centre (slides posterior then up); **anteverted femoral neck**.
- **Neonatal tests**: **Ortolani** (reduces dislocated hip), **Barlow** (dislocates unstable hip).
- **Late features**: clicking hip, limited abduction (difficult nappies), short leg (unilateral), asymmetrical skin creases, late walking, limping/waddling gait.
- **Diagnosis**: clinical; **US (first 3–6 months)**; **X-ray (after 4 months)** — shallow acetabulum, acetabular angle >30°, broken **Shenton's line**, head displaced up/out (above **Hilgenreiner's**, lateral to **Perkins'** line).
- **Management by age**:
  - **0–6 mo**: risk factors/+ve test → US; +ve → **Pavlik harness**.
  - **6–18 mo**: closed (GA ± traction + adductor tenotomy) or open reduction → **hip spica** (60° flexion, 40° abduction, 20° internal rotation ×3mo).
  - **18mo–4yr**: open reduction + remove obstacles ± femoral derotation/shortening ± acetabular osteotomy.
  - **4–10yr**: unilateral → surgery up to 10yr (older → AVN risk). **Bilateral after 4yr → no operation** (symmetrical, waddling gait tolerated).

## Perthes' Disease (Legg-Calvé-Perthes)
- **Idiopathic AVN of proximal femoral epiphysis** in children. 1/10,000; **boys 2:1**; **age 4–8**; bilateral 10%. Assoc: GU malformation, undescended testis, inguinal hernia, Down's.
- **Pathogenesis**: trauma/synovitis → effusion → presses on lateral epiphyseal vessels → AVN. Process takes 2–4 yrs.
- **Clinical**: boy, painful/painless limp, **pain referred to knee**, worse on activity; **+ve Trendelenburg**; loss of abduction + internal rotation.
- **X-ray**: ↑joint space, ↑density ossific nucleus, flattening, fragmentation, lateral displacement, **mushroom head, sagging rope sign**.
- **Treatment**: **<6yr → non-operative** (activity restriction, physio, abduction brace, crutch). **>6yr → containment** (abduction cast/brace ≥1yr, OR varus femoral / innominate osteotomy).

## Irritable Hip (Transient Synovitis)
- 1/1000; **boys 2:1**; age 3–9; recurrent 10%. Causes: systemic infection, synovial allergy, trauma.
- **Acute painful hip** (referred thigh/knee/buttock), lasts 5–10 days, ↓internal rotation, healthy-looking ± low fever, **X-ray negative**.
- **Diagnosis of exclusion**: CBC + ESR + CRP + X-ray ± US/MRI. **DDx: septic arthritis, osteomyelitis, Perthes.**
- **Treatment**: bed rest, NSAID, weight-bearing aids.

## Femoral Anteversion (In-toe gait)
- Commonest cause of in-toe gait; ↑internal rotation, ↓external rotation. Clumsy gait, trips; **squinting patellae**; sits in **"television position"**.
- Improves with growth (anteversion 40°→20° by age 10). Encourage **Buddha position**. Osteotomy rarely needed, not before age 8.

## Congenital Talipes Equinovarus (Clubfoot)
- Foot curved down + in: **ankle equinus (plantarflexion), heel varus, forefoot adducted/supinated**. 1–3/1000; **boys 2:1**; bilateral 50%; cause unknown.
- Sole faces posteromedially; small high heel; deep creases; cannot dorsiflex/evert. **Examine for DDH + spina bifida.**
- **Treatment**: start within days of birth. **Ponseti method** (international standard, >90% success): weekly manipulation + casting ×5–6 → percutaneous Achilles tenotomy + cast ×3wk → foot abduction brace (3mo continuous, then nights ×3yr). Resistant → open release; relapsed older → Ilizarov.

# PERIPHERAL NERVE INJURIES

## Degrees of injury
- **Neurapraxia**: demyelination (mechanical pressure); **full recovery expected**.
- **Axonotmesis**: axon interruption (closed fracture/dislocation); recovery variable.
- **Neurotmesis**: nerve trunk division (open wounds); needs surgical repair.

## Clinical & treatment principles
- Features: numbness/tingling, weakness, abnormal posture, **trophic ulcers, loss of sweating**. Electrodiagnostics accurate after few weeks.
- **Closed (neurapraxia/axonotmesis)**: wait weeks for spontaneous recovery.
- **Open**: early end-to-end suture or nerve graft. Late presentation → delayed repair.
- Care of paralysed part: full ROM twice daily, protect skin, splint to prevent contracture.
- **Tendon transfer** if no recovery in 18–24 months (donor must have power).

## Obstetric brachial plexus injury
- Excessive traction at birth. **Erb's palsy (commonest, C5–6±C7)**: paralysis of shoulder abductors/external rotators + supinators → arm **adducted, internally rotated, pronated ("waiter's tip")**. **Klumpke's** = lower roots. Physio; surgery if no biceps recovery by 6 months.

## Radial nerve injury
- **Low (elbow)**: fractures/dislocations → loss of MCP joint extension.
- **High (upper arm)**: humerus fracture, tourniquet → **wrist drop** + small sensory loss dorsum thumb base.
- **Very high (axilla)**: crutches (crutch palsy), **Saturday night palsy** → triceps paralysis + wrist drop.
- Closed → usually neurapraxia, spontaneous recovery (explore if none by 8–12wk); open → early repair. **Splint wrist in extension while awaiting.** Tendon transfer if no recovery.

## Ulnar nerve injury
- **Low (wrist)**: laceration/ganglion → hypothenar + interossei wasting, **claw hand**, weak finger abduction, loss thumb adduction (**Froment's test**), sensory loss ulnar 1½ fingers.
- **High (elbow)**: fractures, cubitus valgus, **cubital tunnel** → same but **less claw (ulnar paradox)** (long flexors not working).

## Median nerve injury
- Supplies most forearm flexors, thenar muscles, lateral 3½ fingers.
- **Low (wrist)**: cuts/carpal dislocation → thenar wasting, weak thumb abduction/opposition, sensory loss radial 3½ digits, trophic ulcers.
- **High (forearm/elbow)**: + paralysed long flexors thumb/index → **pointing index sign**. Disability mainly from sensory loss.

## Lower limb nerves
- **Femoral**: gunshot, traction, thigh bleed → weak knee extension (quadriceps), anterior thigh/shin numbness, depressed knee jerk.
- **Sciatic**: gunshot, stab, hip surgery/dislocation, pelvic fracture → paralysis all muscles below knee, weak knee flexion, sensory loss below knee (except shin), **foot drop**, absent ankle jerk.
- **Common peroneal**: varus stress, splint/POP pressure, leg externally rotated → **foot drop** + sensory loss leg (except sole + shin).
- **Deep peroneal**: anterior compartment syndrome → foot drop + sensory loss 1st web space. **Compartment syndrome → immediate decompression.**

## High-yield one-liners
- **DDH: Ortolani reduces, Barlow dislocates; girls 7:1; US <6mo, X-ray >4mo; Pavlik harness 0–6mo.**
- **Perthes: boy 4–8, AVN femoral head, pain referred to knee; <6yr conservative, >6yr containment.**
- **Irritable hip = diagnosis of exclusion** (rule out septic arthritis).
- **Clubfoot = Ponseti method** (>90%); always check for DDH + spina bifida.
- **Neurapraxia = full recovery; neurotmesis = needs repair.**
- **Erb's = waiter's tip (C5–6).** Radial = wrist drop. Ulnar = claw + Froment's. Median = pointing index + thenar wasting.
- **Foot drop = common peroneal / sciatic.** Compartment syndrome → urgent fasciotomy.
