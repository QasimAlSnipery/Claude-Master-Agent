Why Everything You’ve Tried Has Only Worked Temporarily

I have recommended versions of everything on this counter. I understand now why none of them address the underlying problem in aging adults.

Let me be direct about something that most physicians do not say clearly enough: ibuprofen, naproxen, and other NSAIDs are not pain treatments. They are pain suppressants. They block COX enzymes, which reduces prostaglandin production, which reduces the amplification of pain signals. While you are taking them, you feel better. The moment you stop, the underlying signaling system is in exactly the same state it was. Nothing has been corrected. Nothing has been restored.

On CBD: I am not dismissive of cannabidiol as a compound. It has legitimate effects on the endocannabinoid system and may support anxiety and sleep in some patients. But for the specific mechanism driving chronic joint and nerve discomfort in adults over 50 — which I will explain shortly — the endocannabinoid system is not the primary pathway involved. CBD is addressing the wrong target. That is why its effects, if they appear at all, tend to be modest and inconsistent in this population.
On turmeric and glucosamine: Curcumin has genuine anti-inflammatory properties in laboratory conditions. The clinical results are inconsistent because standard turmeric supplements have very poor bioavailability — most passes through without being absorbed. Glucosamine supports cartilage structure over time, which is a legitimate benefit, but it does nothing to address nerve hypersensitivity or the signaling dysfunction that produces the daily aching my patients describe. Healthy cartilage and constant pain are not mutually exclusive.
These are not bad products. They are products aimed at the wrong mechanism. And no amount of the wrong treatment corrects the right problem.

What I Found in the Research That Changed Everything

In 1957, researchers first identified a compound produced naturally by the body in response to tissue stress and irritation. They called it Palmitoylethanolamide — PEA. It is a fatty acid amide synthesized by immune cells, neurons, and connective tissue as part of the body’s own protective response system. Its function is to act as a natural dampener: when tissue becomes overactivated or inflamed, PEA signals the system to stand down.

What the research shows — and what I found when I finally looked — is that PEA production declines measurably after age 50. Not catastrophically. Not all at once. But enough that the self-regulating system begins to fail. The tissue stays overactivated. The nerve sensitivity that should resolve stays switched on. The result is the chronic, cumulative discomfort that patients like Carol describe: not severe, not acute, just relentlessly present.

Since 1957, 47 randomized controlled trials have studied PEA across multiple patient populations and pain types. The evidence base is larger than for CBD, turmeric, and glucosamine combined. A 2024 meta-analysis of these trials documented a 35.1% average reduction in pain intensity within the first 30 days of daily supplementation — with a further 35.4% reduction from day 30 to day 60. The cumulative effect is up to 70% pain reduction after a complete 60-day protocol.

How PEA Works — And Why the Mechanism Is Different From Anything Else

PEA activates a nuclear receptor called PPAR-alpha. When PPAR-alpha is activated, it down-regulates the inflammatory signaling cascade at the tissue level — not by blocking one enzyme the way an NSAID does, but by resetting the entire system’s sensitivity. The tissue stops overreacting. Nerve hypersensitivity decreases. The internal alarm that was stuck in the on position gradually resets to baseline.

PEA also stabilizes mast cells — the immune cells responsible for releasing compounds that sensitize nerve endings — and supports healthy microglial function in the central nervous system. As a clinician, what I find most significant about this mechanism is that it is restorative, not suppressive. It does not mask the signal. It corrects the dysregulation that is producing the signal in the first place.
