# Approach to Regional Anaesthesia

## The Big Picture
Regional anaesthesia = block the nerves, skip the general. Ideal for **limbs, lower abdomen, Caesarean**, and patients with **major heart/lung disease** who'd be high-risk under GA. But it's never 100% — always have a **plan B (GA)**. Key dangers to know: **neuraxial complications** and **local-anaesthetic systemic toxicity (LAST)**.

## What Counts as Regional
- **Neuraxial:** spinal (abdomen/pelvis/lower limb), epidural (neck→lower limb), caudal (perineal).
- **IV regional block (Bier)** — upper/lower limb.
- **Peripheral nerve blocks** — interscalene (shoulder/elbow), axillary/supra-/infraclavicular (arm), **sciatic** (most of leg), **femoral** (rest of leg).

## Why Use It
- Immobility + analgesia without GA risks.
- Great for distal-extremity surgery without sedation — attractive in severe pulmonary/cardiac/renal disease.
- Can complement GA for **lasting post-op pain relief**.
- **Spinal/epidural = technique of choice for Caesarean.**

## Safe Practice (the checklist)
Consent + preparation, equipment, **monitoring**, trained assistance, environment, documentation. Requires anatomy knowledge, manual dexterity, resuscitation skills, and **GA capability as backup**.

## Neuraxial Contraindications
- **Absolute:** patient refusal, **anticoagulation**, local sepsis, raised ICP, (true amide allergy — rare).
- **Relative:** severe valvular stenosis/Eisenmenger/peripartum cardiomyopathy, hypovolaemia, expected haemorrhage, previous back surgery, neurological disease, systemic sepsis.

## Neuraxial Complications
- **Hypotension**, bradycardia (mid-thoracic block → can arrest), **high/total spinal** (compromises breathing), urinary retention, nerve damage.
- **PDPH** (post-dural-puncture headache), infection (abscess, meningitis), **spinal canal haematoma** (worse with coagulopathy → can cause permanent paraplegia).
- Serious (death/paraplegia) ~**1:50,000–1:140,000**.

## LAST (Local Anaesthetic Systemic Toxicity)
- **Cause:** too much LA into circulation. **Avoid** by slow injection, small boluses, frequent aspiration.
- **Presentation (progressive):** light-headedness, **peri-oral tingling, metallic taste, tinnitus**, confusion, tremors → **convulsions, coma** → bradycardia, hypotension, **CVS collapse, respiratory arrest**.
- **Management:** stop injection; **ABC + 100% O₂**; intubate/ventilate; treat seizures (midazolam/propofol/thiopental); **IV 20% lipid emulsion (Intralipid) 1.5 mL/kg bolus** then infusion; CPR/ALS if pulseless.

## Allergy
Rare. **Esters** more allergenic than **amides**. Ranges from rash to laryngeal oedema/anaphylaxis.

## Choice of Agent
- **Lidocaine** — medium-acting amide, dose **2 mg/kg** (cerebral irritation before cardiac depression); topical airway forms (4%, spray).
- **Bupivacaine** — long-acting amide for post-op analgesia, dose **3 mg/kg (7 with adrenaline)**; **prolonged cardiotoxicity** in high doses.

## Adding Epinephrine (1:200,000)
- **Advantages:** limits systemic absorption, prolongs duration, lowers toxicity risk, epidural test dose.
- **AVOID in:** unstable angina, dysrhythmias, uncontrolled HTN, uteroplacental insufficiency, **end-arteries (digits, penis)**, IV regional anaesthesia.

## Walk-Away Points
- Regional shines in high-risk cardiac/pulmonary patients + **Caesarean (spinal/epidural)**; always have GA backup.
- Neuraxial absolute CIs: **refusal, anticoagulation, local sepsis, raised ICP**.
- **LAST** → stop, ABC/O₂, **lipid emulsion**.
- Bupivacaine = cardiotoxic; **no adrenaline in end-arteries**.
