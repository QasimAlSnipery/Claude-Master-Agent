Pathophysiology
Abdominal pain is caused by irritation of the peritoneum.
The peritoneal membrane consists of 2 layers, visceral & parietal,
I- visceral pain ; from viscera
A. Visceral peritoneum is innervated bilaterally by the autonomic NS
The bilateral innervation causes visceral pain that is:
midline, vague, deep, dull & poorly localized aching or cramping
B. visceral pain is triggered by: inflammation, Ischemia and geometric
changes such as: distention pressure(constriction), traction on the bowel
mesentery.
C. signifies intra-abdominal diseases but not necessarily The need

Nonspecific Abdominal pain
(NSAP)
Comprising ⅓ of all cases, is most common cause of the, acute
abdomen especially in children.
Generally mild, short-lived, & seldom associated with other serious
symptoms, it resolves without specific treatment.
Most cases represent undiagnosed viral and mild bacterial infections,
The mean hospital stay 4-6 days using the traditional "wait & see”.
Mx: repeated clinical examination, radiologic investigation, and a
gynecologic consultation.
The end result: unsatisfactory discharge from hospital after a prolonged stay

Progression
Has the pain abated or increased?
Have there been intervals of total absence of pain or
Has the pain present( continuous), changing only the character?
Intermittent attacks of abdominal pain that progresses to a steady,
constant ache suggest: a small bowel obstruction with vascular
compromise that will lead to the grave complication of ischemic
bowel — peritonitis.

=====PAGE 25 [T]=====
Migration - Shifting
Pain that shifts from original site of onset to another location is most
often associated with Acute Appendicitis.
peri-umbilical (visceral) pain that is present early in the course of the
disease is replaced with right lower quadrant (RIF) (somatic pain)
later in the illness when the parietal peritoneum becomes involved with
the inflammatory process.

Conditions that are less acute
Peritonism (not peritonitis): due to localized inflammation of the
peritoneum, usually by inflammation of a viscus that then irritates the
visceral and subsequently, parietal peritoneum.
The pain starts in one place before
(irritation of visceral peritoneum)
localizing to another area or
(irritation of the parietal peritoneum)
becoming generalized.
Classical example; is acute appendicitis with the pain migrating from
the umbilical region to the right iliac fossa.

Common Errors
1. Focus only on the abdomen.
2.begin with palpation prior to inspection, auscultation & percussion
3. Not asking the patient to localize the pain & therefore beginning
palpation of the affected area first, exacerbating the pain & thus
precluding complete examination of the abdomen.
4. Skipping the rectal, pelvic and groin examination.
25/10/2024 DR. FARMAN O HASSAN 49
=====PAGE 50 [T]=====
Common Errors
5.Putting too much weight on the absence of rebound tenderness
to rule out peritonitis.
6. Putting too much weight on the physical examination in an
immunosuppressed patient who may not exhibit normal signs of
peritonitis.
7. Forgetting to consider mesenteric ischemia when there is a
pain out of proportion to the clinical examination.

Conclusions:
1. Acute abdomen is commonly caused by appendicitis, cholecystitis,
and perforated ulcers.
2. Diagnostic approach involves clinical history, physical examination,
and imaging studies like ultrasound or CT scan.
3. Symptoms include severe abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting, and
possible fever.
4. Initial management includes pain relief, fluid resuscitation, and
stabilization of the patient.
5. Surgery is indicated when there is suspicion of perforation, bowel
ischemia, or unresolved obstruction.
