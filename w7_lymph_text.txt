Introduction:
•
Its a chronic disease marked by increased collection of
lymphatic fluid in the body causing swelling and
eventually skin and tissue changes.
•
Its due to: impairment in lymphatic outflow (
obstruction ) or increased production of lymphatic fluid
that exceeds the transport capacity of the lymphatic
conduit.

=====PAGE 7 [T]=====
Introduction:
•
Lymph is composed of: white blood cells,
triglycerides, cell debris, water and protein.
•
The lymph drainage system is complex and
comprises of: lymph capillaries, pre
collectors, collectors, lymphatic trunk and
lymph nodes.

•
Primary and secondary
•
Primary is further classified based on:
•
Genetics ( familial vs. sporadic )
•

•
Morphology ( aplasia, hypoplasia, numerical hyperplasia,
hyperplasia )
•
Anatomy ( distal obstruction, proximal obstruction )

Primary lymphedema (
morphology ) :
•
Aplasia : no collecting vessels can be found.
•
Hypoplasia: diminished number of vessels seen.
•
Numerical hyperplasia: increased number of vessels seen .
•
Hyperplasia: in addition to increase in number, the vessels haves
valvular incompetence, tortouisity and dilation.
•
Megalymphatics and hyperplasia are less common than aplasia
and hypoplasia.
•
Megalymphatics are associated with greater extent of
involvement and a word prognosis.

Primary lymphedema (
anatomy ) :
•
Distal obstruction: one third of all cases result form
agenesis, hypoplasia or obstruction of the distal
vessels with relatively normal proximal vessels.
•
Usually swelling is bilateral and mild,
•
Females > males.
•
Good prognosis, after the first year of symptoms
there’s little extension.
•
Familial occurrence, female predominance and
indolent progression characterizes this pattern.

=====PAGE 17 [T]=====
Primary lymphedema (
anatomy ) :
•
Proximal obstruction: in half of cases the defect
involves obstruction of the proximal lymphatics and
nodes with an absence of distal lymphatic involvement.
•
Pathologic studies involve intranodal fibrosis.
•
Swelling tends to be unilateral and severe.
•
Slight female predominance.
•
The abnormality tends to progress with relentless
worsening of edema, potentially requiring surgical
intervention.

=====PAGE 18 [T]=====
Secondary lymphedema:
•
Acquired ( secondary ) lymphedema is the most common
form of lymphatic dysfunction.
•
Common causes are:
•
Cancer
•
Filariasis
•
Lymphatic vascular trauma like : burns, large circumferential
wounds to the extremity, infection after snake and insect bite,
contact dermatitis and pregnancy.
6-12-2025 Dezhin F. Solai 18
=====PAGE 19 [T]=====
Secondary lymphedema:
•
Cancer:
•
Treatment of breast cancer is most commonly associated with
secondary lymphedema of the upper limbs.
•
Lymph node dissection and radiotherapy predispose to
lymphatic insufficiency.
•
Similar lymphatic sequelae are encountered in the lower limbs
and pelvis after interventions for gynecologic or urologic
malignancies.
•
Malignant melanoma can cause either upper or lower extremity
lymphedema after radical dissection in the axilla and groin.

=====PAGE 20 [T]=====
Secondary lymphedema:
•
Filariasis:
•
Caused by infestation with parasites such as Wuchereria
Bancroft, Brugia malayi and Brugia timori, its the most
frequent cause of secondary lymphedema in third world
countries.
•
The disease is most frequent in subtropical and tropical
countries like China, India and Indonesia.
•
An estimated 90 million people are infected, 90% of
them have Bancroft filariasis.

=====PAGE 21 [T]=====
Secondary lymphedema:
•
Filariasis:
•
Perilymphatic inflammation, fibrosis and sclerosis of the
lymph nodes are caused by indwelling adult worms.
•
Eosinophilia is found in the peripheral blood smear.
•
Microfilariae can be seen in peripheral nocturnal blood,
centrifuged urine sediment or lymphatic fluid.
•
Filarial lymphedema rapidly develops into grossly
incapacitating elephantiasis that is extremely difficult to
treat.

=====PAGE 22 [T]=====
Lymphedema:
•
Clinical staging:
•
Latent phase: excess fluid accumulates and fibrosis
occurs around the lymphatics, but no clinical edema is
apparent.
•
Grade 1: edema pits on pressure and is largely reduced
by elevation with np clinical evidence of fibrosis.
•
Grade 2: edema does non pit on pressure and is not
reduced by elevation, with marked fibrosis.
•
Grade 3:there is irreversible edema, fibrosis, and
sclerosis of skin and subcutaneous tissues. This is the
stage of elephantiasis.

Clinical presentation:
•
Edema: the interstitial phase is expanded by accumulation of protein
rich fluid, the swelling is soft and displaced with pressure ( pitting ).
•
Stemmer’s sign is positive when the skin at the base of the second
toe is inelastic and the examiner can not tent the skin.
•
Square toes: edema extends to the distal aspects of the feet.
•
Buffalo hump: when the dorm of the foot is involved.
•
Over time the the limb may take on a woody texture.

Complications:
•
Infection: the tendency for recurrent
infection is one of the most troublesome
aspects of lymphedema.
•
Once established, soft tissue infection
exacerbates the existing lymphedema.
•
Recurrent attacks of cellulitis.

lymphedema:

Complications:
•
Malnutrition and immunodeficiency:
•
Lymphangectaisia with protein losing enteropathy or chylous

long chain triglycerides, cholesterol and calcium.
•
Loss of lymphocytes, immunoglobulins, polypeptides and

immunodeficiency.
