Physiology of the esophagus
• The function of the esophagus is to transport
ingested material from pharynx to the stomach. This
is achieved by primary, secondary and tertiary
peristalsis.
• Primary peristalsis is triggered by swallowing center
in brain stem and the contraction wave travel at
speed 2cm/second
• Secondary peristalsis is triggered by esophageal
distension from retained bolus, refluxed material.
• Tertiary peristalsis is non propulsive and
incoordinate. It move the bolus up and down.

Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)
• Common chronic disorder.
• Its prevalence is 8-20% of
general population.
• Abnormal opening of LES will
lead to GERD.
• It will lead to esophageal
mucosal damage.
• Except pregnant women, both
gender are equally affected.

GERD Diagnosis/Diagnostic Tests
• Clinical History
– Patient’s description of typical or classic GERD symptoms such as heart
burn, is often enough to consider GERD as an initial diagnosis
(uncomplicated GERD)
• Empiric trial of proton pump inhibitor (PPI) therapy
– ACG (American College of Gastroenterology) guidelines state that it is
reasonable to assume a GERD diagnosis in patients who respond to
appropriate therapy
=====PAGE 14 [T]=====
GERD Diagnosis/Diagnostic Tests
• Endoscopy
– Endoscopy is the technique of choice to identify complications of GERD such
as ulcerations, erosions, Barrett’s esophagus, etc.
– Biopsy of the esophageal tissue is needed to identify and diagnose Barrett’s
esophagus and esophageal adenocarcinoma
– Many patients with GERD (presenting with typical or atypical symptoms) will
have normal appearing esophageal mucosa on endoscopy
– Usually not part of the work-up except in certain subsets of patients (alarm
symptoms, those refractory to treatment, etc.)
=====PAGE 15 [T]=====
GERD Diagnosis/Diagnostic Tests
• Ambulatory pH Monitoring
• Identifies patients with excessive esophageal acid exposure and helps
determine if symptoms are acid related
• Useful in patients not responding to acid-suppression therapy
• Barium Radiography
• Not routinely used to diagnose GERD due to a lack of sensitivity and
specificity
• Can detect other disease like achalasia and hiatal hernia.
=====PAGE 16 [T]=====
GERD Diagnosis/Diagnostic Tests
• Patients presenting with extraesophageal or atypical symptoms
should be reviewed on a case-by-case basis to be considered for
testing
• Alarm symptoms always warrant further testing

Therapeutic Approach to GERD
• The initial treatment used is determined by the patient’s
condition:
• Frequency of symptoms
• Degree of symptoms
• Presence and/or degree of esophagitis
• Presence of complications

Goals of Treatment
• Alleviate or eliminate acute symptoms
• Decrease frequency of recurrence
• Promote healing if esophageal tissue injury is present
• Prevent complications

General Treatment Approach
• Initial therapy in patients who present with typical GERD
symptoms should include patient-directed (self-care) therapy
(antacids, OTC H -antagonist, or OTC PPIs) and lifestyle
2
modifications
• Those who do not respond after 2 weeks should seek medical
attention and are usually started on empiric therapy consisting of
an acid suppression agent such as a proton pump inhibitor (PPI)
• Those who do not respond to empiric acid suppression therapy or
have alarm symptoms should undergo testing such as an

Lifestyle Modifications
• Weight loss (if the patient is overweight or obese)
• Elevation of the head of the bed 6 to 8 inches
• Eat smaller, more frequent meals (as opposed to larger meals less
frequently)
• Include protein-rich meals in diet (increases LES pressure)
=====PAGE 23 [T]=====
Lifestyle Modifications
• Avoid eating 3 hours prior to sleeping or lying down
• Avoid foods or medications that exacerbate GERD
• Avoid alcohol
• Tobacco cessation

=====PAGE 25 [T]=====
Achalasia
Etiology:
• It is caused by denervation of the esophageal smooth
muscle.
• The exact cause is unknown.
• Trypanosoma Cruzi of the myenteric plexus may be
associated with megaesophagus, called chaga’s disease.
Symptoms:
• Progressive
• Dysphagia, more for solid
• Retrosternal pain
• Regurgitation of the swallowed food.
• Nocturnal cough
• Weight loss

Diffuse esophageal
spasm
• This is probably related to the
fragmental degeneration of the vagal
nerve.
• Affect female more than male.
• Characterized by simultaneous,
repetitive high pressure,
uncoordinated muscular contraction
within the esophagus.
• It may be associated with severe
intermittent chest pain, dysphagia and
food impaction.
10/19/2025 Fahmi H. Kakamad 31
=====PAGE 32 [T]=====
Diagnosis
• Radiography: Corkscrew esophagus or
pseudodiverticulum could be seen on
esophagogram.
• Manometry: simultaneous, multipeaked
contractions and intermittent normal
peristalsis of high amplitude (120 mmHg)
or long duration (more than 2.5 second)

Hypercontractile esophagus (nutcracker
esophagus)
It is a motility disorder of the esophagus characterized by excessively
strong but coordinated peristaltic contractions.
Peristalsis remains coordinated, unlike in diffuse esophageal spasm.
The exact cause is unclear — thought to involve abnormal excitatory
innervation or heightened esophageal sensitivity.
Clinical Features
• Retrosternal chest pain (often severe and squeezing, mimicking
angina)
• Dysphagia, especially to both solids and liquids
• Pain may be related to swallowing or emotional stress
10/19/2025 Fahmi H. Kakamad 34
=====PAGE 35 [T]=====
Diagnosis
• Esophageal manometry is the key test:
• High-amplitude (>180 mmHg) peristaltic waves
• Normal LES relaxation
• Barium swallow: may be normal or show a
“corkscrew” pattern (less typical than in diffuse
spasm)
• Endoscopy: usually normal, done to exclude
structural lesions.
10/19/2025 Fahmi H. Kakamad 35

=====PAGE 38 [T]=====
Etiology of Esophageal
Rupture
Traumatic Causes (MORE COMMON):
• Endoscopy or dilation procedures
• Stent placement most common cause (up to 25% cases)
• Vomiting or severe straining
• Stab wounds / penetrating trauma
• Blunt chest trauma (rarely)
Non-Traumatic Causes (LESS COMMON):
• Neoplasm / Ulceration of esophageal wall
• Ingestion of caustic materials

Esophageal cancer-Treatment
• In the early stage, the goal is cure, in the late
stage, the goal is symptom relief (palliative).
• Cure is obtained by resection which may be
esophagectomy or endoscopic local
resection. Chemo-radiotherapy could be
given pre or post intervention.
