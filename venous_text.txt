Vein wall :
•
Veins are thin walled blood vessels
compared to arteries, how ever they
have the same three layers:
•
- adventitia on the outside

- media in the middle containing

- intima which is the inner layer and

Functional anatomy of the
venous system:
•
Vein wall :
•
The endothelial cells on the inside of the
intima are flat allowing blood to pass over
them giving them anticoagulant property
preventing clot formation when intact.
•
Different veins have different sizes:
•
- venules are less than half a millimeter
•
- great saphenous vein is about 5 mm
•
- inferior vena cava ranges between 10 to
18 mm

=====PAGE 5 [T]=====
Functional anatomy of the
venous system:
•
Venous valves :
•
Presence of valves on the inside of vein walls makes the
difference between veins and arteries.
•
Valves are bicuspid and arranged in pairs on opposite sides
of vein walls.
•
The valve leaflets are very thin and coated with endothelial
cells to prevent blood clots to form.
•
The opening and closing of the valves is a passive action of
the valve responding to the flow of blood through the vein.
•
Bicuspid valve
Although all normal veins have functioning valves, but it’s
rare to find any working valves in the pelvic veins and the
inferior vena cava.

Valve failure and venous reflux ( venous
insufficiency):
•
Varicose veins and pregnancy:
•
In the third trimester of pregnancy a normal female will
increase her blood volume by 40%.
•
Visceral and limbs vessels dilate to accommodate for this
increased volume.
•
The high levels of oestrogen and progesterone dilate the
blood vessels for this accommodation, without any
increase in intraluminal pressure.
•
Thus any woman who has underlying varicose veins that
are too small to be seen before pregnancy will be
uncovered during the pregnancy because of the hormones.

Deep Vein Thrombosis:
•
Introduction:
•
A deep vein thrombosis is a blood clot that
forms within the deep veins of the leg
usually, but can occur in the arms,
mesenteric and cerebral veins as well.
•
It’s a part of the venous thromboembolism
disorder, representing the third most
common cause of death form cardiovascular
diseases after myocardial infarction and
stroke.
6/11/2025 Dezhin F. Solai 30
=====PAGE 35 [T]=====
Deep Vein Thrombosis:
•
Introduction:
•
Usually initiates in the calf are, in the distal veins
below the popliteal trifurcation (distal DVT).
•
When distal DVT extends to popliteal and
femoral veins, patients Develop symptoms.
•
Patients with untreated proximal DVT have 50%
chance to develop symptomatic pulmonary
embolism.

=====PAGE 36 [T]=====
Deep Vein Thrombosis:
•
Introduction:
•
DVT complications include: post
thrombotic syndorme, pulmonary
embolism and death.
•
Post thrombotic syndrome develops in 20-
50 % of patients with proximal
symptomatic DVT and leads to lifelong
limb pain, swelling, heaviness, and
venous ulcers in gaiter area.

Deep Vein Thrombosis:
•
Risk factors:
•
Immobility: stroke, general anesthesia,
operations ( hip and knee replacement
and others ) bed rest, long flights.
•
Mechanical compression: tumors,
pregnancy, congenital anomalies that
reduce blood flow in the veins.
•
Vein injury: trauma, central venous
catheters, drug abuse.
