My name is Dr. Michael Harmon. The Patient Who Sent Me Back to the Research

Eleanor had started taking stairs one at a time, holding the rail with both hands. She had been doing it so long she had stopped noticing.

Eleanor came to my clinic last autumn. Knee pain, lower back stiffness, and a persistent nerve discomfort that ran from her hip down through her left leg. She had been managing it, she told me, for about four years. She was already on a daily NSAID. She had tried CBD oil for ten weeks on her daughter’s advice. She had used two brands of turmeric and a glucosamine product her pharmacist had recommended.

None of it had changed the underlying picture. The ibuprofen worked while she took it. When she tried to reduce the dose — which her GP had been urging for kidney-related reasons — the discomfort was back within 48 hours. “I am not getting better,” she said. “I am maintaining a level I can function at. There is a difference.” She said it without bitterness. Just as a statement of fact. And she was right.

I knew what the next step in the standard protocol looked like. I had taken it dozens of times. A referral to a pain management clinic, possibly a low-dose prescription option, a conversation about long-term expectations. I was about to start that conversation. Then something made me pause. Not certainty — just a quiet discomfort with what I was about to say. I told Eleanor I wanted to review some literature before our next appointment. She looked slightly surprised. Patients are not used to hearing that.

What PEA Is — And Why Nobody Ever Told You About It

Palmitoylethanolamide is a fatty acid amide the body synthesises naturally — in immune cells, neurons, and connective tissue — as a direct response to stress, irritation, and sustained discomfort. Its function is precise: act as the body’s internal dampener. When tissue becomes overactivated, PEA signals the local immune response to stand down. When nerve sensitivity escalates beyond what is useful, PEA quiets it. In a system with adequate PEA, chronic discomfort has a built-in regulator. The system self-corrects.

After age 50, that production declines. Not all at once. Not dramatically. But measurably — enough that the self-correcting mechanism starts to fail. Tissue stays overactivated. The nerve hypersensitivity that should resolve stays switched on. The body loses its ability to quiet itself, and the result is the chronic, cumulative discomfort patients describe as “always there” — not severe, not acute, just relentlessly present.

PEA was first identified in 1957. The research has been building for 67 years. The reason most people — patients and physicians alike — have never seriously engaged with it is not complicated: there is no pharmaceutical version. You cannot patent a molecule the body already makes. There has never been a marketing budget behind it, no sales rep pushing it, no conference sponsored by its manufacturer. It has simply sat in the research literature accumulating evidence, while the supplement and pharmaceutical industries directed attention elsewhere.

Why the Mechanism Is Different From Everything You’ve Already Tried

PEA works by activating a nuclear receptor called PPAR-alpha. When PPAR-alpha is activated, it down-regulates the entire inflammatory signalling cascade at the tissue level — not by blocking a single enzyme, but by resetting the system’s overall sensitivity. The tissue stops overreacting. Nerve hypersensitivity decreases. What was producing constant discomfort begins to quiet — not because the signal is being masked, but because the dysregulation producing it has been corrected from the inside.

Why ibuprofen and NSAIDs never solved the underlying problem: NSAIDs block COX enzymes to suppress prostaglandin production. While you take them, the amplification of the pain signal is reduced. The moment you stop, the signalling system is in exactly the same state it was. Nothing has been restored. Nothing has been corrected. The relief was borrowed — and the loan is called in the moment you stop making payments.
Why CBD and turmeric produce inconsistent results in this population: CBD interacts with the endocannabinoid system — a legitimate pathway, but not the primary driver of chronic joint and nerve discomfort in adults over 50. It is addressing the wrong target. Curcumin, the active compound in turmeric, has real anti-inflammatory properties in laboratory conditions, but standard oral bioavailability is too low for therapeutic doses to reach the tissue. Neither compound touches the PEA pathway. Neither can replace what the body has stopped making.
These are not bad products. They are products pointed at the wrong problem. When the molecule responsible for regulating your pain system has declined, no amount of the wrong treatment compensates for it.

One clinical distinction that matters: PEA is naturally poorly soluble. Standard-grade PEA largely passes through unabsorbed. The 47-trial evidence base was built entirely on micronized PEA — a form ground to a particle size that dramatically increases surface area and absorption. If a product does not specify micronized format, the results in that research literature do not apply to it. This is not a minor labelling distinction. It is the difference between a therapeutic dose reaching the tissue and passing through the body unused.

What Happened to Eleanor

At Eleanor’s next appointment, I did not discuss a referral. I recommended a micronized PEA supplement at the clinical dose. She was sceptical. I told her I understood. I asked her to give it seven days and pay attention — not to whether the pain was gone, but to whether the baseline had shifted.

She called my office on day nine. The morning stiffness, she said, was noticeably lighter. She had walked up the stairs at her house that morning — twice — without thinking about it. Not without difficulty, but without the calculation she had grown used to making. At her six-week follow-up, she reported she had not taken ibuprofen in three weeks. Not because she had decided to stop. Because she had not needed it.

I want to be precise about what PEA does and does not do. It does not regenerate damaged tissue. It does not cure structural problems. What it does — reliably, in the largest evidence base of any comfort supplement I have encountered — is restore the signalling environment that allows the body to regulate itself again. For patients whose chronic discomfort is driven by nervous system hypersensitivity and inflammatory dysregulation rather than structural damage, that is the difference between years of managing symptoms and actually recovering function.

What to Expect — And When

When I recommend PEA to patients now, I tell them the same thing I told Eleanor: do not expect it to feel like a painkiller. There is no rush, no sudden absence of discomfort in the first hour. What you will notice, typically between day five and day seven, is that the background level has shifted. The discomfort that was a seven is closer to a five. The stiffness is still there, but it is less insistent. You stop thinking about it as often.
